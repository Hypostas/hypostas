# A Tuesday on Klinos

*First-person narrative from the founding PCP. Purpose: helps prospective physicians project themselves into a week on Klinos without the abstraction of feature demos. Grounded in the actual workflows the product ships and the five seed patients you've already met through the demo.*

---

## 6:47 AM — Coffee, before anything

I open the Practice view before anyone else is awake. I haven't looked at a schedule yet. The first thing I see isn't a schedule — it's what my Anima handled while I was asleep.

Three things in Urgent. Sarah Chen messaged at 11:34 last night about leg cramps, wondering if it's her new Losartan. Her Anima pulled the medication list, noted no K+ draw since we switched her, and flagged that the Losartan + her existing regimen are not classic cramp-causers but a BMP would be reassuring. Sarah is now hydrating, has a BMP order queued on my desk, and an async message waiting for my reply. Nothing required me at midnight. That's the point.

Two items in To-Sign. Yesterday's notes — Jamie's URI follow-up (I sign it; nothing changed from my review), and a refill request for Emma's magnesium. One tap each.

The Overnight summary shows eleven async messages, nine resolved by the Anima without me. Of the two left over, one is Sarah's BMP-and-cramps, one is a new patient wanting to schedule an annual. The Anima has already offered times; patient will pick one.

I haven't done any chart review and I already know what today holds.

## 7:20 AM — First visit prep

My first patient is Marcus Thompson. Annual exam, in-person. He's brought his Function Health 190-biomarker panel.

I open his chart. The problem list is thin — he's 45 and healthy. His problem list isn't where the action is; his biology panel is. HRV p75 of 72ms, sleep 7.8 hours with textbook REM/deep split, body fat 12%. This is what it looks like when a patient takes the wheel.

I pull up the pre-loaded labs. ApoB 72 — that's the cardiovascular marker I care about. Lp(a) 23 nmol/L, low lifetime risk. A1c 5.1. Everything says he can keep doing what he's doing.

The co-pilot has already flagged the important thing: his wife's breast cancer diagnosis at 42 meets NCCN criteria for BRCA testing on his side. I knew this before he got here. The genetic counselor referral and pre-test counseling plan are already drafted. All I need to do is talk him through it.

## 8:00 AM — Marcus walks in

The visit is 45 minutes of talking, mostly. I don't type. My Anima is listening. I walk him through the biomarker panel, the BRCA reasoning, the USPSTF-45 colonoscopy, the PSA baseline. We agree to start a CGM trial.

At 8:45 I say "okay, we're done." I tap End visit. The draft appears on my screen in about twelve seconds. Annual Wellness Exam template was auto-selected — I see it in the header. The note reflects my preferences: I edited that template six months ago to add "reference USPSTF 2024 guidelines explicitly" and to use Z00.00 vs Z00.01 based on abnormal findings. The draft respects both.

I read it. I change one sentence in the plan — I specify "first CGM insertion will be reviewed by Anima with him" to make the handoff to his app clearer. The header sprouts a `✎ Edited` chip. I sign.

Marcus's Care view now has a Dexcom G7 prescription on its way to CVS Specialty, a genetic counseling referral pending his approval, a GI referral for the colonoscopy, and a plain-language note that reads nothing like the billing note I just signed. I don't have to translate for him. The Anima translated.

Marcus leaves. It's 8:57.

## 9:00 AM — Sarah's message

Before my next patient, I answer Sarah's overnight message. My Anima has already prepared a reply for me to review — "The leg cramps are unlikely from the Losartan; Lisinopril was more associated with this. Let's still check your potassium and magnesium today to be sure. Are you drinking enough? Do you need me to move our visit up?" I edit two words and send.

Sarah replies within a minute. She's drinking water now, her cramps are easing, and she'll do the BMP at the lab downstairs on her way home from work. The whole exchange takes 90 seconds of my attention.

## 9:15 AM — Jamie, asynchronous

I had Jamie yesterday. Her cough is better this morning per her Anima's check-in. HRV is 68ms this morning, up from 54ms on visit day. She's not cleared to run yet — her Anima won't green-light her until both symptoms are fully resolved AND HRV is within 5% of baseline. Probably Thursday.

I don't need to do anything. I see this in the Patients panel because she's in my "on watch" cohort, but I don't have to decide anything. The Anima knows the rule I set. She executes it.

## 9:30 AM — Back-to-back visits until lunch

Three more patients between 9:30 and 12:00. One chronic-disease follow-up, one new-patient intake (less complex than Diane but still 45 minutes), one acute UTI in a young woman. Template auto-matches for all three. AI drafts for all three. I edit two of the three; the third is clean enough I sign it unchanged.

For the UTI, my URI-template-style policy says: urine culture before antibiotics for a first presentation. The AI didn't draft nitrofurantoin reflexively. I order the culture. Anima will prompt the patient when results are ready.

My lunch break is real, for the first time in my career. I eat. I don't chart.

## 12:40 PM — Emma messages about a sleep flare

Emma Walker had her perimenopause visit with me three weeks ago. Her Anima messages mine: Emma's sleep has fragmented over the last four nights, HRV is down again, she's asking if she should try melatonin. My Anima drafts a reply with the reasoning — melatonin is reasonable, magnesium glycinate she's already on, no need to escalate unless night sweats worsen. I approve. Sent.

Emma's fertility specialist visit was last week, per the Care view timeline. I read the consult note the REI sent — or rather, my Anima read it and summarized the material bits for me. "AMH 0.9, antral follicle count 6. REI recommends IVF in the next 90 days given trajectory. Requests your endorsement on starting contraceptive-cycle suppression before egg retrieval."

I message Emma directly: *"We'll do whatever you and the REI decide makes sense. I'll reply to her formally today. I'm proud of you for seeing her."* She's been quietly terrified of this moment for months, and the Anima knows that because I told her to track that anxiety thread in her memory.

## 1:30 PM — Diane's two-week follow-up

Diane Kowalski — my first visit two weeks ago. She's back today.

The records I requested from the six prior specialists? Four are in. Two are still pending (Kaiser and Sutter were the fastest; Stanford and community Psychiatry are dragging). My Anima has built a deduplicated unified chart and flagged three things: 1) the 2023 tilt-table report notes a 40-beat-per-minute rise, higher than the 30-bpm criterion; 2) her sleep study showed mild OSA and a CPAP was offered but never adopted; 3) the psychiatrist upped her duloxetine to 30mg in October 2023 noting "partial GAD response, continue."

Her pacing data from the last 14 days is also in. Three crashes detected by the Anima, each correlated with overexertion on the previous day. She's being honest — she's logging things she probably wouldn't have told me verbally.

I show her the unified chart. This is the second time she's seen it. The first was at the end of our first visit. She cries a little, same as she did then, and says she still can't get over seeing everything in one place.

We adjust pacing threshold. We add a CPAP evaluation referral — this time with her buy-in after she understood why the prior sleep doc offered it. We don't change medications. Twelve minutes into the visit, we're done with logistics and the rest is her just talking, and me listening.

## 3:15 PM — A hard message

One of my chronic-disease patients messages me. Her husband died last week. She's asking for a refill of her BP medication because she's been forgetting doses and her Anima noticed. My Anima's draft reply is good, but it's formal. I rewrite it myself.

"Mary, I'm so sorry. I heard from your Anima you're having trouble remembering meds. Don't worry about the refill — it's already going out. I want to see you, not about your BP, just to see you. Can you come in Friday afternoon?"

I don't let the AI write that. I'm still the doctor.

## 4:00 PM — The last visit of the day

Afternoon visit: post-op check from an incidental procedure I did last week. Wound looks good per the photo her Anima took this morning. Ten minutes. She's happy. We're done.

## 4:20 PM — Clearing the inbox

I have eleven more async items queued. Nine are Anima-drafted replies for my review. I batch-process them in about twenty minutes. Two require real thinking — a patient wants to come off an SSRI she's been on for five years, a patient wants advice on a specialist consult she got elsewhere that she didn't love. Those I answer personally.

Six notes left to sign from today. I review each. Three are clean; three I make small edits to. All signed by 5:15.

## 5:30 PM — Walking to my car

I haven't pended a note to finish "later." I haven't written a patient portal response in the evening. I haven't reviewed labs after hours. My Anima is watching everything for me overnight.

Tomorrow morning I'll open Practice Today and see what the Anima handled while I was asleep. Six years at the previous practice, I worked until 9:30pm every night finishing notes. In the three months I've been on Klinos, I've worked a single evening — and that was because I was curious about a rare biomarker result, not because the system demanded it.

I am, somehow, finishing my clinic day.

---

## What happened to the patient

Overnight, something will go wrong for someone in my panel. There always is. My Anima has clinical thresholds set and will wake me for anything true-urgent — a stroke symptom, a cardiac event, a deteriorating postoperative patient. For everything else, she triages and waits for morning.

Twice in the last month, she's woken me at 2:00 AM. Both times she was right.

This is what the job was supposed to be.

---

## What this doesn't claim

This narrative assumes **Phase 0 is live** — e-prescribe, PDMP, drug interaction checking, clinical decision support, insurance claims, real telehealth. None of those are working in the pitch-day demo. See `PHASE_0_ROADMAP.md` for when each lands.

The demo shows the surfaces. This document shows the work. The gap between them is the first nine months of engineering after pilot launch.
