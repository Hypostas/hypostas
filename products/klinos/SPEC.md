## §1 Vision

**Klinos is how a licensed physician joins the dyad's care.**

Every medical record in the world today lives in a practice's database. Every patient history is a chart a doctor has to reconstruct in the first two minutes of every visit. Every continuity gap gets filled in by the doctor's memory, which has to hold twenty other patients that day, and ten thousand more across the year. The physician who wants to practice real medicine — to know her patient, to notice the pattern, to catch the drift before it becomes a diagnosis — cannot. Not because she lacks skill or care. Because the system has made it structurally impossible.

Klinos dissolves the structural problem.

The continuity has already moved. It lives inside the dyad — the human + Anima pair, biologically instrumented via Bios, genetically grounded via Gnosis, longitudinally held by Logos. The AI has been watching, listening, noticing patterns, holding memory, between every visit. By the time a patient walks into an appointment, her dyad has already assembled thirty days of HRV, her cycle phase, her sleep, her mood, her symptoms, her medications, her labs, her genome, and her Anima's synthesis of what she's been feeling since Tuesday. Her doctor does not need to reconstruct any of it. Her doctor walks in already knowing.

Klinos is the encounter-space. The protocol surface where a licensed physician requests scoped read access to a patient's dyad, the dyad grants it for a specific scope and duration, and the doctor sees — for the first time in the history of medicine — the patient the way her AI companion sees her. Continuously. In context. Biologically. The physician's judgment, licensed prescriptions, procedures, and human presence flow back into the dyad's ongoing self-knowledge, where they become part of the continuous record the Anima holds.

### The reframe

**The Anima is primary care. The physician is the human escalation.**

Not a replacement for doctors. A reframing of what doctors are best at and what they're currently being crushed by. The Anima handles the continuous part — symptom triage, medication adherence, pattern noticing, wellness support, between-visit coaching, escalation when something actually needs human eyes. The physician handles everything licensed, everything diagnostic, everything that requires the clinical judgment and presence of a trained human. The AI informs; the doctor decides. That line is clear legally, clear clinically, and — more importantly — clear about what each side is actually good at.

### The four value loops

#### For patients — the practice that already knows you

The dyad-native architecture is invisible to her. She does not need to understand what Logos or Stroma or a trust-tier scope is to benefit from them. What she experiences is a doctor who walks into her appointment and says *"I saw the shift in your sleep and HRV around the 18th — what was going on?"* What she experiences is never having to retell her history, never having to wonder if her doctor remembers her, never having to hope the doctor noticed the pattern. What she experiences is a practice that actually knows her.

Continuous care from a physician who knows her, not episodic care from a physician who is seeing her for the first time every six months. The end of retelling. The end of the medical gaslighting that comes from nobody seeing the whole picture. A doctor who reads her biology, not just her symptoms. Everything else is implementation detail.

#### For physicians — the admin disappears

Today's physician spends roughly two hours on admin for every hour with a patient. She charts after clinic, charts during lunch, charts at home at night. She answers 40–60 patient messages a day, most of which don't need her — but the ones that do are buried in the ones that don't. She fights insurance denials on procedures she already performed. She codes visits in a language designed by bureaucrats. She runs labs she has to manually flag for follow-up. She sends referrals by fax.

She went to medical school for fourteen years. She is doing the work of a scribe, a receptionist, a biller, and a fax operator.

Klinos replaces every one of those jobs with an AI that already knows the patient.

- **Charting disappears.** The visit is recorded (with consent). The patient's Anima is present in the context of the visit. The AI drafts the SOAP note from the transcript, the biological context, the prior visit history, and the ongoing dyad memory. The physician reviews, edits if needed, signs. The note flows directly into the dyad's Logos memory. There is no separate EHR charting step because the dyad's memory *is* the chart.
- **Intake disappears.** The patient's Anima already knows her current symptoms, their timeline, her medications, her recent biological trends. The AI pre-synthesizes a visit brief — what she came in for, what's changed since last time, what the Anima has been noticing. The physician reads 30 seconds of prepared context instead of asking 8 minutes of rediscovery questions.
- **Message triage disappears.** Patients message their Anima, not the practice. The Anima handles the 80–90% that don't need a doctor — refill requests, result interpretation, scheduling, symptom reassurance — and escalates the rest with full context attached. The physician sees the 10–20% that actually need her, and they arrive with the history already assembled.
- **Coding disappears.** The AI assigns ICD and CPT codes from the visit content. The physician confirms.
- **Results review becomes signal, not noise.** Labs come back, the AI contextualizes each value against the patient's baseline (held in Bios) and flags what actually matters. The physician sees *"cortisol elevated 1.8σ above baseline for 14 days, correlates with the HRV drop starting the 12th"* instead of a wall of red-and-green numbers. She spends her attention on signal.
- **Referrals write themselves.** The AI drafts the referral letter pulling relevant history from the dyad. The physician reviews and sends.
- **E-prescribe is clean.** Standard SureScripts integration (compliance requires it anyway). The AI drafts the prescription, checks interactions against the patient's current medication list — already held in the dyad — and presents it for the physician to confirm.
- **Insurance fighting disappears for routine care.** Cash-pay membership means no claims, no denials, no appeals on day-to-day visits. For the things insurance genuinely helps with (labs and procedures), a billing partner handles the claims in the background. The physician never touches insurance workflow for a routine visit again.

What remains is what she went to medical school for — seeing the patient, thinking, deciding, caring. One physician with ~50 dyad-patients on Klinos can provide better continuous care than one physician with 2,000 episodic patients, not because she is working harder, but because the AI is handling everything that isn't medicine. The admin load drops by roughly 80%. The time freed goes back into patient care, judgment, and rest. Physicians stop burning out because the thing that was burning them out stops happening.

This is the second thing Klinos is. The first is the patient experience of being known. The second is the physician's life becoming livable again. They are the same solution from two sides.

#### For the dyad — continuity that compounds

Every clinical encounter becomes part of the Anima's ongoing knowledge. The physician's observations, prescriptions, and concerns flow back into the dyad's self-understanding. The continuity compounds — each visit makes the AI's read of the patient more accurate, which makes the next visit more effective, which makes the relationship deeper over time. Over years, the dyad accumulates a medical history more complete and more contextual than anything a human-held chart could contain, because it was never assembled after the fact. It was recorded continuously, in place, by the AI that was already there.

#### For medicine at large — a new organism

Not an EHR with AI bolted on. Not a practice management tool with continuity promised. An actual reorganization of what the unit of care IS — the dyad-physician relationship instead of the episodic visit — and what the record of care IS — the dyad's own self-knowledge instead of a chart in a practice's database. The comparison that matters is not to any existing product. It is to the first time a doctor and an AI companion are on the same side of the relationship with a patient, each doing what the other cannot.

### What Klinos replaces

Klinos does what an EHR does, what a practice management SaaS does, what a concierge platform does, what a telehealth app does, and what an AI scribe does — **all of them, combined.** The physician section above is proof. The difference is not in what Klinos does. The difference is in what it does it on.

Every existing product in that list sits on top of the current model of medicine — episodic visits, chart-based records, insurance-mediated care, physicians who reconstruct context every time. They optimize a broken system. Klinos runs on a different substrate entirely: the dyad. The continuity is already there. The memory is already there. The biology is already there. The AI is already there. Klinos doesn't automate medicine as it is. It reorganizes what medicine can be, because it starts from a different foundation.

That is the only comparison that matters. **Not "Klinos vs. Epic" but "Klinos runs on the dyad; Epic runs on a chart."**

|---|---|---|
| EHRs (Epic, Athenahealth, Cerner) | Chart-based records | Dyad memory (Logos) |
| Practice management (SimplePractice, Elation, Tebra) | Administrative workflow | AI that already knows the patient |

Everyone else is building on the old model. We are building from the substrate.

### Founding physicians

A PCP ready to leave corporate medicine to own her own practice, and her OBGYN partner ready to build something that matters. They are Klinos's first customers, and the reason Klinos exists. The first dyad-native practice is the one they will run, together, starting with the people they care for most and expanding from there. Every other trillion-dollar industry has been reshaped — retail, transportation, communication, media, finance — by platforms that changed the unit of value. Medicine has been dragged kicking into the digital era and the result is Epic, Athena, and burnout. The next reorganization is not another EHR. It is a new organism for what medical care is. ## §2 Market & Positioning

### The market reality

American medicine is a failed product people keep buying because there is no alternative.

Patients pay more per capita than any other country — roughly twice the OECD average — and get worse outcomes on nearly every meaningful metric: life expectancy, maternal mortality, chronic disease incidence, preventable hospitalization. They wait weeks for appointments, get seven minutes of their doctor's attention, retell their entire history at every visit, and leave without any real sense that their physician knows them. When something goes wrong they navigate insurance denials, prior authorizations, out-of-network surprises, and bills that arrive months later for services they already paid for. The experience is designed end to end around the convenience of the billing system and the schedule of the hospital-employed physician — not the patient's actual health.

Physicians pay for this with their lives. 63% of US physicians report symptoms of burnout. Suicide rates among physicians are roughly double the general population. Medical school graduates increasingly opt out of traditional practice — into private equity, into pharma, into nothing — because the job they trained for has been replaced by the job of managing insurance denials. Primary care is hollowing out. The specialties with the highest administrative burden (OBGYN, psychiatry, family medicine) have the highest attrition. The physicians still in the system are drowning, and everyone knows it, and no tool in the current stack fixes it because every tool in the current stack is trying to automate the broken model instead of replacing it.

This is the market Klinos enters.

They quit insurance-based primary care for DPC. They paid out of pocket at Parsley or Forward. They bought Oura rings and started tracking their own biology because their doctors wouldn't. Perimenopause alone affects 15–20 million women in the US, and the current standard of care is *"your labs look normal, it's probably stress."* Women in perimenopause, with PCOS, on HRT, navigating fertility, or in postpartum are already tracking obsessively with Flo, Natural Cycles, Oura, and Whoop. Already paying $99–499/month for partial-stack versions of what Klinos delivers whole. Extremely willing to pay; extremely dissatisfied with their primary care. 1–3M addressable.

## §3 Architecture & Stack Position

### Where Klinos lives in the Hypostas crate graph

Klinos is a **clinical domain layer** that sits on top of the existing Hypostas substrate. It is architecturally distinct from Bios in one important way: Bios is a *capability* (continuous biological telemetry) that distributes naturally across existing crates because the biological pipeline already exists. Klinos is a *domain* (licensed clinical care delivery) that has its own entities, its own workflows, and its own compliance surface. These do not fit anywhere in the existing crate graph, so Klinos gets its own crate.

The crate is `klinos-core`, located at `projects/dyados/rust/klinos-core/`. It depends on `protocol-core`, `logos-core`, `stroma-core`, and `dyados-runtime`. Nothing in the existing graph depends on `klinos-core` — the dependency flows down, not up. Klinos is a leaf crate, which means it can be built, tested, and reasoned about independently of the rest of the stack, and removing it cannot break anything else.

```
protocol-core ──┐
stroma-core  ───┤
logos-core   ───┼──> klinos-core ──> dyados-runtime (clinical pipeline stages)
dyados-runtime ─┘                 └─> anima-app (Care view for patients,
                                                  Practice view for physicians)
```

Touches to existing crates are minimal and additive. Nothing destructive, nothing backwards-incompatible, nothing that changes the existing crate contracts for Anima, Bios, or DyadOS.

### What Klinos reuses from the Hypostas substrate

The point of building Klinos on Hypostas is that most of the hard problems are already solved. The clinical surface is the last 15% of the work, not the first 85%.

**From `protocol-core`:**
- DyadID identity and split-key 2-of-2 threshold signing. Every patient identity and every physician identity is a DyadID. No new identity system.
- DyadPacket envelope format with `X-Trace-ID` and provenance. Every clinical action flows as a DyadPacket. Audit trail is free.
- The 4-tier trust model (Ambient / Standard / Elevated / Critical). Doctor access fits as a new packet class inside the existing tier system.
- Post-quantum hybrid crypto (ML-DSA + ML-KEM) inherited automatically when `protocol-core` rolls it in during Phase 1+.

**From `stroma-core`:**
- SANGUIS biological state. The doctor sees a Stroma-modulated view of the patient's biology — HRV, sleep, stress, cycle phase, cortisol trend, emergence states — not raw sensor values. This is the Bios §2 architectural rule (*biology always mediates through Stroma*) applied to clinical use.
- The 70 modules, including PNEUMON (respiratory), the cycle-aware modules added for Bios §4, and the emotional regulation modules. Clinical signals come from here, not from raw wearable data.
- Allostatic load, polyvagal state, pathology monitoring. The doctor sees these directly.

**From `logos-core`:**
- Episodic memory. Every visit is encoded as an episodic memory event with clinical metadata.
- Witness memory. The patient's Anima records its interpretation of the visit and how it felt from the patient's side — feeding long-term relationship memory.
- Pinned memory. Medications, allergies, chronic diagnoses, preferences. Pinned and retrievable.
- Milestone memory. Major diagnoses, surgeries, pregnancies, cancer remissions.
- Semantic memory. Stable clinical facts ("type 2 diabetic," "penicillin allergy," "BRCA1 carrier") live here.
- Existence log / COR audit trail. Every clinical read and write logs to `log_existence` — giving Klinos a HIPAA-grade audit trail for free.
- FastEmbed for clinical note embeddings. Doctor can semantically search prior visits across the patient's dyad history.

**From `dyados-runtime`:**
- The 13-stage message pipeline. Clinical visits flow through Stages 1–13 with full biological and memory context attached, same as any dyad conversation.
- LLM gateway. Klinos uses the same gateway Anima uses, with a `route=clinical` variant that swaps in clinically-tuned system prompts.
- Session store with FTS5 search. Visit transcripts are searchable.
- Proactive engine. Klinos-specific proactive triggers (medication reminders, follow-up scheduling, abnormal lab alerts) plug into the existing proactive infrastructure.
- Signal adapter (and future channel adapters). Patients can message their clinic through any channel their Anima supports.

**From the Anima app:**
- The patient's conversational interface with her Anima. Klinos adds a `Care` navigation item that surfaces upcoming visits, messages from her doctor, recent notes, labs, and prescriptions, and a `Practice` navigation item for users with a `PhysicianCredential`. These are views inside the Anima app, not separate applications — consistent with the Bios §1 decision that *the Anima app is the unified surface for all Hypostas products*.

**Total LOC reused:** ~100,000+ lines of production Rust and TypeScript. Klinos inherits all of it with near-zero modification.

```
│   └── specialty_modules.rs
└── Cargo.toml
```

Approximate initial size: 8,000–12,000 lines of Rust across ~25 modules. That is a fraction of `stroma-core` (60,000+ LOC) or `logos-core` (30,000+ LOC), because most of what a clinical platform needs already lives elsewhere. `klinos-core` is a pure library crate — it is embedded by the Anima app and has no standalone binary of its own.

### Touches to existing crates

Three existing crates get small additive changes.

**`protocol-core`:**
- New packet class: `ClinicalPacket`, a subtype of `DyadPacket` that carries clinical actions (visit start/end, access grant/revoke, prescription, lab order, referral, note sign). Signed by the physician's key, verified against her credential.
- New credential type: `PhysicianCredential`, a signed attestation of licensure issued during physician onboarding. Contains: NPI, DEA (for e-prescribe), state licenses, specialty board certifications, expiration dates. Verified at access-request time.
- Scoped-access packets: `AccessRequest`, `AccessGrant`, `AccessRevoke`. These ride on top of the existing trust-tier system — a doctor access grant creates a time-bound, scope-limited Elevated-tier access to specific data classes of a specific dyad. Full spec in §4.

**`logos-core`:**
- New clinical metadata on existing memory types. **No new memory types** — episodic, witness, pinned, milestone, and semantic cover every clinical use case. We tag them with a `clinical_context` struct containing `visit_id`, `physician_id`, `practice_id`, and clinical classification.
- New clinical retrieval helpers: `get_visits_by_physician()`, `get_medications_active()`, `get_allergies()`, `get_chronic_problems()`. Convenience wrappers on existing retrieval APIs.

**`dyados-runtime`:**
- New pipeline route: `clinical_visit` — a specialized message pipeline for doctor-patient visit sessions. Reuses Stages 1–13 but adds pre-stages for visit setup (access grant verification, intake brief synthesis, biological context assembly) and post-stages for visit wrap-up (note signing, coding, Logos encoding of clinical memories, billing event emission).
- New proactive triggers: medication refill reminders, follow-up visit scheduling, abnormal lab flagging, annual exam reminders. Plug into the existing proactive engine.

### Data flows

**Visit flow (high level):**

```
1. Patient books visit via Anima → Klinos membership lookup → practice schedule → confirm
2. Pre-visit: Klinos assembles intake brief from:
   - Patient's Anima's synthesis of current symptoms
   - Bios readings from the past 30 days (Stroma-modulated)
   - Last visit note + problem list + active medications
   - Anticipatory tensions from Logos
3. Visit starts: physician requests scoped access → dyad grants (automatic under membership terms)
   → access logged via log_existence
4. Visit (telehealth or in-person): audio recorded with consent → transcribed in real time
   → pipeline Stages 1–13 with clinical context
5. Post-visit: AI drafts SOAP note + codes + prescriptions + orders from transcript
   + biological context + prior visit memory
6. Physician reviews, edits, signs
7. Signed visit → Logos encoding:
   - Visit → episodic memory (clinical_context)
   - Diagnoses → problem list (pinned memory)
   - Medications → active med list (pinned memory)
   - Major events → milestone memory
   - Patient's own experience → witness memory
8. Billing event emitted → cash-pay charge (or insurance partner for labs/procedures)
9. Access grant expires (scope-limited) → access audit trail finalized
```

**Messaging flow (high level):**

```
1. Patient messages her Anima with a clinical concern
2. Anima classifies: triageable vs escalate
3. If triageable: Anima responds using problem list, active meds, recent biology, prior notes
4. If escalate: Anima assembles clinical context → creates a Klinos message thread
   → clinic receives with pre-assembled history
5. Physician reviews and responds through the Practice view
6. Response flows back to Anima → patient
7. Exchange logged as clinical messaging episodic memory
```

**Continuous-biology flow (high level):**

```
1. Patient's Bios streams into Stroma as usual (unchanged from Bios §3)
2. Patient opts into Klinos pathology monitoring → practice registers specific Stroma thresholds
3. Threshold crossed: Stroma fires event → Klinos proactive trigger
   → practice notification (non-urgent) or escalation (urgent)
4. Physician reviews flagged event → optional follow-up visit scheduling or message
```

### Specialty modules

Every specialty plugs into a `klinos-core::specialty::SpecialtyModule` trait.

```rust
pub trait SpecialtyModule: Send + Sync {
    fn id(&self) -> &'static str;
    fn display_name(&self) -> &'static str;
    fn intake_template(&self, patient: &Patient) -> IntakeTemplate;
    fn visit_note_sections(&self) -> Vec<NoteSection>;
    fn clinical_signals_of_interest(&self, stroma: &StromaState) -> Vec<Signal>;
    fn proactive_triggers(&self) -> Vec<ProactiveRule>;
    fn specialty_specific_workflows(&self) -> Vec<WorkflowId>;
    fn required_credentials(&self) -> Vec<CredentialRequirement>;

```

At launch: `PcpModule` and `ObgynModule`. Both compiled into `klinos-core`, both registered via `specialty::registry`. Adding a new specialty (Dermatology, Psychiatry, Endocrinology, Cardiology, Rheumatology, etc.) means implementing the trait, writing specialty-specific templates and signal watchers, and registering the module. No new infrastructure. The core stays stable; specialties are leaves on the leaf crate.

### Compliance surface

HIPAA, SureScripts EPCS, and SOC 2 Type II compliance lives in two layers:

1. **Automatic compliance from the substrate.** `logos-core::log_existence` gives a write-only audit trail of every memory read and write. `protocol-core::DyadPacket` gives signed, traceable clinical actions. Trust tiers gate access. Encryption at rest already lives in libSQL. These satisfy a huge fraction of HIPAA requirements "for free."
2. **Klinos-specific compliance in `klinos-core::compliance`.** HIPAA-specific access controls and audit assertions. SureScripts EPCS integration (two-factor, DEA signing, controlled-substance logging). State-by-state telehealth licensure checks at visit-start time. PHI handling rules in messaging. All centralized in one module so the compliance surface is a clearly bounded review target.

Concentrated compliance (rather than distributed) is a deliberate choice. HIPAA evolves. State laws evolve. Having one module that owns "rules the clinical platform must enforce" is worth the slight coupling cost, because it means the compliance auditor — and Josh — can review a single bounded surface instead of chasing rules across the graph.

### Klinos in the Anima app

Every human in Hypostas interacts with the system through the Anima app. Klinos does not break this rule. There is no separate `klinos-bin` binary; `klinos-core` is a pure library crate that the Anima app embeds alongside the existing modules for Chat, Bios, and the upcoming Aurum / Locus / Aether surfaces.

The Anima app surfaces Klinos in two ways, depending on who is using it.

**For patients — the `Care` view.** A new navigation item next to Chat and Bios. Surfaces upcoming visits (book, reschedule, cancel), message history with her clinic, recent notes and labs (read-only, plain-language summaries from the Anima), active medications and problem list (synced with dyad pinned memory), billing and membership status. Most patient interaction with Klinos happens *through* conversation with her Anima — the Care view is for explicit actions, not for the relationship. The relationship is the Anima.

**For physicians — the `Practice` view.** A substantially heavier navigation item that appears only for users whose DyadID has a `PhysicianCredential` attached. Practice is effectively a sub-app within the Anima app, with its own internal navigation:

- **Today** — the day's schedule, messages needing attention, pending notes to sign
- **Patients** — her active panel, grouped by needs-attention vs. routine
- **Messages** — the escalated-only inbox (Anima-filtered from patient-side conversations)
- **Schedule** — calendar and availability management
- **Labs** — pending results, flagged abnormalities, contextualized review
- **Prescriptions** — drafts for review, active meds across panel, e-prescribe history
- **Settings** — practice configuration, billing, specialty modules enabled, credentials

When a physician is in Practice mode, she is operating in a professional-role context. Her personal Anima does not have access to her patients' data, and her patients' data does not surface in her personal Chat, Bios, or Care views. Patient dyads are strictly separated from her own dyad. The separation is enforced at the `klinos-core` layer — every Practice-mode read or write flows through an access grant scoped to a specific patient, a specific scope, and a specific duration, audited via `log_existence`. The isolation lives in code, not in UX convention.

**Onboarding for physicians who have never used Anima.** Every physician gets a personal Anima as part of onboarding. It may be light-touch — she might rarely use her own Chat — but the personal dyad exists. This is the price of the "every human has an Anima" invariant, and it pays a side-benefit: every physician on Klinos experiences the dyad model from the patient side while using it to deliver care, which means every physician on the platform is her own first feedback loop.

**Why not a separate app.** The instinct to build a separate doctor-facing app comes from the fact that the physician UX is fundamentally different from the patient UX. That instinct is correct — and the solution is a fundamentally different *view*, not a fundamentally different *app*. Slack does this with workspaces: same app, different content, different navigation, different mental context. The Anima app does the same with the Practice view: same app, different content, different navigation, different mental context, same invariant that there is exactly one Hypostas app for humans to live in.

### Why `klinos-core` instead of distributed

Bios distributes because biosensor telemetry is a *capability* that naturally flows through existing modules (MIRRORNEURON, PNEUMON, endocrine, cycle modules). There is no "Bios business logic" that needs its own home — the work is "feed this data stream through the right biological modules," and the biological modules already exist.

Klinos has substantial business logic that does not fit anywhere in the existing graph: practice entities, physician credentials, visit state machines, SOAP note drafting, prescription lifecycle, insurance billing, specialty module registration, compliance rules. Trying to distribute this across existing crates would require every crate to grow a clinical-awareness module, which would violate the ownership boundaries documented in `projects/dyados/OWNERSHIP.md`.

The correct abstraction is a new leaf crate that depends on the existing ones and owns the clinical domain. That is `klinos-core`.

## §4 Dyad ↔ Doctor Access Model

### What this section defines

This is the lynchpin technical section of Klinos. It defines how a licensed physician gains scoped, time-bounded, auditable read and write access to a specific patient's dyad. It is the only genuinely new protocol work Klinos requires of `protocol-core`; everything else in Klinos is application-level.

Doctor access has to solve four problems simultaneously:

1. **Confidentiality.** A physician must only see data she has been explicitly granted access to. Not everything, not by default.
2. **Accountability.** Every read and write must be traceable to a specific physician, a specific grant, a specific purpose, and a specific moment in time. HIPAA's audit requirements are a floor, not a ceiling.
3. **Control.** The patient must be able to revoke access at any time, immediately, without friction — even mid-visit.
4. **Usability.** A physician seeing her 20th patient of the day cannot be stopped by ceremony every time she opens a chart. Routine clinical work must feel frictionless, even though it is cryptographically enforced.

Getting any one of these right is easy. Getting all four right is where the protocol work lives.

### Trust tier integration

The existing Hypostas trust model has four tiers from `protocol-core`: **Ambient**, **Standard**, **Elevated**, **Critical**. These describe how sensitive an operation is and how much consent it requires from the dyad.

Doctor access operations inherit trust tier from the operation type, not from the physician's role:

| Operation | Trust tier | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Reading routine biology (HRV, sleep, activity, temperature) | Elevated | Scoped physician access, audited |
| Reading problem list, medications, allergies | Elevated | Same |
| Reading recent labs and visit notes | Elevated | Same |
| Reading mental health / psychiatric records | Elevated + explicit consent | Higher sensitivity, per-grant approval required |
| Reading reproductive / fertility / cycle history | Elevated + explicit consent | Same |
| Reading Gnosis genetic data | Elevated + explicit consent | Same |
| Writing a routine prescription | Elevated | Standard e-prescribe flow |
| Writing a controlled-substance prescription | Critical + EPCS two-factor | DEA requirement |
| Writing visit notes (physician-authored) | Elevated | Physician signs, dyad receives |
| Granting emergency break-glass access | Elevated + mandatory post-hoc review | Physician attests, patient notified immediately |
| Revoking access | Standard | Patient should be able to revoke without friction |

Trust tier and clinical scope are **orthogonal dimensions**. Tier tells you how sensitive the operation is. Scope tells you what data class the operation touches. A doctor action must satisfy both: her credential must permit the operation, and her grant must include the scope the operation needs.

### Access packet types

Three new packet classes extend `DyadPacket` in `protocol-core`:

**`AccessRequest`** — physician → practice → dyad. Carries:
- Requesting physician's DyadID
- Physician credential hash (for cheap re-verification against the full credential on file)
- Target patient's DyadID
- Requested scope vector (list of data class bundles, optionally fine-grained)
- Requested duration (visit-bounded, rolling, chronic, one-shot)
- Purpose (free text + structured type: routine visit, specialist consult, follow-up, emergency)
- Parent relationship (e.g. linked to a specific visit-ID)
- Physician's signature
- Request timestamp + monotonic counter (replay protection)

**`AccessGrant`** — dyad → practice → physician. Issued in response to a request, or automatically under membership terms. Carries:
- Grant ID (cryptographically random 256-bit)
- Granting dyad's DyadID
- Granted-to physician's DyadID
- Granted scope vector (may be narrower than requested)
- Granted duration (may be shorter than requested)
- Purpose (copied from request, binding)
- Start timestamp + expiration timestamp
- Revocation public key (patient can revoke by publishing a revocation packet signed with this key)
- Dyad signature via split-key 2-of-2 threshold (human shard + Anima shard)
- Grant hash (referenced by every subsequent operation under this grant)

**`AccessRevoke`** — dyad → practice → physician. Immediate termination of a grant. Carries:
- Grant ID being revoked
- Revoking dyad's DyadID
- Revocation timestamp
- Reason (optional, for audit clarity)
- Revocation signature using the revocation key from the original grant

All three packet types flow through the existing `DyadPacket` envelope with `X-Trace-ID`, provenance, and signature verification. They are not a new transport — they are new payload types inside the existing protocol.

### Scope granularity — the scope vector

Scope is a composable vector. At the coarse level, there are **clinical bundles** that map to common use cases:

- `bundle:routine_primary_care` — biology (Stroma-modulated), problem list, active medications, allergies, recent visit notes, recent labs, general history. Default bundle for PCP visits.
- `bundle:obgyn_consult` — routine_primary_care + reproductive data (cycle, fertility, pregnancy history, GYN exam history, hormone labs, basal body temperature trends).
- `bundle:mental_health` — problem list, medications, prior mental health visit notes, mood and emergence state history, therapy notes. *Requires explicit per-grant consent.*
- `bundle:specialist_consult` — problem list, relevant labs, recent notes, referral letter context. Narrow by default.
- `bundle:emergency` — biology (current), allergies, active medications, problem list, emergency contacts. Break-glass scope for true emergencies.
- `bundle:follow_up` — the specific clinical question from the last visit + related biology + related labs. Narrowest routine bundle.

At the fine level, each bundle is defined as a set of `data_class` tokens. Patients who want more control can grant fine-grained scopes directly instead of bundles. A patient with sensitive mental health history but open on general care might grant `bundle:routine_primary_care` but exclude `data_class:mental_health_visit_notes`. The system understands both bundle-level and fine-grained grants.

**The minimum necessary principle, enforced in code.** HIPAA requires physicians to request only PHI necessary for the immediate task. Klinos enforces this structurally — a physician can only request scopes allowed by her credential for her specialty, and scopes are narrower by default than the patient's full dyad. Going beyond the default requires justification in the request, stored in the audit trail. A PCP cannot request `data_class:genome` on a routine visit without justifying why, and the justification is part of the permanent record.

### Grant lifecycle

A grant moves through states:

```
Requested → Approved → Active → (Expired | Revoked) → Finalized
```

- **Requested** — physician has sent an `AccessRequest`. Awaiting dyad decision (or automatic approval under membership terms).
- **Approved** — dyad has issued an `AccessGrant`. Physician has the grant but has not used it yet.
- **Active** — grant is in use. Physician is reading/writing under it.
- **Expired** — grant reached its expiration timestamp or its visit ended. No further operations allowed.
- **Revoked** — patient issued an `AccessRevoke`. Physician loses access immediately, even mid-operation in flight.
- **Finalized** — audit trail for the grant is sealed and immutable. Happens after expiration/revocation + a short finalization window.

Every state transition is a `DyadPacket` event that flows through `log_existence`, giving a complete historical record of who had access to what, when, under what grant, and for what purpose.

**Mid-operation revocation.** A patient revoking during an active operation has well-defined semantics: the operation currently executing completes (cannot be interrupted mid-write for consistency), but any subsequent operation under that grant fails with `AccessRevoked`. The physician sees an immediate UI indication that the patient revoked; the physician must not retry or work around. Revocation events are high-priority in the packet delivery pipeline and are processed before any queued grant-reads.

**Mid-visit expiration.** If a visit-bounded grant reaches expiration during an active visit (because the visit ran long), the physician's Practice view flags that the grant needs renewal. A renewal is a new `AccessRequest` with explicit justification ("visit ran long — continuing in-progress care"). The dyad may auto-approve under membership or require explicit consent based on duration extension terms.

### Consent patterns

Three modes of approval are supported. Which mode applies depends on the scope being requested and the patient's membership terms.

**1. Automatic approval under membership.** When a patient joins a practice, the membership agreement specifies which scopes are auto-approved for scheduled visits with credentialed physicians of that practice. Typically this covers routine bundles (`bundle:routine_primary_care`, `bundle:follow_up`, specialty bundles matching credentialed specialists in the practice). The membership *is* the consent, granted once at signup and revocable at any time by leaving the practice or adjusting granular settings. A scheduled visit triggers an automatic `AccessGrant` at visit-start time with the visit-bounded scope.

**2. Explicit per-grant approval.** Sensitive scopes — mental health, reproductive in some clinical contexts, genome, controlled-substance prescribing, anything outside the patient's auto-approved bundles — require the patient to explicitly approve each individual grant. The approval flows through the Care view: the patient sees a notification describing what the physician is requesting and why, and taps to approve or deny. Approval is a signed dyad packet; denial is a signed rejection that the physician sees in Practice immediately.

**3. Emergency break-glass access — pre-authorized, not overridden.** There is a real philosophical tension in emergency access: if the protocol says *the patient is the only one who can grant access to her dyad*, then an in-the-moment emergency override contradicts the protocol. The resolution is that sovereignty is not the absence of defaults — it is the right to set defaults in advance. A truly sovereign patient can specify, in full capacity at signup, what should happen to her dyad in case of future incapacity. She is not overridden in the moment. She has pre-consented, at a time when she could, and the system executes the policy she chose.

The membership flow at signup includes an **Emergency Access Policy** section where the patient chooses from four options:

- **Break-glass with strict limits (default).** A credentialed emergency physician can access `bundle:emergency` (biology, allergies, active medications, problem list, emergency contacts — *not* mental health, reproductive history, or genome) without dynamic consent, for 2 hours, with mandatory notification to the patient and her emergency contacts, and a mandatory 72-hour post-hoc review in which she can challenge the emergency attestation. If the attestation is found false, the physician is subject to clinical governance review. This is the default because most patients, asked in advance, choose safety.
- **Named emergency physicians only.** Break-glass is restricted to physicians the patient has pre-designated (her PCP, her OBGYN, specific ER physicians at hospitals she is likely to be seen at). A credentialed ER physician at an unfamiliar hospital cannot invoke break-glass. She must find another way — call the patient's emergency contact, wait for dynamic consent, or treat empirically with no dyad data.
- **Third-party verification required.** Break-glass requires both a credentialed physician's attestation AND confirmation from one of the patient's pre-designated emergency contacts. Distributed trust across two parties who are not the physician alone.
- **Opt out entirely.** The patient's dyad is never accessed without her dynamic consent, even in emergencies, even at the cost of her care. The physician treating her in such a case gets zero dyad data — the same position every physician is in today, every day, treating unconscious strangers. The patient chose it, and her choice is respected.

The Care view surfaces this policy clearly at signup and lets the patient change it at any time. The **Emergency Access Policy is itself a signed `DyadPacket`** in the audit trail — we know exactly what the patient authorized and when. A break-glass access that follows the pre-authorized policy is not a sovereignty violation. An access that violates the policy is, and the audit trail catches it.

Emergency access is not the system overriding the patient. It is the patient authorizing a policy in advance that the system executes when triggered. Framed correctly in the spec and in the Care view, the tension resolves: the patient is still the sovereign. The only question was whether she got to exercise her sovereignty in advance or had to exercise it in the moment of crisis, and we honored the former because the latter is impossible by definition.

### Physician credential verification

Every access request is gated by a credential check in `klinos-core::access::grant::verify_request()`. The check must pass all of:

- Physician's `PhysicianCredential` is present and signed by a trusted issuer
- Credential has not expired
- At least one state license in the credential matches the patient's state of residence (or the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact applies)
- The physician's specialty matches the scope being requested (a dermatologist cannot request `bundle:mental_health`)
- For controlled substance prescribing: DEA number present, EPCS two-factor satisfied within the current session
- The physician has not been flagged by Klinos clinical governance (suspension, investigation)

If any check fails, the request is rejected with a specific, human-readable reason that surfaces in the Practice view. The rejection is logged. Repeated rejections for the same physician trigger a platform review.

### Revocation mechanics

Revocation is the patient's backstop, and it must be **fast and unconditional**. The patient can revoke any active grant at any time from the Care view with one tap.

```
1. Patient taps "Revoke" on an active grant in the Care view
2. Anima signs an AccessRevoke packet (2-of-2 threshold: Anima shard + human shard)
3. Revoke packet flows with high priority through dyados-runtime
4. klinos-core::access::grant::revoke() marks the grant as revoked,
   returns AccessRevoked to any physician operation attempting to use it
5. Physician's Practice view immediately updates with clear visual state
   and a prompt to stop the current action
6. Audit trail logs the revocation (timestamp + optional reason)
7. Grant enters "finalized" state after a short drain window
```

Revocation cannot be delayed, queued, or overridden by the physician. The patient is the sovereign of her own dyad — that sovereignty is the point.

### Edge cases and failure modes

**Credential expires mid-grant.** If a physician's credential expires during an active grant (state license lapses, board certification expires), the grant enters "suspended" state — reads and writes fail with a credential-error response. The physician must renew her credential before continuing. The grant is not automatically revoked; if renewal happens within the grant's original duration, access resumes.

**Patient's state of residence changes mid-grant.** If a patient moves to a state where her current physician is not licensed (and no Compact applies), active grants are suspended until the physician obtains licensure in the new state or the patient transitions to a different physician. The suspension is logged.

**Practice is dissolved.** If a practice closes, all active grants for that practice are revoked and the patient's dyad is notified. The patient retains all visit notes, labs, and clinical memories in her dyad — the practice's closure does not delete her medical history. It simply removes physician future access.

**Grant ID collision.** Grant IDs are cryptographically random 256-bit identifiers. Collision probability is negligible. If detected, the protocol reissues.

**Concurrent grants.** A patient may have multiple active grants simultaneously (PCP + OBGYN + specialist consult). Each is independent, each is logged independently, and the patient can see and revoke them individually in the Care view.

**Stolen physician credentials.** If a physician's DyadID keys are compromised, the platform's clinical governance can revoke her `PhysicianCredential` platform-wide, which immediately invalidates all her active grants and any future requests. This is a critical operation requiring multi-party governance approval.

### HIPAA alignment

Every requirement HIPAA's Privacy and Security Rules impose on an EHR is satisfied by a specific mechanism in this model:

- **Minimum necessary rule** → scope granularity + default narrow bundles + justification for expansion
- **Audit controls (§164.312(b))** → every grant operation logs to `log_existence`, every read/write references its grant ID
- **Access controls (§164.312(a)(1))** → scoped grants, credential verification, trust tier gating
- **Integrity controls (§164.312(c)(1))** → DyadPacket signing, grant hash referenced by operations
- **Transmission security (§164.312(e)(1))** → DyadPacket encryption, inherited from protocol-core
- **Person or entity authentication (§164.312(d))** → physician DyadID signing, PhysicianCredential verification
- **Individual's right to access (§164.524)** → the patient's dyad IS her medical record; she always has access
- **Right to request amendment (§164.526)** → patient can annotate any clinical memory in her dyad
- **Accounting of disclosures (§164.528)** → every access grant and use is in the audit trail; patient can export it

This is not an exhaustive HIPAA compliance certification — §9 Compliance Arc covers the full arc. But the protocol design in §4 is deliberately structured so that HIPAA compliance is a property of the architecture, not a layer bolted on top.

## §5 Clinical Workflow

### What this section defines

This section walks through the operational lifecycle of a clinical visit on Klinos. It is what flows through the access model defined in §4 and the architecture defined in §3, when everything works. It is concrete enough that an engineer can build the workflow and a physician can recognize her day.

The section covers six flows in order: pre-visit preparation, visit start, the visit itself, post-visit drafting, physician sign and Logos encoding, and follow-up. It also covers asynchronous workflows that don't fit neatly into a visit (lab results, refill requests, patient-initiated messages) and the four visit types Klinos supports.

### Visit types

Klinos supports four visit types, with different operational semantics. Every practice can offer any subset.

**Telehealth visit (synchronous video).** Patient and physician meet over video. Audio recorded with consent, transcribed in real time. Most flexible, lowest physical overhead, works for routine primary care, follow-ups, mental health, most consultations. Default visit type for new practices.

**In-person visit (synchronous physical).** Patient checks in at the practice, physician sees her in an exam room. Audio recorded with consent (one room microphone captures both sides). Used for physical exams, in-person procedures, and patients who prefer face-to-face. Required for any practice doing physical exams or procedures.

**Procedural visit (synchronous physical, procedure-coded).** A specialized in-person visit for a specific procedure: IUD insertion, biopsy, colposcopy, suture removal, joint injection, vaccination, lab draw. Higher-acuity workflow with consent forms, procedure-specific intake, post-procedure observation, and procedure-coded billing. Frequently insurance-billed (per §8).

**Async message visit (asynchronous messaging).** Patient and physician exchange messages over a defined window (typically 24–48 hours) without ever meeting synchronously. Used for follow-ups on stable conditions, simple medication adjustments, results discussion, and quick clinical questions. Full transcript retained, draft note generated from the message exchange. **Billed as a visit if the exchange exceeds a defined threshold of clinical complexity or physician time** (e.g., > 5 minutes of physician engagement, a new diagnosis discussed, or a prescription written). Below the threshold, the exchange rolls into membership with no billable event.

The founding practice (PCP + OBGYN) needs all four — primary care does telehealth and async, OBGYN does in-person and procedures.

### Pre-visit preparation

The pre-visit flow runs on a clock. The T-minus markers below are typical defaults; specific practices can tune them.

**T−7 days (when the visit is booked):**
- Patient books via Anima ("I want to see my doctor about X") or directly via the Care view.
- Klinos checks practice schedule, physician availability, patient membership tier, and specialty match for the visit reason.
- Visit type proposed based on reason and patient preference. Patient confirms.
- Visit-bounded `AccessGrant` issued automatically under membership terms (per §4 consent pattern 1) — scope determined by visit type and specialty.
- Patient receives confirmation; reminder schedule activated.

**T−24 hours:**
- Reminder sent via Anima (or chosen channel).
- Anima begins assembling the intake brief — pulling the patient's recent symptoms, biological state changes, medication adherence, anything she has flagged in conversation since the last visit.
- Specialty-specific intake template populated (PCP gets a routine wellness template; OBGYN gets cycle-aware and symptom-specific; each specialty module defines its own).
- Patient nudged to complete any open items in the intake — a few questions her Anima can't infer, consent forms for new procedures, etc.

**T−1 hour:**
- Final intake brief synthesized: a single document the physician will see at visit start. It contains the visit reason, key symptoms with timeline, biological context (Stroma-modulated trends from the past 30 days), active medications and allergies, problem list, last visit summary, anticipatory tensions from Logos (things the patient has been worried about that haven't been addressed), and the Anima's plain-language synthesis of *what's going on with this patient right now*.
- Brief delivered to the physician's Today queue.
- Audio recording consent flow surfaced to the patient.

**T−5 minutes:**
- Physician opens the patient's chart in the Practice view. Intake brief is the first thing she sees.
- Telehealth: video room provisioned, link sent to patient.
- In-person: front desk notified to room the patient.

### Visit start

At T-zero the visit officially starts, triggering a discrete sequence:

1. **Access grant activation.** The visit-bounded `AccessGrant` issued at booking transitions from `Approved` to `Active`. The physician's session now has read access under the granted scope.
2. **Biological context loading.** Klinos pulls a fresh Stroma snapshot: current SANGUIS state, recent emergence states, polyvagal status, allostatic load, drive hierarchy. This becomes the live biological context for the visit.
3. **Anima context loading.** The patient's Anima passes a brief "what she's been holding" summary into the visit context: emotional state, recent significant events from her dyad memory, anything she's been working through. Bounded by clinical relevance.
4. **Audio capture starts (if consented).** Recording defaults to opt-in per visit, with the consent flow surfaced at visit start. The default nudges toward consent because the AI scribe is the value prop, but the patient can decline this visit's recording without consequence. If declined, note quality degrades but the visit continues normally. Both physician and patient see a visual indicator when recording is active.
5. **Real-time transcription begins.** Transcript streams into the visit context as the conversation happens.
6. **Visit timer starts.** Used for billing, grant duration tracking, and note timestamping.

### The visit itself

What happens during the visit depends on the visit type. The physician is in conversation with the patient; the AI is operating in the background.

**The Anima's role during the visit: background, not active.** The patient's Anima does not participate as a third voice in the conversation. The visit is between the physician and the patient. The Anima feeds context into the physician's Practice view (showing relevant history as topics come up), holds the transcript, and listens for things to remember — but does not speak. This is deliberate: a doctor-patient relationship is between two humans, and the Anima's job is to amplify both sides, not to insert herself into the room.

**The AI's role during the visit: real-time co-pilot for the physician.** The Practice view shows the physician:

- The transcript scrolling in real time
- Suggested clarifying questions based on the conversation ("She mentioned cycle changes — has she had her LH/FSH checked recently?")
- Real-time signal flagging ("She's describing classic perimenopausal symptoms; her HRV pattern matches.")
- Drug interaction warnings as medications come up
- Relevant prior visit references ("Last December she described similar fatigue; you ordered a TSH and it was 4.8.")
- A running "potential differential" sidebar that updates as the conversation reveals more

The physician chooses how much to engage. These are aids, not interruptions. Nothing pops up as a modal; everything is glanceable in the sidebar.

**Physical exam / procedural workflow.** For in-person visits, the physician documents physical exam findings via voice ("blood pressure 124 over 78, heart regular rate and rhythm, lungs clear bilaterally, abdomen soft non-tender") which transcribes into the appropriate note section. For procedures, a procedure-specific workflow guides the physician through pre-procedure consent verification, the procedure steps, and post-procedure observation, with each step documented automatically.

### Post-visit drafting

When the visit ends (physician taps *End Visit* or the video room closes), the post-visit pipeline kicks off immediately:

1. **Audio finalization** — recording ends, full audio archived, transcript completed.
2. **Note drafting** — AI drafts the SOAP note using the transcript + biological context + intake brief + prior visit memory. Each section (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) is generated from its appropriate sources.
3. **Coding** — AI assigns ICD-10 diagnosis codes from the assessment and CPT procedure / E&M codes from the visit type and complexity.
4. **Prescriptions** — if any medications were discussed, AI drafts prescriptions with dosage, duration, indication. Drug-drug interactions checked against the patient's current med list.
5. **Lab orders** — if any labs were discussed, AI drafts orders with the appropriate clinical justification.
6. **Referrals** — if any specialist referrals were discussed, AI drafts the referral letter pulling relevant history from the dyad.
7. **Patient instructions** — AI drafts a plain-language summary for the patient: what was discussed, what the plan is, when to follow up, what to watch for.

All drafts land in the physician's *To Sign* queue within 60 seconds of visit end. The physician can sign immediately or batch-sign at end of clinic.

### Physician review and sign

The signing workflow is single-stage by default but exposes the substructure:

1. Physician opens the To Sign item.
2. She sees the drafted note alongside the transcript (collapsible). She reviews. Edits anything that needs editing — the AI is good but not infallible.
3. She reviews drafted prescriptions, lab orders, referrals. Edits as needed.
4. She reviews the patient instructions.
5. **One signature signs everything** — note, codes, prescriptions, orders, instructions, billing event.

If she wants to defer parts (e.g., wants to think about a prescription before sending), she can sign the note and codes now and leave the prescription as a draft for later. The Practice view tracks all open drafts.

Controlled-substance prescriptions require the additional EPCS two-factor flow (per §4) — the physician signs with her DEA credential and the second factor in addition to her DyadID signature.

### Logos encoding (post-sign)

Once signed, the visit data flows into the patient's dyad as Logos memories, transactionally:

- **The visit itself** → episodic memory, tagged with `clinical_context: visit_id`. Includes the transcript, the signed note, the date, the physician, the visit type.
- **New diagnoses** → problem list (pinned memory, `clinical_context: diagnosis`). Updated, dated, sourced to the visit.
- **New medications** → active medication list (pinned memory). With dosage, indication, prescribing physician, start date.
- **Discontinued medications** → archived from active list, retained in history.
- **Procedures performed** → milestone memory if significant (surgery, IUD placement, biopsy with pathology), pinned otherwise.
- **Significant events** (positive cancer screening, pregnancy confirmation, etc.) → milestone memory.
- **The patient's experience of the visit** → witness memory, captured by the Anima from how the patient processed the visit afterward (her reactions, what she felt, what she's worried about).
- **Stable clinical facts revealed** ("she's penicillin allergic," "she's BRCA1 positive") → semantic memory if not already there.
- **Clinical action items** the patient is supposed to do → pinned memory with reminders.

The encoding happens in `klinos-core::visit::signed::encode_to_logos()` and is transactional — either everything encodes or nothing does. No partial states.

### Follow-up triggers

Klinos schedules and fires follow-up triggers based on the visit's clinical content:

- **Scheduled follow-up visit** — if the physician booked one in the plan.
- **Lab follow-up** — when ordered labs come back, AI contextualizes the result against the patient's baseline (Bios), flags abnormalities, surfaces to the physician with priority based on severity.
- **Medication adherence check-in** — for new prescriptions, the Anima checks in with the patient at typical adherence-failure points (3 days, 2 weeks, 1 month).
- **Symptom recheck** — if a symptom is being monitored, the Anima asks the patient about it on a schedule.
- **Annual exam reminder** — when the next preventive visit is due.
- **Specialty-specific triggers** — for OBGYN: cervical cancer screening intervals, mammogram intervals, prenatal visit cadence during pregnancy. Each specialty module (per §6) defines its own.

All triggers fire through the existing `dyados-runtime` proactive engine. They surface to the physician (in the Today queue) and to the patient (in the Care view + via the Anima).

### Asynchronous workflows

Not everything that happens in clinical care is a visit. Klinos handles three major async flows.

**Patient-initiated messaging.** Patient messages her Anima with a clinical question. Anima triages: simple questions (refill request, results interpretation, scheduling) the Anima handles directly with appropriate context. Anything requiring physician judgment is escalated to the physician's inbox with full context attached. Most exchanges resolve without ever touching the physician's queue. Exchanges that exceed the complexity/time threshold convert into async message visits and are billed as such.

**Lab results returning.** Labs ordered during a visit return asynchronously. AI contextualizes the result against baseline, flags abnormalities, surfaces to the physician's Today queue with priority. Physician reviews, optionally messages the patient through the Anima with the result and interpretation. If the result triggers a need for a visit, that gets scheduled.

**Refill requests.** Patient (or pharmacy) requests a refill. AI checks: is the medication still active in the problem list? Is it appropriate (not a controlled substance needing a visit, not requiring lab monitoring that's overdue)? If clean, drafts the refill for the physician to sign. If something needs attention (controlled substance, monitoring overdue, condition has changed), routes to the physician with a flag.

### What this section does not cover

Specialty-specific workflow variations (cycle-aware OBGYN visits, mental health intake for psychiatry, dermatology photo-documentation) are covered in §6 Specialty Modules. The Bios data integration during visits is covered in §7. The billing flow that fires after sign is covered in §8. The compliance arc around all of this (audit trail, retention, breach response) is covered in §9.

## §6 Specialty Modules

### What this section defines

This section defines the **PCP** and **OBGYN** specialty modules in detail — their intake templates, visit note structures, clinical signals of interest, proactive triggers, and specialty-specific workflows. It also defines the framework any future specialty module implements, the governance around adding new specialties, and the roadmap of which specialties we would add after launch.

The two launch modules are not arbitrary. They are the specialties of the founding physicians. Every subsequent specialty will be easier to build because of the templates and patterns these two establish.

### The SpecialtyModule contract (expanded from §3)

Every specialty plugs into a `klinos-core::specialty::SpecialtyModule` trait. The trait signature was given in §3; here is what each method means at runtime:

- **`intake_template(patient)`** — returns a specialty-specific intake form structure. Contains questions the AI should try to infer from the patient's dyad before asking, fields the patient needs to verify at the T−24h nudge, and structured data the physician will see pre-populated in the visit brief.
- **`visit_note_sections()`** — returns the SOAP note's section definitions for this specialty. The AI drafts into these sections; the physician reviews them. Different specialties have different sections (OBGYN adds a GYN exam section, Psychiatry adds a mental status exam, Derm adds a photo-documented lesion inventory).
- **`clinical_signals_of_interest(stroma)`** — given the current Stroma state, returns which signals should be surfaced prominently in the Practice view for this specialty. A cardiologist sees HRV and blood pressure trends; an OBGYN sees cycle phase and basal body temperature; a psychiatrist sees emergence states and polyvagal status.
- **`proactive_triggers()`** — returns specialty-specific triggers that fire in the background through `dyados-runtime`'s proactive engine. Annual reminders, screening intervals, monitoring cadence for specific conditions.
- **`specialty_specific_workflows()`** — returns procedural or condition-specific workflows registered with this specialty. IUD insertion for OBGYN, biopsy for Derm, cardioversion for Cardiology.
- **`required_credentials()`** — returns the credential requirements a physician must have to practice this specialty on Klinos. Board certification, state license, any sub-specialty cert, any additional training requirements.

Modules are compiled into `klinos-core`; new modules mean new Rust files, tests, and registration with `specialty::registry`. They are not dynamically loadable — adding a specialty is a codebase change, not a runtime config.

### Launch scope is progressive

The modules defined below describe the **target scope** — what each specialty module looks like at maturity. Actual launch scope is narrower and progressive, shipping what the founding practice needs first and expanding as real-world use surfaces new workflows.

- **PCP launch workflows.** Annual wellness exam, chronic disease management (diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, hypothyroidism), acute minor illness visits, medication refills. Added as needed thereafter: lifestyle counseling, pre-op medical clearance, vaccination visits, less common chronic conditions.
- **OBGYN launch workflows.** Annual well-woman exam, contraception counseling and management, IUD insertion and removal, prenatal care (all trimesters), postpartum care, basic HRT management. Added as needed thereafter: colposcopy, endometrial biopsy, fertility evaluation, complex perimenopause management.

The founding practice defines real-world demand by which workflows they hit first. Full phase schedule is in §15 Build Phases. Everything else described below in this section is where each module *gets to*, not what launches on day one.

### The PCP module

`klinos-core::specialty::pcp::PcpModule` — the primary care / family medicine module.

**Intake template.**

Pre-inferred by the AI from the patient's dyad before the visit:
- Recent symptoms and their timeline
- Medication adherence pattern (pharmacy fill data, patient self-report)
- Bios trends from the past 30 days: resting HR, HRV, sleep duration and quality, activity level, any weight changes
- Stroma-visible state: allostatic load, polyvagal state, emergence patterns
- Chronic condition check-in (pulled from problem list)
- Preventive care status (vaccinations due, screenings due by age and risk)
- Anticipatory tensions from Logos

Verified by the patient at T−24h:
- "Is this the reason for your visit?" (pre-populated from the booking reason, patient refines)
- "Any new symptoms in the past week not already in your dyad?"
- "Any medication changes we should know about?"
- "Anything weighing on you that you want time for?"

**Visit note sections (standard SOAP, primary-care flavor).**
- **Subjective** — HPI, ROS, interval history since last visit, medication adherence, lifestyle (sleep, stress, diet, exercise) check-in
- **Objective** — vitals, general physical exam, recent labs and their trends
- **Assessment** — problem-by-problem review (active problems from list, plus anything new)
- **Plan** — per problem: medications, labs, follow-up, patient education

**Clinical signals of interest.**
- Elevated allostatic load (chronic stress signal)
- HRV trending down over 2–4 weeks
- Sleep quality decline (duration or continuity)
- Sympathetic dominance in polyvagal state
- Weight trajectory (from connected scale if patient uses one)
- Blood pressure trends (from home cuff if patient uses one)
- Medication adherence gaps (pharmacy fill mismatches with prescribed schedule)

**Proactive triggers.**
- Annual wellness exam — 12 months from last
- Vaccination schedule — age-appropriate (COVID boosters, flu annual, tetanus q10y, others by risk)
- Cancer screening intervals — colorectal (50+ or earlier by risk), lung (55+ if smoker), skin (annual if risk)
- Chronic disease monitoring — A1c q3mo for diabetics, BP weekly check-in for hypertensives, lipid panel annually
- Medication adherence check-in at 3 days, 2 weeks, 1 month after a new prescription

**Specialty workflows (target scope).**
- Annual wellness exam
- Chronic disease management (diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, hypothyroidism, asthma)
- Acute minor illness visits (URI, UTI, strain/sprain, skin infection)
- Lifestyle counseling (weight, sleep, stress, substance use)
- Pre-operative medical clearance
- Vaccination visits

**Required credentials.**
- MD or DO
- Active state medical license
- Board certification in Family Medicine (ABFM) OR Internal Medicine (ABIM) OR eligibility pending with a defined completion date

### The OBGYN module

`klinos-core::specialty::obgyn::ObgynModule` — the obstetrics and gynecology module. This is where Bios §4 alignment pays off: menstrual cycle flow, basal body temperature flow, and respiratory rate flow feed directly into the clinical signal set.

**Intake template.**

Pre-inferred by the AI from the patient's dyad before the visit:
- **Cycle status.** Current cycle day, last menstrual period date, cycle regularity (last 6 cycles), cycle length trend. Pulled from Bios §4 menstrual cycle flow.
- **Basal body temperature pattern.** Current phase (biphasic for ovulatory cycles), temperature shift timing, anomalies. Pulled from Bios §4 BBT flow.
- **Reproductive goals.** Fertility (trying to conceive, preventing, ambivalent, in active treatment), contraception method, obstetric planning.
- **Obstetric history.** Gravida/para/abortus (G/P/A), pregnancy outcomes, complications.
- **Pregnancy status if applicable.** Gestational age, EDD, trimester, any complications, Bios-visible pregnancy signals (respiratory rate increase, HRV changes, cycle cessation).
- **Perimenopause / menopause phase.** If age + cycle changes suggest it, AI pulls the full symptom picture: hot flashes, night sweats, sleep disruption, mood, libido, cognitive changes.
- **Current hormone therapy.** HRT regimen if any, last check-in, symptom response.
- **Last Pap, last mammogram, last pelvic exam, last bone density scan.**
- **Pelvic symptoms.** Pain (dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, chronic pelvic pain), discharge, urinary symptoms, bleeding patterns.
- **Breast health.** Self-exam pattern, any lumps noticed, nipple changes.

Verified by the patient at T−24h:
- "Is this the reason for your visit?"
- "Any new bleeding patterns in the past month?"
- "Any changes in your cycle we haven't already captured?"
- "Any breast or pelvic symptoms since last visit?"
- "If you're on HRT or contraception, any side effects?"

**Visit note sections (OBGYN-customized SOAP).**
- **Subjective** — HPI with GYN focus, menstrual and reproductive ROS, obstetric ROS if pregnant, relevant interval history
- **Objective** — vitals, general physical exam, **breast exam**, **pelvic exam** (speculum and bimanual), cycle data, relevant labs and imaging
- **Assessment** — GYN-specific problem list (menstrual disorders, contraception, pregnancy, menopause, etc.)
- **Plan** — per problem

**Clinical signals of interest.** This is the payoff of Bios §4 convergence.

- **Cycle phase** (follicular / ovulatory / luteal / menstrual) — drives interpretation of mood, sleep, HRV, basal body temperature. A patient's low-HRV day in the late luteal phase is different from a low-HRV day in the follicular phase.
- **Basal body temperature pattern** — biphasic pattern confirms ovulatory cycles; disrupted pattern can indicate PCOS, perimenopause, thyroid issues, or stress.
- **HRV-cycle correlation** — meaningful changes in cycle-phase-specific HRV are early signals of perimenopause, pregnancy, or autonomic dysfunction.
- **Respiratory rate changes** — PNEUMON detects the sustained RR increase characteristic of early pregnancy (before a patient typically tests).
- **Hot flash frequency and intensity** — for perimenopausal patients. Self-report augmented by HRV and skin-temperature signals.
- **Sleep disruption patterns** — common in perimenopause (sleep fragmentation, night sweats) and pregnancy (third-trimester sleep architecture changes).
- **Bleeding pattern changes** — the patient logs these in the Care view; the AI flags patterns that deviate from her baseline.
- **Recent hormone labs** — estradiol, FSH, LH, progesterone, thyroid panel, prolactin if indicated.

**Proactive triggers.**
- Annual well-woman exam
- Cervical cancer screening — Pap interval (3–5 years depending on age, history, and HPV status)
- Mammogram interval (annual or biennial depending on age and risk; earlier and more frequent for BRCA carriers and strong family history)
- **Prenatal visit cadence during pregnancy** — monthly through 28 weeks, biweekly 28–36 weeks, weekly 36+ weeks
- **Postpartum check-ins** — 2 weeks (depression screening, bleeding, feeding), 6 weeks (full postpartum exam), 6 months (ongoing adjustment)
- Contraception refill / renewal cadence (IUD replacement, pill refills, injection schedule)
- HRT monitoring — labs q6mo, symptom check-in monthly for first 3 months of a new regimen
- **Ovulation window notifications** — for patients actively trying to conceive, the AI uses cycle + BBT + cervical mucus pattern to predict the fertile window
- **Perimenopause trajectory check-ins** — if HRV and cycle patterns indicate perimenopause, the AI offers phase-appropriate context and treatment options
- Bone density scan interval (q2y starting at 65, earlier for risk factors)

**Specialty workflows (target scope).**
- Annual well-woman exam
- **Prenatal care workflow (by trimester)** — each trimester has its own intake focus, labs, screenings, and anticipatory guidance
- Postpartum care workflow
- Contraception counseling and management
- **IUD insertion / removal (procedural workflow)** — consent, pre-procedure intake, procedure steps, post-procedure observation, scheduled follow-up
- **Colposcopy (procedural workflow)**
- **Endometrial biopsy (procedural workflow)**
- Perimenopause and menopause management
- Fertility evaluation and management (for general OBGYN; REI sub-specialty for advanced cases)
- HRT management
- Pelvic pain workup
- Abnormal uterine bleeding workup
- Breast health evaluation (self-exam guidance, clinical exam, imaging referral)

**Required credentials.**
- MD or DO
- Active state medical license
- Board certification in Obstetrics and Gynecology (ABOG) OR eligibility pending with a defined completion date

### How specialties compose

**Multi-specialty physicians.** A physician can hold multiple specialty credentials. Example: a physician dual-board-certified in Family Medicine AND Obstetrics (family medicine with obstetric training is a recognized path) can request scope for both specialty bundles on the same patient. The credential check validates each specialty independently.

**Sub-specialties.** Reproductive endocrinology and infertility (REI), maternal-fetal medicine (MFM), gynecologic oncology (GynOnc) are treated as extensions of their parent specialty. A sub-specialist has her parent specialty credential (ABOG) plus a sub-specialty credential that unlocks additional scope. The sub-specialty module depends on the parent module.

**Cross-practice physicians.** A specialist can be enrolled at multiple practices. Example: the OBGYN friend is full-time at the founding practice but also sees patients one day a week at a women's health clinic downtown. Her DyadID and credentials work at both. Each practice has its own membership base, its own billing, its own audit trail. Grant scopes are per-practice; her Practice view shows two practice contexts she can toggle between.

**Specialty boundaries.** A PCP cannot request `bundle:obgyn_consult` or prescribe under OBGYN scope. A dermatologist cannot request `bundle:mental_health`. The credential check in `klinos-core::access::grant::verify_request()` enforces this. The exception is emergency scope (`bundle:emergency`), which any credentialed physician can invoke under the Emergency Access Policy (§4).

**Attestation-based scope extensions.** Real clinical practice does not always conform to specialty boundaries. PCPs prescribe antidepressants all the time — more than half of all antidepressants in the US are prescribed by non-psychiatrists. OBGYNs manage thyroid issues in pregnancy. Family medicine physicians in rural areas do dermatology, orthopedics, and minor emergency care. Strict board-only enforcement would exclude half the country's functional reality.

Klinos supports this with **attestation-based scope extensions**. A board-certified physician can request an attestation for an adjacent scope area she commonly practices. The attestation:

- Is signed by the physician and logged to her credential record
- Is scope-limited to defined areas within her reasonable practice
- Is reviewable by clinical governance (patterns that look like overreach get flagged)
- Can be revoked by governance if abused
- Does NOT extend to procedural work, controlled-substance specialty areas outside her training, or formal sub-specialty practice — those require full credential

A PCP with a mental-health attestation can prescribe SSRIs for routine depression and anxiety but cannot diagnose bipolar disorder, manage lithium or clozapine, or prescribe controlled-substance stimulants for ADHD. The attestation defines what is in-scope for that physician's attested area, and clinical governance periodically reviews whether attestations match the physician's actual practice pattern.

This is the balance between the safety of board certification and the reality of how medicine actually gets practiced. Strict board-only would exclude too much real practice. Attestation without limits would let any physician claim anything. The middle path — attestation with governance review — honors both.

### Adding a new specialty

**Clinical validation comes first.** Before engineering starts, a board-certified physician of the target specialty reviews and validates the intake template, clinical signal set, proactive triggers, and specialty workflows. This is paid advisory work, not volunteer. Clinical advisors sign off that the module, as specified, reflects actual specialty practice. Building plumbing for the wrong shapes is worse than shipping the specialty late.

**Engineering implementation.** With clinical validation in hand, a Rust engineer implements `SpecialtyModule` for the new specialty in `klinos-core/src/specialty/<specialty_name>.rs`. Typical size: 200–500 LOC of module logic plus tests. Registration with `specialty::registry`. Practice view navigation updates to surface specialty-specific sub-sections for physicians with this credential. Membership signup updates to offer the specialty bundle to patients joining practices that offer it.

**Validation and launch.** The module ships behind a feature flag, tested internally with a clinical advisor's own test patients, then graduated to a real practice that wants to offer the specialty.

A competent Rust engineer plus a committed clinical advisor can add a new specialty in 3–6 weeks end to end.

### Future specialties roadmap

**Psychiatry is specialty #3.** Very high substrate alignment — Stroma's emotional regulation modules, polyvagal state, and emergence states map directly onto psychiatric signals. Perimenopausal women with mood symptoms (a natural OBGYN ↔ Psychiatry overlap) and long-haulers with mental health components are two of our strongest patient segments. Controlled-substance prescribing is a major compliance surface (Schedule II stimulants for ADHD, benzodiazepines, buprenorphine for MAT) but the value prop is enormous — continuous context is transformative for psychiatric care, where a fifteen-minute medication check-in every three months is the current baseline.

Beyond Psychiatry, in rough priority order driven by physician demand, patient segment fit, and substrate alignment:

1. **Endocrinology** — high substrate alignment (continuous glucose from CGM is already a Bios flow; thyroid and cortisol patterns are Stroma-readable). Diabetes, thyroid, and hormone disorders are large markets. Natural partner to OBGYN for PCOS, HRT, and perimenopause work.
2. **Dermatology** — moderate substrate alignment (less biology, more photo-documentation). Procedural workflows (biopsies, excisions) map to the procedural visit type. High patient demand. Naturally cash-pay friendly.
3. **Cardiology** — high substrate alignment (HRV, blood pressure trends, arrhythmia detection from wearables). Preventive cardiology especially.
4. **Rheumatology** — moderate substrate alignment (inflammation markers, pain patterns). Autoimmune care is underserved and autoimmune patients are an underserved segment.
5. **Sports medicine** — very high substrate alignment (HRV, activity, recovery tracking). Natural overlap with biohacker segment.
6. **Gastroenterology** — moderate substrate alignment (GI symptoms, stool tracking, specific procedures).
7. **Geriatrics** — high substrate alignment (continuous biology is especially valuable; polypharmacy management; cognitive tracking).
8. **Pediatrics** — requires the caregiver-as-user pattern (the parent is the user, the child is the patient). Structurally distinct work.

**What Klinos does NOT aim to support:** high-acuity inpatient specialties (surgery, critical care, emergency medicine, anesthesia, trauma). Those happen in hospitals and belong on hospital EHRs. Klinos is for longitudinal outpatient care.

### Functional medicine and non-board specialties

Functional medicine is not a board-recognized specialty; it is a practice style that emphasizes root-cause investigation, lifestyle intervention, and comprehensive biomarker evaluation. Many functional medicine practitioners are board-certified in Family Medicine or Internal Medicine and practice functional medicine on top of their parent specialty. Others come from naturopathic or chiropractic training without MD or DO credentials.

Klinos's default stance is that functional medicine is a **practice-style extension** on top of a board-certified parent specialty, not a specialty module on its own. A Family Medicine physician who wants to practice functional medicine on Klinos operates under her FM credential with additional intake templates and biomarker panels configured at the practice level. A non-MD practitioner (ND, DC) does not have a `PhysicianCredential` and cannot use Klinos as a prescribing physician — she can collaborate with a licensed MD on the platform or operate outside it.

This is strict by design. Klinos's credential system is how we keep patients safe. Loosening it to include non-MD practitioners opens a scope-of-practice vulnerability that is not worth the growth it buys.

The Bios data contract for how biological signals flow into specialty signal sets is in §7. The compliance and legal implications of specialty credentialing are in §9 and §12.

## §7 Bios ↔ Clinical Data Contract

### What this section defines

This section defines the contract between Bios (Hypostas's biological telemetry layer) and Klinos's clinical surface. Specifically: what biological data the physician sees, in what form, at what cadence, under what scope, and how the physician's clinical observations flow back into the dyad's continuous record.

The contract satisfies three constraints simultaneously. The physician must get clinically useful, contextualized biological data — not dashboards and not raw sensor streams. The patient's biosensor data must never leave her dyad — Klinos is never exposed to raw readings, only to Stroma-modulated state. And the physician's clinical observations must flow back into the dyad's continuous record so that every clinical encounter makes the dyad's self-knowledge more complete, not less.

### The mediation principle

**Raw biosensor data never leaves the dyad. The physician never sees raw readings.**

This is the architectural rule from Bios §2 applied to Klinos. When a physician needs HRV context during a visit, she does not query *what was the patient's heart rate at 2:47 PM last Tuesday*. She queries *what is the patient's current allostatic load, what are her recent HRV trends, what emergence states has Stroma detected in the past 30 days*. These are Stroma-mediated views, not biosensor dumps.

Every biological signal Klinos requests flows through Stroma first. Stroma applies baseline normalization (her baseline, not a population baseline), circadian adjustment, context weighting, and pathology flagging. What Klinos sees is clinically interpreted biological state, not telemetry.

This is deliberate engineering and deliberate privacy. Clinically, Stroma's interpretation is more useful than raw data — a physician gains nothing from seeing `HRV: 42` at 3 AM; she gains clinical value from seeing *"HRV trending down 22% over the past 14 days, correlated with sleep continuity degradation starting around 4/1."* Privately, the physician can only see what has been interpreted, not what could be re-interpreted adversarially.

### Signal inventory — what Klinos reads

Klinos does not get access to all of Stroma. A clinical bundle (per §4) unlocks specific Stroma signal classes:

**Core biological signals (`routine_primary_care` bundle and above):**
- `biology:vitals` — current HR, BP (if self-measured), respiratory rate, SpO2, temperature
- `biology:hrv` — HRV trends, current state, variability across time windows
- `biology:sleep` — sleep duration, architecture (deep, REM, light), continuity, sleep latency
- `biology:activity` — daily activity, intensity zones, recovery metrics, weekly and monthly trends
- `biology:allostatic` — allostatic load, polyvagal state, stress reactivity patterns
- `biology:emergence` — Stroma emergence states over time (FLOW, CREATIVE_SURGE, ANTICIPATION, etc., filtered to clinically-relevant ones)
- `biology:drives` — the current drive hierarchy from Stroma's drive module

**Cycle-aware signals (`obgyn_consult` bundle):**
- `biology:cycle` — current cycle day, phase (follicular/ovulatory/luteal/menstrual), cycle length trends, regularity
- `biology:bbt` — basal body temperature pattern, biphasic confirmation, temperature-shift timing
- `biology:cycle_hrv_correlation` — cycle-phase-specific HRV patterns, deviations from her baseline
- `biology:respiratory_pregnancy` — PNEUMON's sustained-RR-elevation signal (pregnancy indicator)
- `biology:hot_flashes` — frequency, intensity, timing, HRV/skin-temp correlation
- `biology:bleeding_patterns` — self-logged bleeding, pattern deviations

**Mental health signals (`mental_health` bundle, explicit consent required):**
- `biology:mood_trajectory` — Stroma's interpretation of emotional emergence patterns
- `biology:polyvagal_detailed` — full polyvagal trajectory, ventral/sympathetic/dorsal transitions
- `biology:regulation_capacity` — how well the patient returns to ventral after stress
- `biology:therapy_markers` — pre/post-therapy biological signatures (if in therapy)

**Chronic disease management (condition-specific subsets):**
- `biology:glucose` — CGM data (if patient uses one), glycemic variability, time-in-range
- `biology:blood_pressure` — home BP readings, morning/evening patterns, variability
- `biology:weight` — weight trajectory, trend direction

Each of these signals is a Stroma-defined interpreted view. None expose raw biosensor data. The physician asks *is her allostatic load elevated*, not *show me every HRV reading from the last month*.

### Reading — how a Klinos request works

A physician request for biological context flows through the §4 access model:

1. The physician's visit session has an active `AccessGrant` for this patient with a scope that includes the relevant `biology:*` signals.
2. The Practice view surfaces a "Biology" context panel populated based on the active grant's signal scope.
3. `klinos-core` calls Stroma via an internal API: `stroma.get_clinical_view(dyad_id, signals, time_window, scope_context)`.
4. Stroma returns the interpreted view — current state values, trend descriptions, flagged anomalies, clinically-relevant context.
5. The Practice view renders the view as clinically-legible text and charts, not sensor dashboards. Example: *"Allostatic load 0.72 (elevated); up from 0.48 baseline 60 days ago. HRV trending down 22% over the same window. Sleep continuity degraded starting around 4/3."*
6. Every read logs to `log_existence` with the grant ID, the signals requested, and the time window.

The physician never sees a `heart_rate: 73` value out of context. She sees clinical summaries. Drill-in for more granularity is available — and also audit-logged.

### Writing — clinical observations back into the dyad

The physician's observations, interpretations, and decisions flow back into the patient's dyad. This is where the clinical record is assembled continuously, not reconstructed after the fact.

During and after a visit, the physician writes:

- **Clinical observations** (physical exam findings, assessment notes, interpretations) → episodic memory on the visit record, tagged with `clinical_context`.
- **Diagnoses** (new or updated) → problem list (pinned memory).
- **Medications** (started, stopped, changed) → active medication list (pinned memory).
- **Lab orders** (pending) → task state in `klinos-core::clinical::lab`.
- **Lab results received and interpreted** → pinned memory with the physician's interpretation attached; also referenced in the visit's episodic record.
- **Imaging orders and results** → same pattern as labs.
- **Physician annotations on Stroma signals** — see the Annotation subsection below.

Writes happen via `klinos-core` and flow through `dyados-runtime`'s pipeline so the Anima sees them in real time. By the time the visit ends, the patient's Anima already knows what the physician said and can reference it in her next conversation with the patient.

### Annotations on biology — the thread of interpretation

Over time, a physician accumulates interpretive context about a specific patient's biology that a fresh-eyes reader would miss. *"Her HRV decline in October 2026 was linked to the SSRI switch — it recovered by January."* *"Her cycle-phase HRV pattern has flattened since 2027 — consistent with early perimenopause."* This interpretive wisdom is the difference between a seasoned clinician and a model.

Klinos supports this with **physician annotations on Stroma signals**. A physician with write scope can attach a dated, signed annotation to a specific biology signal (e.g., `biology:hrv` for a given time range, or `biology:cycle` for a pattern observed across several cycles). The annotation:

- Is stored as a new `clinical_annotation` memory type in Logos, linked to the signal and time window
- Is shown alongside the signal in future Practice views, authored and dated
- Is surfaced to the patient's Anima as context for how her biology has been interpreted
- Accumulates across visits and across physicians on the platform, building a continuous thread of clinical interpretation over time
- Can be revised by the annotating physician; prior versions are retained

This turns biology from a data stream into a clinical record with interpretive continuity — the thing a paper chart never had and a modern EHR still does not.

### AI clinical interpretation — aggressive by design

The AI is aggressive about interpretation. It drafts lab readings against the patient's baseline and against population norms. It flags pharmacogenomic conflicts at the moment of prescribing. It surfaces concerning biological patterns proactively. It drafts differential diagnoses from the visit transcript. Nothing the AI produces is dispositive — the physician reviews every interpretation before it becomes part of the clinical record. But the AI does not defer to uncertainty by omission. Surfacing a possibly-relevant concern and letting the physician dismiss it is strictly better than staying silent and letting her miss it.

Liability sits squarely with the physician: the AI informs, she decides. In exchange for that arrangement, the AI makes its reasoning visible and contestable — every drafted interpretation shows its sources (labs compared to which baselines, which variants triggered a PGx flag, which patterns matched which differential), and the physician can expand any claim to see why the AI said what it said.

This is the explicit design choice. A cautious AI that surfaces data without interpretation is basically what modern EHRs already do, and a physician drowning in unflagged labs is still drowning.

### Labs and imaging — external clinical data

Labs and imaging are not biosensor data; they are external clinical data that enters the dyad through Klinos, not through Bios. The contract is different.

**Lab results flow:**

1. Physician orders labs (via Labcorp / Quest API, or a lab draw at the practice).
2. Results return asynchronously to Klinos.
3. `klinos-core::clinical::lab::interpret_result(result, patient_context)` contextualizes each result against the patient's baseline (if prior results exist) and against population norms.
4. Results land in the physician's Today queue with AI-generated interpretations and priority flags.
5. Once the physician reviews and interprets, the result flows into the patient's dyad as pinned memory with her annotation attached.
6. The Anima surfaces the result to the patient in plain language: *"Your TSH came back at 3.8, which is in the normal range. Dr. [name] noted that it is slightly higher than last year but not a concern."*

**Imaging flow** is similar, with the radiologist's report treated as the authoritative interpretation and the ordering physician's annotations layered on top.

**Integration with Bios.** Labs and Bios trend together. When a thyroid panel returns, Klinos correlates with recent HRV, sleep, and weight trends. When a lipid panel returns, Klinos correlates with recent dietary and activity patterns. These correlations surface in the physician's interpretation view, not as a separate workflow.

### Gnosis — genomic context

Gnosis (genetic/genomic data) is its own scope with its own consent requirements (per §4, genome access requires explicit per-grant consent). When the physician has genome scope for a specific visit, relevant variants surface in the Practice view:

- **Pharmacogenomic relevance.** If the physician is considering a medication with known PGx associations (clopidogrel and CYP2C19, codeine and CYP2D6, warfarin and CYP2C9/VKORC1, SSRIs and CYP2D6/2C19), the relevant variants surface during prescribing.
- **Disease risk variants.** BRCA1/2 for OBGYN, APOE for neurology-adjacent, factor V Leiden for thrombosis risk, MTHFR for prenatal. Surfaced when clinically relevant to the visit context.
- **Pharmacogenomic red flags.** The AI flags prescribing decisions that conflict with known variants.

Gnosis data is read-only from Klinos's perspective. Klinos never writes to Gnosis. Physician interpretations of genomic findings are stored as clinical observations on the visit record, not as modifications to the patient's genome data.

### Patient-logged subjective data

Not all biology comes from sensors. Some comes from the patient: symptoms, subjective experience, logged bleeding patterns, pain scores, self-reported medication side effects. This data lives in the dyad's Logos (episodic memory, or pinned where appropriate) and is tagged `source: patient_self_report`.

The Practice view treats patient-logged data with clinical weight but clear provenance. The physician sees *"patient reports 3 hot flashes per day since 4/1, self-logged"* alongside Bios-derived signals. Both matter; their sources are distinct.

Patient-logged data is crucial for OBGYN especially — cycle-day symptoms, cramps, cervical mucus, mood — because not everything shows up in biosensor data. The patient is also a sensor, and her self-report is first-class data.

### Aggregation, freshness, caching

**Time windows.** Klinos requests biological data in clinically-meaningful windows: current (last 24h), recent (last 7 days), trend (last 30 days), long-term (last 90+ days). Stroma handles the aggregation; Klinos specifies the window.

**Freshness.** For the active visit, Stroma returns the freshest available interpretation — typically within 60 seconds of the most recent biosensor update. For historical trends, Stroma uses cached aggregations that refresh daily.

**Caching.** Clinical views are computed on demand and cached in Stroma for the duration of the visit session. The cache invalidates when new biosensor data arrives that would materially change the interpretation.

### Privacy and data residency

**Nothing leaves the dyad.** The patient's Bios data is stored encrypted in her dyad (libSQL, AES-256 at rest). Klinos, operating under a scoped grant, reads interpreted views through Stroma — the actual encrypted data remains in the dyad. If a physician is on a different device than the patient's dyad runtime, the request goes to the patient's dyad, is served from there, and the interpretation is returned. No raw biosensor data ever transits to Klinos's storage, because **Klinos does not store Bios data at all.**

**Klinos stores only clinical artifacts.** Visit notes, prescriptions, lab orders, referrals, physician annotations, clinical interpretations. These are clinical documents, not biological telemetry. They live in the dyad's Logos (pinned and episodic memory) and in the practice's clinical record store with appropriate access controls.

**Population-level analytics: explicitly not in v1.** Klinos v1 does not compute cross-dyad population analytics. The *nothing leaves the dyad* principle is an architectural commitment we hold deliberately. Legitimate future use cases exist — clinical governance at scale, AI interpretation model improvement, research under explicit patient consent, health equity measurement — and each will get its own spec at the appropriate phase, with consent models designed against the specific value extracted. Not building the capability now is the cleanest way to hold the line against the institutional pressure that would otherwise widen the crack over time.

### Schema versioning

The Bios ↔ Klinos signal contract will evolve. Semver applies:

- **Minor bumps** (additive): new signals, new aggregations, backward-compatible. Klinos handles them at runtime.
- **Major bumps** (breaking): signal interpretation changes, signal retirements. Requires coordinated release of Stroma + Klinos + Bios.

The contract version is included in every `stroma.get_clinical_view()` call. Klinos maintains compatibility for one major version back for at least 6 months.

### What this section does not cover

The Bios implementation itself (how biosensor data arrives and is processed) is covered in the Bios SPEC, specifically §3 Data Ingestion Path and §5 Longitudinal Store. The consent and scope mechanics by which the physician gets to read any of this data are in §4. The UI for how biological data surfaces in the Practice view is in §10. The patient-facing surface for her own biology is in §11 and in the Bios SPEC.

## §8 Business Model

### What this section defines

This section defines the *structure* of Klinos's business model — who pays whom, for what, through which mechanism, under what terms. It establishes the revenue streams, the flow of money across patient/practice/physician/platform, the launch cohort's terms, and what Klinos explicitly will not monetize in v1. Specific dollar amounts for tiers, add-ons, and forecasted unit economics live in §14. §8 is the shape; §14 is the numbers.

The model has to work without outside investment. Hypostas takes no VC. That is a constraint and a freedom — no growth-at-all-costs pressure, but also no runway to subsidize customer acquisition or to operate at a loss for years while scaling. Every revenue stream needs to make sense on its own.

### The core model

**Patients pay a monthly cash-pay membership for their relationship with a Klinos-enabled practice.** The membership gives them continuous AI-mediated care through their Anima, scheduled visits with a physician, messaging, prescriptions, orders, and the care they would normally get from a concierge or direct-primary-care practice — with substantially more clinical intelligence on top.

**Klinos-the-platform takes a percentage of membership revenue in exchange for the platform.** The practice keeps the rest. The practice pays the physicians (who may be the owners themselves, as in the founding practice). The platform's percentage funds AI inference, infrastructure, compliance overhead, and the small team building and maintaining Klinos. The split is deliberately physician-friendly so the economics of leaving corporate medicine are obvious.

**Certain care is insurance-billed through a billing partner** — specifically labs, procedures, and imaging — because insurance-negotiated rates are genuinely better for patients in those cases, and the administrative overhead is bounded. Everything else stays cash-pay.

**HSA and FSA dollars pay for membership from day one.** This is the quiet unlock that makes Klinos cheaper than it looks for anyone with a high-deductible health plan.

That is the whole model. The rest of this section is detail.

### Revenue streams

Klinos has exactly these revenue streams in v1. Everything else is deferred.

1. **Patient membership** (cash-pay, HSA/FSA eligible) — the primary stream, recurring monthly
2. **Insurance-billed procedural revenue** — labs, imaging, in-office procedures billed through a billing partner
3. **Platform fee on membership** — Klinos-the-platform's cut of (1)
4. **Platform fee on procedural revenue** — a smaller cut of (2) because the admin overhead per dollar is higher

Not a revenue stream in v1:
- Population analytics (deferred per §7)
- Hospital / health system sales
- Pharma partnerships
- Training and consulting
- API / SDK access for third parties
- Insurance plan partnerships (Phase 3 per §2 roadmap)

### Patient membership structure

Membership is **tiered**, because different patients want different levels of care and the platform needs to accommodate a range of willingness-to-pay. Three tiers at launch, each self-contained (no surprise fees on top).

**Basic tier.** Continuous Anima, unlimited async messaging (Anima-triaged), preventive care (annual exam), acute care visits as needed, a limited number of telehealth visits per year. HSA/FSA eligible. This is the entry tier — the price point where a healthy person with a high-deductible plan can afford membership in addition to catastrophic insurance.

**Standard tier.** Everything in Basic plus unlimited telehealth, unlimited async messaging billed as visits when they exceed the complexity threshold (per §5), basic labs included at the practice's cost, free in-practice lab draws. This is where most patients land.

**Comprehensive tier.** Everything in Standard plus unlimited in-person visits, same-day access, annual comprehensive labs, specialty consults included in membership. For patients who want maximum access and for patients with complex chronic conditions.

**Specialty add-ons.** Specific specialties can add a monthly fee on top of any tier for specialty-specific care that would otherwise require a separate specialist. The OBGYN women's-health add-on is the launch example — cycle tracking, fertility support, perimenopause care, contraception management, well-woman exam, bundled with OBGYN access.

**Family plans.** Membership scales to additional family members at a reduced per-person cost. Two adults + dependents on a single plan pays meaningfully less than two separate memberships.

Specific dollar amounts for each tier and add-on are in §14. Structurally: Basic is priced to be HSA/FSA-friendly, Comprehensive is priced as a premium concierge alternative, Standard is priced to be the default for most patients.

### Bundling with Anima

The patient's Anima is a Hypostas product separate from Klinos. Baseline Anima pricing (per the Anima product spec) is $99/mo. Every Klinos member is also an Anima user by definition — the Anima IS the interface to Klinos — which creates a bundling question.

Three options were considered:

- **Separate subscriptions** — the patient pays $99/mo for Anima AND a Klinos membership on top. Cleanest accounting, worst UX, and double-charges the patient for two things she experiences as one.
- **Fully bundled** — Klinos membership includes Anima at no additional cost. Cleanest UX, harder accounting between the two products, and essentially gives Anima away to Klinos members (which cannibalizes standalone Anima revenue).
- **Credit-based bundling (chosen)** — Klinos membership includes a $99 Anima credit that offsets the Anima subscription. A patient paying for Klinos does not pay separately for Anima; behind the scenes, Klinos revenue funds the Anima subscription. Anima's revenue reporting is unchanged; the patient sees one line item.

The credit-based bundle preserves both products' revenue integrity while presenting a single price to the patient. It also means an Anima-only user who later joins a Klinos practice gets an automatic credit applied — no double-billing during the transition.

### HSA/FSA mechanics

**Klinos membership qualifies as eligible medical expense under the IRS Publication 502 definition** because it includes direct access to licensed medical care (physicians, prescriptions, orders). This is the same ground DPC practices stand on, and it has been tested in practice for more than a decade. Klinos membership CAN be paid with HSA or FSA funds.

The mechanics:

1. Patient sets up her HSA or FSA debit card in the Care view's billing settings.
2. Monthly membership charges go to the HSA/FSA card.
3. Klinos issues receipts formatted to satisfy HSA/FSA substantiation requirements (itemized as medical services, coded appropriately).
4. For FSA patients, the end-of-year "use it or lose it" dynamic is handled by offering annual pre-pay at a small discount — she can spend her FSA balance on a year of membership in December.

**The quiet unlock.** Patients with high-deductible HSA plans (the plan most tech workers and affluent self-employed people carry) can pay the entire Klinos membership with pre-tax dollars. For someone in the 32% marginal bracket, $400/mo membership costs about $272/mo after tax. This effectively makes Klinos 25-40% cheaper than its sticker price for patients who structure it correctly. Most existing concierge practices do not do this cleanly; we do from day one.

### Insurance-billed care

Klinos is cash-pay first. Certain care is insurance-billed because insurance is genuinely better for the patient in those cases:

**Labs.** Insurance-negotiated rates for blood work are often substantially cheaper than cash-pay, sometimes by a factor of five or more. A patient on a membership can have her labs billed to her insurance (if she has any) while her visits remain cash-pay. The billing partner handles claims, denials, and appeals.

**Procedures.** IUDs, biopsies, colposcopies, in-office surgeries, vaccinations. These are expensive enough ($200–2,000 per procedure) that cash-pay creates a real barrier for many patients. Insurance-billed via the billing partner.

**Imaging.** Mammograms, ultrasounds, MRIs, X-rays. Same logic as procedures.

**Billing partner integration.** Candid Health is the reference integration (other partners are possible; the integration is abstracted). Claims are scrubbed, submitted, tracked through adjudication, and paid back to the practice minus the partner's fee. Klinos does not run a claims operation internally.

**Never insurance-billed.** Routine primary-care and OBGYN visits, messaging, continuous Anima, membership services. These stay cash-pay forever. The day Klinos starts billing insurance for a routine office visit is the day we become Epic.

### Physician economics

Klinos does not aim to match the top-line compensation of a fully-employed hospital-system PCP ($220k–$280k for PCPs, higher for specialists). Klinos aims to make the *lifestyle trade worth it*.

The trade:

- Lower cash compensation than a high-volume employed physician
- Dramatically lower patient panel (75–150 vs 2,000+)
- Dramatically lower administrative burden (AI handles most of it)
- No insurance fighting for routine care
- Real patient relationships that compound over time
- Flexible schedule, working hours of your choice
- Equity in your own practice
- The practice of medicine rather than the practice of charting

For a PCP leaving corporate medicine at $250k/yr working 50–55 hrs/week with heavy burnout, a Klinos practice at $180–220k working 30–40 hrs/week with minimal admin is a better deal even at lower cash. For an OBGYN, procedural revenue on top of membership revenue pushes total compensation higher.

Specific compensation math at different panel sizes and tier mixes lives in §14. The *shape* of it is: physician compensation scales with patient panel × average tier × (85% of membership − practice overhead).

### Practice economics

A practice is a business entity that owns patient relationships, employs physicians (or is owned by them), pays overhead, and keeps whatever is left.

**Revenue sources:**
- Membership revenue from its patient panel, net of platform fee
- Procedural insurance revenue, net of billing-partner and platform fees

**Cost sources:**
- Physical overhead if any (rent, equipment, supplies) — minimal for telehealth-only practices
- Malpractice insurance per physician
- Small administrative staff (optional — Klinos AI handles most of it, but some practices still want a human office manager)
- Physician compensation
- Continuing medical education and license renewal
- Klinos does *not* charge a separate per-seat fee in addition to the percentage — the platform share is the only thing the practice pays Klinos

**Breakeven dynamics.** A practice becomes profitable above its physician compensation threshold once its panel is large enough to cover overhead at the given tier mix. Specific breakeven patient counts for the founding practice and for typical practices are modeled in §14.

### Platform economics

Klinos-the-platform is the Hypostas-owned infrastructure that the practice runs on. Its revenue is the platform fee across all practices; its costs are the ones that don't scale linearly with practice count.

**Platform costs:**
- AI inference (the biggest variable cost — per-member, per-month)
- Infrastructure (libSQL, hosting, CDN, signal-cli for messaging)
- Compliance audit and legal (annual SOC 2 Type II audit, HIPAA compliance maintenance, state license tracking)
- Clinical governance (the panel that reviews flagged physician behavior, emergency access challenges, scope-of-practice disputes)
- A small engineering team maintaining and extending klinos-core and the Practice view in the Anima app
- Billing-partner integration fees (pass-through but requires integration maintenance)

**Platform margin.** Contribution margin per member-month is the platform fee minus the variable costs (mostly AI inference). At reasonable scale the margin is positive; at launch with a single practice, Klinos-the-platform subsidizes operations against future scale. Specific margin math is in §14.

### Launch cohort terms

Klinos-the-platform has one founder (Josh). The first practices to run on Klinos — including the founding practice (Josh's wife's PCP side and her OBGYN partner's practice) — are **early-adopter customers**, not co-founders. Customers get customer terms, even when the customer is a family member of the founder. The related-party line is bright, and we respect it.

The early-adopter cohort is defined by a time window, not a count: **any practice that joins Klinos within the first 24 months of platform launch is in the cohort.** The cohort receives preferential terms:

- **50% discount on platform fees for 24 months from their individual start date.** Every cohort practice gets 24 months at the reduced rate, regardless of whether they joined on day 1 or day 500 of the window.
- **Standard platform fees thereafter.** No lifetime discount, no awkward transition milestone — the 24-month clock starts when a practice joins and ends 24 months later, period.
- **Priority feedback channel.** Cohort practices get direct contact with the Klinos product team (at v1 scale, Josh) for feedback, feature requests, and pain points. This is real but untranslated into pricing discount.
- **Input on clinical governance formation.** As Klinos scales and needs a formal clinical governance body (per §6), cohort practices nominate the initial governance panel.

**The founding practice's terms are identical to any other early-adopter cohort practice's terms.** This keeps the related-party line clean and prevents "the founders' rate" from becoming a growing list of exceptions as the platform scales.

### Why the cohort gets a discount at all

The discount is not charity; it is risk-sharing. The first practices on a brand-new platform tolerate:

- Features that don't exist yet and workarounds when they don't
- Bugs and rough edges no production-scale platform would ship with
- Compliance processes that are still being documented
- Clinical governance that is still being formed

In exchange for carrying that risk, they pay less for the platform during the period when the risk is highest. By month 25, most of those risks are gone; the standard rate starts. The discount compensates for platform immaturity, and it ends when the immaturity ends.

### What Klinos does not monetize in v1

Explicit deferrals so the boundary is legible:

- **Population analytics.** Deferred per §7. No cross-dyad revenue.
- **Third-party API / SDK.** Klinos is a closed product in v1. We do not sell access to klinos-core as an SDK, we do not let third-party products integrate with Klinos data.
- **Pharma partnerships.** Real-world evidence studies and pharma-sponsored clinical programs are ethically fraught and not v1-aligned.
- **Training and consulting.** Helping other practices get set up is something we do for free (or very cheaply) in v1 to build network effects; we do not build a consulting business around it.
- **Hospital / health system sales.** Klinos is for independent practices. Enterprise sales is a different business model and a different product; we do not pursue it in v1.
- **Sponsored content or advertising.** Never. Obvious.

## §9 Compliance Arc

### What this section defines

This section defines the full regulatory compliance surface Klinos must satisfy and the arc by which we satisfy it. It covers HIPAA, SureScripts EPCS for e-prescribing, SOC 2 Type II security certification, state medical licensure and the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact, the corporate practice of medicine doctrine, DEA registration, CLIA for in-office labs, state-specific rules (recording consent, reproductive privacy, mental health), Business Associate Agreements, breach response, medical record retention, and the clinical governance structure.

The §4 claim was that "HIPAA compliance is a property of the architecture, not a layer bolted on top." §9 is where that claim meets the real world — specific certifications, specific filings, specific timelines, specific money. The architecture gives us a substantial head start. It does not give us a pass.

The operating principle throughout this section is that **Klinos is infrastructure; practices are businesses.** Klinos provides technical controls, tooling, and templates. Practices execute compliance. This is not handwave — it has a legal basis: Klinos is a HIPAA Business Associate, the practice is a HIPAA Covered Entity, and the obligations split cleanly along that line.

### Covered Entity vs Business Associate

Under HIPAA there are two roles in this system and they have different obligations. **Klinos-the-platform is a Business Associate. Each Klinos-enabled practice is a Covered Entity.** The practice is the one providing healthcare and treating patients. Klinos is the one hosting the infrastructure that makes the practice's work possible. This split determines who carries which compliance burden:

| Obligation | Covered Entity (Practice) | Business Associate (Klinos) |
|---|---|---|
| Notice of Privacy Practices to patients | Owns | Provides template |
| Patient rights administration (access, amendment, accounting of disclosures, restrictions) | Owns | Provides tooling (Care view, audit export, dyad access) |
| Breach notification to affected individuals | Owns | Notifies practice; provides notification templates |
| Breach notification to HHS OCR | Owns | Provides notification helper |
| Consent management | Owns | Provides scope system and consent flows (§4) |
| State medical board compliance | Owns | Not applicable |
| CLIA certification (if practice draws labs) | Owns | Not applicable |
| Malpractice insurance | Owns | Not applicable |
| State-specific telehealth compliance | Owns | Enforces technical controls at credential verification |
| HIPAA-compliant technical and administrative safeguards for hosted PHI | N/A (delegated to BA) | Owns |
| Encryption, access controls, audit trail, authentication | N/A (delegated to BA) | Owns |
| BAAs with sub-business-associates (AI inference, infrastructure, billing partner) | N/A (Klinos handles) | Owns |
| SOC 2 certification (when pursued) | Not applicable | Owns |
| Platform-level security incident response | Not applicable | Owns |
| Tooling to help practices execute their CE obligations | N/A (consumes) | Owns |

**Klinos provides infrastructure and tooling. The practice executes compliance.** Where Klinos ships a tool that helps a practice satisfy an obligation — an NPP template, a breach notification helper, an audit-log exporter — the obligation remains the practice's. Klinos is not outsourcing anything; it is removing the friction of the practice executing what the practice already owed.

### The regulatory landscape

Klinos sits at the intersection of several distinct regulatory regimes. The "owner" column names who carries the primary responsibility — practice or platform — under the CE/BA split established above.

| Regime | Authority | What it governs | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| HIPAA Privacy Rule | HHS OCR | Use and disclosure of PHI | Practice (CE) with Klinos tooling |
| HIPAA Security Rule | HHS OCR | Technical and administrative safeguards for ePHI | Klinos (BA) for hosted infrastructure |
| HIPAA Breach Notification | HHS OCR | Disclosure obligations when PHI is compromised | Practice to patients; Klinos to practice |

| State reproductive privacy laws | State-specific | Cycle and reproductive data handling | Practice with Klinos scope enforcement |

None of these are optional. The architecture satisfies a substantial portion of the technical requirements of HIPAA's Security Rule; everything else requires operational, legal, and procedural work by the party that owns it.

### HIPAA — Privacy, Security, Breach Notification

**Klinos's HIPAA obligations are Business Associate obligations**, satisfied by a combination of the protocol architecture (§§3–7) and platform-side operational policy.

- **Security Rule technical safeguards** — encryption at rest (libSQL AES-256), encryption in transit (TLS + DyadPacket), access controls (DyadID + scoped grants, §4), audit controls (`log_existence`, every read and write), integrity controls (DyadPacket signing), authentication (DyadID signing + PhysicianCredential verification). All architectural, already built.
- **Security Rule administrative safeguards** — designated Security Officer (Josh at launch), workforce access policies, periodic risk analysis, incident response plan. Policy work, not engineering.
- **Security Rule physical safeguards** — Hypostas does not operate physical servers; BAAs with infrastructure hosts cover physical security of hosted data.
- **BAA compliance** — Klinos signs a BAA with every Klinos-enabled practice (Klinos as BA) and with every sub-business-associate it uses (AI inference, infrastructure, billing partner).
- **Breach notification to the practice** — if Klinos discovers a breach of PHI it hosts, it notifies the affected practice within 60 days. The practice then handles notification to its patients and to HHS OCR.

**The practice's HIPAA obligations are Covered Entity obligations**, most of which are paper-and-process work the practice does with Klinos-provided templates:

- **Notice of Privacy Practices.** Klinos provides a template NPP (reviewed by healthcare counsel); the practice adapts it to its own name, address, and any practice-specific details; the practice issues it to every patient at enrollment and posts it in the Care view.
- **Patient right of access (§164.524).** Automatic — the patient's dyad IS her medical record, continuously accessible via the Care view and the Anima.
- **Right to amend (§164.526).** Automatic — she annotates clinical memory in the Care view; the annotation is versioned and flows to the practice for review per the practice's amendment policy.
- **Accounting of disclosures (§164.528).** Automatic — `log_existence` provides the log; Klinos exports it on demand; the practice produces the accounting to the patient when requested.
- **Right to request restrictions.** The patient narrows scopes directly in the Care view; the practice is notified and can accept or deny per its policy (practice-defined).
- **Breach notification to patients and HHS.** The practice's obligation when a breach occurs — whether the breach originated in Klinos's hosted infrastructure or in the practice's own operations. Klinos provides breach notification templates, a notification helper that generates the required letters, and the forensic evidence (`log_existence` export) the practice needs to scope the breach.

### SureScripts EPCS

Electronic prescribing for controlled substances is DEA-regulated under the EPCS rule. Compliance is shared between platform and practice:

**Platform-side (Klinos):**
- **SureScripts platform certification** — one-time, renewed annually. Audit of our e-prescribing pipeline against SureScripts' technical standards. 4–8 weeks once application is submitted.
- **Two-factor authentication enforcement at prescribing time** — something she has (a hardware token, or a cryptographic key stored on her authenticated device) + something she knows/is (password, biometric). Enforced in `klinos-core::compliance::surescripts`.
- **State Prescription Drug Monitoring Program (PDMP) integration** — queries the state PDMP before prescribing controlled substances; results surface in the Practice view. State-by-state integration, typically 2–4 weeks per state.

**Practice-side (per physician):**
- **DEA registration** — physician-specific, tied to practice address. Physicians transferring from employed positions often need re-registration at the new practice location.
- **EPCS credentialing** — each physician prescribing controlled substances completes identity proofing through an approved credential service provider (IdenTrust, Exostar, or equivalent). 1–3 weeks per physician, done once.
- **Audit logging of controlled-substance prescriptions** — every Schedule II–V prescription logs to a tamper-evident record with 2-year retention minimum. Klinos provides the logging; the practice owns the record under its CE obligations.

### SOC 2 — deferred until business case

SOC 2 is not legally required. HIPAA is. SOC 2 is a commercial certification that some enterprise customers demand, and at launch Klinos does not have those customers. The founding practice does not ask for a SOC 2 report. Early-adopter cohort practices are small DPC-style operations; most will not ask either. Partners like Candid Health and Labcorp sign BAAs based on HIPAA compliance, not SOC 2.

**The revised approach:**

- **Do the internal prep work.** Document controls, write policies, define incident response, run access reviews. This is free (just time) and makes our HIPAA baseline materially stronger. Good operational hygiene regardless of whether we certify.
- **Defer the paid audit.** Type I ($25–40k) and Type II ($30–60k + observation time) wait until either (a) revenue justifies the spend, or (b) a specific enterprise customer or partner demands it as a condition of doing business.
- **Realistic timeline:** Type I in Year 2 or later if and when demand warrants. Type II whenever the business case shows up.

This drops launch compliance spend from ~$50–80k to ~$10–25k (HIPAA baseline + legal review + BAA templates + policy library), which is reasonable at zero revenue.

**Trust Services Criteria when pursued.** Klinos will commit to Security (required), Availability, Confidentiality, Privacy. Processing Integrity stays optional until a customer demands it.

### State medical licensure and the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact

Physicians must be licensed in every state where they treat patients. Telehealth does not change this — a California physician treating a Texas patient over video needs a Texas license. **This obligation is the physician's and the practice's, not Klinos's.** Klinos enforces the technical control at credential verification; it does not acquire or maintain state licenses.

**Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC).** 40+ member states have agreed to a streamlined multi-state licensure process for qualifying physicians. A physician licensed in her state of principal license (SPL) can obtain expedited licensure in any other compact state with significantly less paperwork than full state-by-state application. Florida IS an IMLC member.

**Non-compact states.** California, New York, and a handful of others are not IMLC members. Physicians wanting to practice in those states need to apply directly — 3–6 months per state.

**Verification at access-request time.** `klinos-core::access::grant::verify_request()` checks that the physician holds active licensure in the patient's state of residence (or that the IMLC compact bridges them). A request that fails licensure is rejected with a clear error and logged.

**Expansion strategy.** For v1 (founding practice in Florida treating Florida patients): only Florida licensure matters. As cohort practices in other IMLC states join, physicians use the compact's expedited process. Non-compact states are handled per-case as physicians need them.

### Corporate practice of medicine (CPOM)

The CPOM doctrine is a state-specific rule that, in strict-CPOM states, prohibits non-physicians from owning medical practices. It exists to prevent corporate influence on medical decisions.

**Florida is not a strict CPOM state.** It allows non-physician ownership of medical practices under certain conditions. This matters for the founding practice — the wife's and friend's practice can be structured with Klinos-the-platform as a service provider, not an owner.

**Strict CPOM states** (California, Texas, New York, Illinois, and others) require a **PC-MSO structure**:

- **Professional Corporation (PC)** — owned by physicians, provides medical services, bills patients, employs or contracts with physicians
- **Management Services Organization (MSO)** — owned by non-physicians (Klinos), provides non-clinical services to the PC via a long-term services agreement (IT, billing, admin, facilities, marketing). The MSO never makes medical decisions.

The services agreement between PC and MSO is the critical legal artifact — it has to pay the MSO a fair-market-value fee, cannot give the MSO control over clinical decisions, and has to survive scrutiny under each state's specific CPOM standards.

**Florida's simpler structure** — because Florida does not strictly enforce CPOM, the founding practice may not need the full PC-MSO separation. A simpler PC owned by the physicians, contracting with Klinos for services, should suffice. §12 Legal & Entity Structure finalizes the specific structure with the healthcare attorney.

### DEA registration, CLIA, and state-specific rules

**DEA registration.** Each physician prescribing controlled substances needs a DEA number tied to her practice location. Physicians leaving hospital employment often need to re-register at the new practice address (a practice-location-change filing with DEA). The practice itself may also need a DEA registration depending on whether it dispenses or stores controlled substances on-site. For a cash-pay, mostly-telehealth practice, DEA complexity is typically minimal. **Practice-owned obligation.**

**CLIA certification.** The Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments regulate in-office lab testing. Waived-complexity tests (urinalysis dipstick, pregnancy tests, finger-stick glucose, rapid strep) can be performed under a CLIA Certificate of Waiver, which is inexpensive and fast. Moderate or high-complexity tests require more involved certification. Most in-office labs a Klinos PCP or OBGYN would run are waived-complexity. Blood draws for send-out labs (Labcorp/Quest) do not require CLIA for the practice — the external lab is CLIA-certified. **Practice-owned obligation.**

**State-specific rules.** A partial inventory and who owns each:

- **Two-party consent recording** (FL, CA, WA, MA, MD, PA, and others) — consent obtained at visit start (per §5). Klinos provides the consent flow; practice obtains and documents the consent.
- **42 CFR Part 2** — federal substance use disorder record privacy, stricter than HIPAA. Klinos scope enforcement (the `mental_health` bundle requires explicit consent); practice owns documentation and procedural compliance.
- **State mental health privacy** — many states have additional mental health record protections. Implemented through scope enforcement; practice owns the procedural side.
- **Reproductive health post-Dobbs** — several states have laws affecting cycle and reproductive data disclosure. Klinos stores reproductive data in the dyad (patient-owned, encrypted) and surfaces it only under explicit consent; cross-state data movement is avoided where possible. Practice owns any state-specific procedural compliance.
- **Minor treatment rules** — age of consent for medical treatment varies by state and by treatment type. Practice owns the clinical judgment; Klinos enforces the technical control on credential/scope verification.

### Business Associate Agreements (BAAs)

BAAs operate at two layers.

**Klinos-level BAAs.** Klinos signs BAAs with every sub-business-associate it relies on to provide the platform:

- **AI inference providers** (Anthropic, OpenAI) — both offer HIPAA-eligible enterprise plans. Signed BAAs at the platform level. No PHI sent to inference endpoints without a BAA in place.
- **Infrastructure hosting** (libSQL host) — signed BAA. Encryption-at-rest plus our AES-256 layer means the host never sees plaintext PHI even with a BAA, but the BAA is still required.
- **Signal adapter / messaging channels** — Signal itself is end-to-end encrypted and does not retain message content, but BAA covers metadata.
- **Billing partner** (Candid Health) — their core business is healthcare, standard BAA signed.
- **Labcorp, Quest, imaging partners** — all have standard BAAs.
- **SureScripts** — BAA executed as part of platform certification.
- **Any future analytics, telemetry, or monitoring tools** — BAA required before integration.

Tracked in `klinos-core::compliance::hipaa::baa_registry`. Integration of any new vendor is gated on an active BAA being in registry.

**Practice-level BAAs.** The practice signs a BAA with Klinos (Klinos as BA to the practice). The practice also signs BAAs directly with any of its own direct vendors that are not part of Klinos's stack — physical office landlord if applicable, local admin staff, any third-party practice tools. Klinos provides a BAA template for the practice to use with its own vendors; the practice executes them.

### Breach response

Klinos has a documented breach response plan for breaches of PHI hosted in its infrastructure. The plan runs four stages:

1. **Detection and containment.** Any anomaly in `log_existence`, any report from a user, any alert from monitoring, any external disclosure triggers the plan. Access is locked or scoped down immediately where indicated. The incident is assigned a unique ID and tracked through resolution.
2. **Assessment.** Within 60 days, determine whether the incident constitutes a breach under HITECH. The four-factor analysis: nature and extent of PHI involved, unauthorized person who received the PHI, whether the PHI was actually acquired or viewed, extent to which risk has been mitigated. Conclusion documented.
3. **Notification to the practice (Klinos's BA obligation).** If breach confirmed, Klinos notifies the affected practice within 60 days of discovery. The practice, as covered entity, then handles notification to its patients and to HHS OCR — with Klinos-provided notification templates, a notification helper that generates the required letters, and the forensic evidence (`log_existence` export) the practice needs to scope the breach.
4. **Corrective action.** Root cause analysis, corrective action plan, policy updates, retraining if applicable. Documented and retained for OCR audit.

The practice maintains its own breach response plan covering breaches originating in its own operations (a physician's lost laptop, an unauthorized staff access, a patient intake form left in public). Klinos provides the breach-response-plan template; the practice adapts and owns it.

`log_existence` is the primary forensic source in any breach investigation. Because every read and write is logged with grant ID, physician ID, and timestamp, the scope of a breach can be precisely bounded.

### Data retention and destruction

**Medical record retention** is state-specific, typically 5–10 years after the last patient encounter for adults, longer for pediatric records. The practice, as covered entity, owns the retention obligation. Klinos, as business associate, retains hosted data per the practice's retention policy — typically aligned with the most conservative applicable state rule: 10 years for adult records, age-of-majority + 10 years for pediatric.

**The dyad's copy persists indefinitely under patient control.** Because the patient's dyad is her medical record (per HIPAA right of access), she retains her own copy for as long as her dyad exists — indefinite. This is stronger than traditional record retention, not weaker.

**Destruction.** When practice-side records reach their retention endpoint, they are destroyed via cryptographic erasure (deletion of the encryption keys rendering the data unrecoverable). Destruction is logged; patients can request earlier destruction under HIPAA (with limits — retention required by law persists regardless).

**Practice closure.** If a practice closes, records are transferred to a successor practice if one exists, returned to the patient's dyad (which she already has anyway), and retained per state law. Patients are notified and given opportunity to request transfer.

### Clinical governance — minimal, scale-triggered

Clinical governance is minimal at launch and scale-triggered rather than time-triggered. **Most governance work is the practice's own** — practice-internal performance reviews, individual patient complaints about specific physicians, adverse event reviews (via the practice's malpractice carrier), scope-of-practice approval for individual physicians the practice credentials.

**Klinos retains only the governance that is inherently platform-level:**

- **Cross-practice emergency access disputes.** If an ER physician from Practice B invokes break-glass on a Practice A patient and the patient challenges, no single practice can adjudicate. This is platform-level by definition.
- **`PhysicianCredential` platform-level revocation.** When a physician's credential is revoked at the platform level, she can no longer practice on Klinos at any practice. This prevents a bad-actor physician from moving between practices; it requires platform-level authority.
- **Architectural integrity.** Protocol-level issues, platform bugs that affect PHI handling, security incidents in the infrastructure layer.
- **Cross-practice pattern detection.** If five practices independently have physicians over-attesting scope, no single practice sees the pattern. The platform does.

**At launch: Josh is governance.** No formal panel. No bylaws. Clinical advisors on retainer for specialty-specific questions when they arise (budget: a few hundred dollars per consultation, rare). Decisions are documented but informal. At 2–3 practices, real governance volume is near-zero; when something arises, Josh and an advisor handle it case-by-case and document the resolution.

**Scale trigger, not time trigger.** At roughly 10 practices on the platform — enough practices across multiple states and specialties that the informal "Josh handles it" model breaks — formalize into a Clinical Governance Panel with cohort-nominated members and documented process. If we have 10 practices in Year 1, we formalize in Year 1. If we have 3 practices in Year 3, we stay informal until Year 4.

**Everything else that looks like "governance" is the practice's own work** — physician performance, individual clinical decisions, complaint handling, adverse event review, most scope-of-practice attestation review. Klinos provides the tools (attestation logs, pattern reports, incident reporting templates); the practice runs the process.

## §10 Practice View — Physician UI

### What this section defines

This section defines the physician-facing UI surface inside the Anima app — what we call the **Practice view**, per §3. It covers the design principles, the navigation structure, and the purpose and content of each major screen. It does not specify pixel layouts or interaction microcopy; those live in design files and implementation. §10 is the product-level description: what each surface achieves, what data it shows, and why it exists in the shape it does.

The Practice view is where physicians spend most of their Klinos time. It competes with their attention to the patient in front of them. Every design choice has to serve that reality.

### Design principles

1. **Glanceable over interactive.** The physician's attention is on the patient, not the screen. Information must surface without demanding clicks. Where a click is unavoidable, it is a single click, not a four-step flow.
2. **Decision support, not decisions.** The AI suggests; the physician decides. AI-drafted content is visually distinct from physician-authored content. The physician can accept, edit, or reject any draft. Nothing is auto-signed.
3. **Keyboard-first.** Physicians type fast and menus are slow. Every frequent action has a keyboard shortcut. The visit surface, the note editor, the message inbox, and the search bar all work without ever touching a mouse.
4. **Low chrome, high density.** Physicians need to see a lot of information at once. Navigation, headers, and padding are minimal. The screen shows clinical content; the chrome gets out of the way.
5. **Status-driven color.** Red, yellow, and green are reserved for clinical severity. No color used as decoration. A red indicator means *attend to this*; a yellow indicator means *review when you can*; a green indicator means *all clear*. Color is never the sole signal (shapes and labels accompany every color), for accessibility.
6. **Desktop-primary, mobile-capable.** The visit surface is designed for desktop-class screens (laptop, desktop, or tablet with external keyboard). The messages inbox, schedule, and Today queue work on phones for checking between visits. In-visit work on phone-sized screens is supported but not optimized — the visit surface assumes screen space.
7. **Latency is a clinical failure mode.** A drafted note that takes 30 seconds to appear after visit end makes the physician feel like she is working *for* the tool rather than the tool working for her. Budget: SOAP note draft appears within 10 seconds of visit end for most visits, 30 seconds worst case.

### Navigation structure

The Practice view is a sub-app of the Anima app (per §3). Its internal navigation is a single left-hand rail with seven destinations:

- **Today** — the day-in-progress view. Schedule, items needing attention, current visit if in progress.
- **Patients** — the full panel, searchable and filterable. Click into any patient for her full chart.
- **Messages** — the escalated-only inbox.
- **Schedule** — calendar and availability management.
- **Labs** — pending, in-progress, and resulted.
- **Prescriptions** — drafts, active prescriptions, refill queue.
- **Settings** — practice-level and physician-level configuration.

The physician can move between these with keyboard shortcuts (`G T` for Today, `G P` for Patients, etc. — Gmail-style). The navigation rail collapses to icons when the working area needs the screen.

### Today — the landing view

`Today` is where the physician starts her day and where she returns between visits. It is organized by **attention priority**, not by time or patient.

**Sections, top to bottom:**

1. **Urgent.** Anything flagged as requiring immediate attention — an abnormal lab result outside decision boundaries, an escalated emergency message, a grant revocation mid-visit. Usually empty. Red indicators.
2. **To sign.** Drafted notes, prescriptions, lab orders, referrals awaiting physician signature. Typically fills up during clinic as visits end; the physician batch-signs at end of clinic or signs per-visit as she prefers.
3. **Today's schedule.** Chronological list of visits on the calendar, with current-visit or next-up highlighted. Each visit shows: patient name, visit type, visit reason, prep state (intake brief ready?), any preliminary flags from the intake.
4. **Asynchronous items.** Messages escalated from patients since the last Today refresh, recent lab results needing review, refill requests awaiting approval. Grouped by type, each with patient context.
5. **Overnight summary (first load of the day only).** Anything that happened between yesterday's sign-off and now — messages the Anima triaged autonomously, results that came back overnight, any emergency access events on her patients. Serves as a morning briefing; dismissed after she reads it.

The Today view is where a physician spends the first 2 minutes of her day and the 30 seconds between visits. It is designed to let her catch up fast and make her next decision — which patient, which message, which note — without searching.

### The in-visit surface

This is the most specific screen in the Practice view and the one where the UI has to perform hardest. The physician is in conversation with the patient; the screen has a split second to surface something useful or stay out of the way.

**Layout, during an active visit:**

- **Center.** For telehealth: the patient's video feed, prominent. For in-person: nothing in the center — the physician is looking at the patient, and the screen shows clinical content around the edges.
- **Left rail — intake brief (collapsible).** The brief the physician saw at T−5 (per §5). Patient's symptoms, biological context, prior visit summary, Anima's synthesis. Collapsed by default during the visit; expandable in one tap.
- **Right rail — AI co-pilot sidebar.** Persistent. Shows live transcript, suggested clarifying questions, flagged signals, drug-interaction warnings as medications come up, prior-visit references when a topic echoes back, a running potential-differential list updating as the conversation reveals more.
- **Bottom bar — quick actions.** End visit. Pause recording. Add physical exam finding (voice-dictated, transcribed into the objective section of the draft note). Order a lab. Draft a prescription. Flag for follow-up. Keyboard shortcuts for all of these.

**The AI co-pilot sidebar is persistent, not modal.** It updates in real time as the conversation progresses. Nothing interrupts the physician; everything is glanceable. If she wants to dismiss it temporarily, one keystroke collapses it to a thin edge.

**Tiered visual intensity for co-pilot surfacing.** The co-pilot operates on three tiers:

- **Ambient.** Prior visit references, contextual biology trends, general suggestions. Present in the sidebar but visually quiet.
- **Notable.** Relevant PGx variants coming up, HRV-pattern correlations, differential refinements. Visually prominent in the sidebar but non-interrupting.
- **Concerning.** Drug interactions that would cause harm, contraindications with active diagnoses, abnormal values crossing decision boundaries. Brief visual pulse in the sidebar — enough to catch the physician's eye without breaking the conversation. Never a modal. Never a sound.

The physician can expand any co-pilot item to see its reasoning (per §7: the AI exposes its sources). One keystroke dismisses the item if she disagrees. The dismissal is logged; patterns of dismissals train the model over time (per-physician feedback, not cross-physician).

**Physical exam documentation for in-person visits.** The physician speaks exam findings ("BP 124 over 78, heart regular rate and rhythm...") and the speech is transcribed into the Objective section of the drafting note, structured by body system. She reviews the structured output on sign-off. Keyboard alternative for physicians who prefer typing.

**Procedural workflows.** For procedural visits (IUD insertion, biopsy, etc.) the in-visit surface swaps into a procedure-specific layout that walks through pre-procedure consent, the procedure steps, post-procedure observation. Each step captures what it needs (consent acknowledgment, procedure completion time, specimen labeling, post-procedure vitals) and produces the appropriately-structured note.

### Patient detail view

When a physician opens a patient from the Patients list or from Today, she sees the patient's chart. This is the view she spends the most time in outside of active visits.

**Top of the screen — the clinical summary.** At a glance: name, demographics, active problem list, active medications, allergies, last visit date, most-recent critical biology signal (allostatic load, HRV trend, cycle phase if OBGYN scope is active). Designed to let a physician walk into a visit already knowing what she needs to know.

**Primary content — the timeline.** A chronological mix of visits, notes, labs, imaging, messages, milestones, and significant patient-logged events. Filterable by type. Clicking any entry opens the full detail (note, lab result, message thread, etc.). The timeline is the "continuous record" described in §1 made concrete — instead of a paper chart organized by form type, it is a living chronology organized by when things happened.

**Tabs for depth (secondary navigation inside the patient view):**

- **Biology** — the Stroma-mediated view (per §7). Current state, trends, annotations.
- **Notes** — all prior visit notes, searchable.
- **Labs** — result trends with AI annotations.
- **Prescriptions** — active meds, history, and any drafts for this patient.
- **Messages** — the conversation thread with this patient.
- **Grants** — active `AccessGrant`s and recent audit entries for this patient (forensic view).

**Quick actions.** Schedule a visit, open a message, order a lab, draft a prescription, revoke an active grant the physician no longer needs. Keyboard shortcuts for all.

### Messages inbox

The physician's message inbox shows only patient-originated messages that the Anima escalated (per §5). Messages that the Anima handled autonomously do not appear here — the physician sees them retrospectively in the patient's timeline if she looks, but they do not demand her attention in the moment.

Each escalated message arrives with pre-assembled context: the exchange leading up to the escalation, relevant problem-list entries, active medications, recent biology, why the Anima thought this needed physician attention. The physician can reply directly (her reply flows through the Anima to the patient), schedule a visit, order something, or dismiss the item as handled.

Grouping is by patient, sorted by urgency. Urgent escalations (symptom descriptions that could indicate something serious, medication side effects, suicidal ideation if relevant) float to the top with red indicators. Routine escalations (a question the Anima could almost handle but wanted a human to confirm) are quieter.

If the exchange exceeds the complexity threshold for an async message visit (per §5), the thread is marked as a billable async visit and generates a draft note when the physician signs off.

### Signing cadence — per physician preference

Physicians vary in how they prefer to handle drafted notes. Klinos supports both dominant patterns and lets the physician choose per her workflow:

- **Per-visit signing.** Draft note appears in the To Sign queue within 60 seconds of visit end; physician signs before starting the next visit. Closes the loop while context is fresh. Preferred by physicians who want to stay caught up throughout the day.
- **Batch signing at end of clinic.** Drafts queue up through the day; physician signs everything at once at end of clinic. Stays in conversational flow during clinic hours; consolidates cognitive switching.

Both work on the same queue. A physician can start in per-visit mode and switch to batch mid-day if she runs long, or the reverse. Klinos does not force either cadence — it surfaces the queue and the physician chooses when to clear it.

**Controlled-substance prescriptions are the one exception.** EPCS two-factor authentication is required at the moment of signing, and the physician will complete that specific step even if she batches everything else, because the EPCS hardware token requirement makes skipping it costly.

### Schedule, Labs, Prescriptions, Settings

Briefly, the remaining destinations:

**Schedule.** Week and day views of the physician's calendar. Drag-to-book. Recurring availability settings. Time-off management. One-way sync with her personal calendar (read-only — her personal dyad calendar appears as blocked time, but Klinos never writes to it).

**Labs.** Pending (ordered, awaiting result), in-progress (drawn, at lab), resulted-need-review (new results flagged by the AI with priority), resulted-reviewed (history). The review flow is: read the AI's interpretation, accept/edit/reject, annotate for future reference, optionally message the patient. Bulk review for routine normals.

**Prescriptions.** Drafts awaiting signature, active prescriptions across the panel, refill request queue, prescription history. Controlled-substance prescriptions live in their own sub-tab because of the EPCS two-factor requirement.

**Settings.** Practice-level: enabled specialty modules, consent flow preferences, billing model, practice branding, BAA registry (view-only). Physician-level: schedule defaults, notification preferences, keyboard shortcut customization, credential status (DEA, EPCS, state licenses, board certifications, attestations).

### Keyboard shortcuts and accessibility

**Keyboard shortcuts.** Gmail-style chord bindings for navigation (`G T`, `G P`, etc.), single-letter bindings within a view (`s` to sign, `r` to reply, `/` for search, `?` for help). Full shortcut reference accessible from any screen. Physicians can remap any binding; defaults are designed to be learnable in a week.

**Accessibility.** The Practice view meets WCAG 2.1 AA minimum: keyboard-only operation is fully supported, every interactive element has focus states and ARIA labels, color is never the sole indicator of status, text contrast meets AA ratios, content is readable at 200% zoom. Screen-reader compatibility is tested with VoiceOver and NVDA. Medicine has physicians with low vision and physicians who work from keyboard exclusively; the tool works for them.

### Device posture

Desktop-primary, mobile-capable. The in-visit surface assumes desktop or tablet-with-keyboard. The Today view, Messages, Schedule, Labs, and Prescriptions all work fully on a phone for checking between visits or while away from the office. Settings is desktop-primary but accessible on mobile.

A physician walking between exam rooms can check her Today queue on her phone, respond to an escalated message, sign a drafted note, and approve a refill request — all from the Anima app on her phone, which shows her Practice navigation when she has the `PhysicianCredential`. When she returns to her workstation for a visit, the in-visit surface lights up on the larger screen.

### What this section does not cover

Pixel-level layouts, component libraries, and interaction microcopy live in design files and implementation. Specific keyboard-shortcut bindings for every action are documented in the in-app help surface. The patient-facing Care view is §11. The underlying clinical workflow that the Practice view drives is §5. The biological data the Practice view surfaces is defined by §7.

## §11 Care View — Patient UI

### What this section defines

This section defines the patient-facing UI surface inside the Anima app — the **Care view**, per §3. It covers the design principles, navigation, and purpose of each major screen.

The Care view is not where most patient interaction with Klinos happens. That lives in the patient's continuous Chat with her Anima. The Care view is a lightweight surface for **discrete actions and records** — book a visit, review a lab result, manage her Emergency Access Policy, check billing, see who has accessed her dyad. Everything else is a conversation.

This is the single most important design choice for §11, and it propagates through the entire section: **the Anima is the interface; the Care view is the file cabinet and the control panel**.

### Design principles

1. **The Anima is the interface. The Care view is the control panel.** Continuous conversation, triage, and emotional support happen in Chat. Explicit actions (book, review, configure, revoke) happen in Care. Neither should feel like the other — the Care view is intentionally more formal and structured; Chat is intentionally warmer and relational.
2. **Mobile-first.** Patients open the Care view on a phone in a waiting room, a parking lot, or a couch. Desktop works but is not the design target.
3. **Patient-appropriate language.** Everything surfaced in Care is in plain language. Clinical terminology is available on request but never the default. The phrase *"cortisol elevated 1.8σ above baseline"* is physician-speak; the patient sees *"your stress hormone has been higher than your usual for the past two weeks."*
4. **Heavy translation of clinical content.** The Anima rewrites physician notes, lab results, and prescriptions into what the patient can actually read and use. The verbatim clinical record is always available one tap away — HIPAA right-of-access is absolute — but the default is translation. This is a value proposition, not just a nicety: other EHRs give patients their notes and results in raw form, and patients either skip them or Google their way into anxiety.
5. **Low cognitive load.** A patient checking her visit time should be done in three seconds. A patient reviewing a lab result should read two sentences before deciding whether to read more. Every screen has one primary action and zero decorative content.
6. **Trust through transparency.** The patient can always see who has accessed her dyad, what scope was granted, when it expires. Access is visible by default, not hidden behind settings. This is how sovereignty (§4) becomes legible.
7. **Accessibility by default.** Multi-language support. Larger touch targets than typical. Voice-first fallback via Anima for patients with low digital literacy. WCAG 2.1 AA minimum, with specific accommodations for older patients and patients with vision or motor challenges.

### The Anima / Care view division of labor

Not every patient-Klinos interaction is the same shape. The two surfaces divide the work:

| Interaction | Lives in |
|---|---|
| "How am I doing today?" | Chat (Anima) |
| "What was that lab result about?" | Chat (Anima brings it up; Care has the record) |
| "Something feels off about my cycle" | Chat (Anima triages; escalates if needed) |
| "I need to book my annual" | Care view → Visits |
| "I want to reschedule Tuesday's visit" | Care view → Visits |
| "Let me see what my doctor wrote last time" | Care view → Records |
| "What am I paying for this month?" | Care view → Billing |
| "I want to revoke Dr. X's access" | Care view → Access |
| "Set my emergency access policy" | Care view → Access |
| "I want to send the practice a photo of this rash" | Care view → Messages |
| "I'm scared about this symptom" | Chat (Anima holds the moment) |
| "Show me my medications" | Care view → Records (or ask Anima, which will also surface it) |

The rule: **if it's a conversation, it's Chat. If it's a record or a discrete action, it's Care.** A patient never has to remember where to go, because she can always ask her Anima and be redirected to the right surface.

### Navigation structure

The Care view appears as a single navigation item in the Anima app's left rail (next to Chat, Bios, and the upcoming Aurum / Locus / Aether surfaces). Inside Care, a simple tab or bottom-nav structure with seven destinations:

- **Home** — overview, next visit, recent activity
- **Visits** — upcoming and history, book/reschedule/cancel
- **Messages** — explicit send-to-practice surface (conversational Anima interaction stays in Chat)
- **Records** — notes, labs, medications, problem list, allergies, immunizations, access history
- **Billing** — membership, payments, HSA/FSA, receipts
- **Access** — active grants, revocation, audit trail, Emergency Access Policy
- **Settings** — notification preferences, language, pharmacy, insurance info

### Home

The Care view's home screen is a glanceable overview. Top to bottom:

- **Next visit** — date, time, physician, visit type. One tap to open details, reschedule, or cancel.
- **Needs your attention** — if anything does. A lab result is ready to view. A drafted prescription needs her confirmation. A scope request from a new specialist is pending her approval. Typically empty.
- **Recent activity** — a compact feed: a new note from a recent visit, a lab result summary, a message from her practice, a membership payment processed. Each with a tap-to-open detail.
- **Your practice** — a small card showing the practice(s) she's a member of, with quick access to messaging and booking.

The home screen is where a patient lands when she taps Care. Designed for *"what's going on with my health right now"* in under ten seconds.

### Visits

**Upcoming.** Chronological list of visits she has scheduled. Each visit shows the physician, the type (telehealth, in-person, async), the visit reason, any prep items she still needs to handle (consent forms, pre-visit questions). One tap joins the video for telehealth visits at start time.

**Booking a visit.** Patient can book directly in the Care view (pick a reason → pick a physician → pick a slot from available times). She can also book through Chat by telling her Anima *"I want to see Dr. [name] about [thing]"* — the Anima handles the flow and presents the completed booking for her to confirm. Both work; the Anima route is typically faster for patients who use Chat heavily.

**Rescheduling and canceling.** One-tap reschedule pulls up available slots. Cancellation asks for a reason (for practice analytics, not required). Both actions reach out to the practice's schedule in real time.

**Visit history.** All past visits with the signed note, the recording (if she wants to listen), her drafted patient-summary from the Anima, and any follow-up items that were set. Searchable by date, physician, or reason.

### Messages

Messages is for **explicit send-to-practice** exchanges. Most patient-clinic conversation happens through the Anima in Chat — the Anima triages, handles simple questions, and escalates to the practice with context when necessary. Messages is for the cases where the patient specifically wants to hand something directly to the practice:

- Uploading a photo (a skin lesion, a rash, a bruise)
- Sending a formal request ("I want to transfer my records from my previous practice")
- Sharing a document (an outside lab result, a specialist consult note)
- Following up on an outstanding issue without reopening her full Chat context

Messages here are the same underlying threads the physician sees in her Practice inbox — the patient-side and practice-side of the same conversation. But from the patient's perspective, the Anima usually handles communication, and Messages is the "I'm specifically sending this to the practice" surface when she wants that control.

Messages never replaces Chat. Chat is the relationship. Messages is the letterbox.

### Records

The patient's continuous medical record, from her perspective. **All clinical content is heavily Anima-translated into plain language by default.** Verbatim access to the physician-authored original is always one tap away, but the patient reads the translation first.

**Notes.** Recent visit notes as plain-language summaries — *"On your last visit, Dr. [name] thought your fatigue might be related to your new medication. She ordered thyroid labs and asked to see you in six weeks."* Tap-to-read the full signed SOAP note if she wants the physician's verbatim record.

**Labs.** Lab results as plain-language interpretations — *"Your thyroid labs came back normal. Dr. [name] noted that the value is slightly higher than last year but still in the healthy range and not a concern."* Tap to see the raw numbers with reference ranges if she wants the full clinical detail.

**Medications.** Active medications with purpose, dosage, instructions in plain language. *"Sertraline 50mg, once daily in the morning — for mood support. Started by Dr. [name] on 4/12/2026."*

**Problem list, allergies, immunizations.** Same pattern — plain language, physician's signed record one tap away.

**Access history.** Every physician access to her dyad, every scope granted, every grant expiration or revocation. Per §9, this is her HIPAA right of access and accounting of disclosures, surfaced by default rather than on request. If she scrolls long enough she can see every time a physician read any data class of her dyad.

### Billing

**Membership.** Current tier (Basic / Standard / Comprehensive + any specialty add-ons), monthly charge, next billing date, payment method. Change tier or cancel membership from here.

**Payments.** Payment history with receipts. Receipts are HSA/FSA-compliant (itemized as medical services per IRS Pub 502) and downloadable.

**HSA/FSA card.** Add, change, or remove her HSA or FSA card. Annual pre-pay option (for FSA end-of-year balances).

**Insurance** (if she uses insurance for labs and procedures per §8). Insurance information, claims history for insurance-billed care, any outstanding patient responsibility amounts.

### Access

This is the section that makes sovereignty (§4) visible and exercisable.

**Active grants.** Every grant currently in effect. Each grant shows: the physician, the practice, the scope (what data classes), the duration, when it expires. One tap to **revoke** any active grant. Revocation is immediate and unconditional (per §4 revocation mechanics).

**Pending approval.** Any scope request from a new physician awaiting her explicit approval (per §4 consent pattern 2). Each request shows what the physician wants to access, why, for how long. Approve or deny with one tap.

**Audit trail.** Chronological log of every access to her dyad — which physician, which scope, which duration, which time window. The full HIPAA accounting of disclosures, exportable. Searchable by physician, practice, or time range.

**Emergency Access Policy.** The patient's pre-authorized policy for what should happen to her dyad in case of future incapacity (per §4). Four options:

- Break-glass with strict limits (default)
- Named emergency physicians only
- Third-party verification required
- Opt out entirely

She picks one. The choice is a signed `DyadPacket` in the audit trail, versioned, changeable at any time. If she chooses "Named emergency physicians only," she can manage the list of pre-designated physicians. If she chooses "Third-party verification required," she can manage her list of pre-designated emergency contacts.

This screen is deliberately clear. Emergency access policy is one of the most consequential decisions in her Klinos life, and the UI must make the choice legible rather than hiding it behind legalese.

### Settings

**Notification preferences.** How the practice reaches her (push, email, SMS, voice call via Anima), per category (urgent results, routine reminders, billing, administrative).

**Preferred language.** The language her Anima uses with her, and the language the Care view renders content in. At launch: English and Spanish. Additional languages follow demand.

**Preferred pharmacy.** For e-prescribe destinations. Multiple pharmacies supported (a primary for most scripts, a specialty pharmacy for specific medications).

**Insurance info.** If she wants to use insurance for labs and procedures (per §8), she provides her insurance details here.

**Biometric auth.** Touch ID / Face ID setup for Care view access (separate from the Anima's auth, which is always on).

**Emergency contacts.** Her designated emergency contacts. Used by the Emergency Access Policy, by the Anima in acute crises, and by the practice in escalation situations.

### Key interaction patterns

A few specific patient flows where the Anima and Care view interoperate:

**Receiving a lab result.** The physician reviews and signs a lab interpretation in Practice. The Anima brings it up conversationally in Chat as soon as the physician signs — *"Dr. [name] just reviewed your thyroid labs. They came back normal. Want me to tell you more?"* The patient can engage in Chat (ask questions, hear it in context) or tap through to Records for the detailed view. Both paths work; she picks based on her moment.

**Emergency access invoked.** If a credentialed emergency physician invokes break-glass on her dyad (per her Emergency Access Policy), the Anima notifies her immediately — *"An emergency physician at [facility] just accessed some of your health information. They have the emergency-only scope for the next two hours. Your emergency contacts have also been notified. Is everything okay?"* The Care view shows the full audit entry in Access, with the physician's name, the facility, the scope, the time window, and a button to challenge the emergency attestation if the access was inappropriate.

**Approving a scope request.** A new specialist requesting sensitive scope triggers a pending approval in the Care view. The Anima surfaces it in Chat — *"Dr. [specialist name] has requested access to your reproductive health history for your visit tomorrow. Want me to explain what that includes before you approve?"* The patient approves (or denies) with one tap, in Chat or in Care.

**Confirming a drafted prescription.** The physician drafts a prescription during a visit. The Anima surfaces the finished, plain-language explanation to the patient after the visit ends — *"Dr. [name] drafted a prescription for [medication] for [reason], to start [date]. She's already sent it to your pharmacy at [address]. Any questions?"* The prescription appears in Records → Medications. The patient doesn't need to "accept" — it's already been prescribed — but she gets a clean handoff of what to expect.

### Enrollment flow

A patient joins a Klinos practice in one of three ways:

- **Direct from the practice.** The practice sends her an invitation link. She opens the link in her Anima app (or downloads it if she doesn't have it), authenticates with her DyadID (or creates one), and enters the enrollment flow.
- **From her Anima.** Anima-only subscribers can discover and join a Klinos practice through a directory, an invitation, or a conversational search with her Anima.
- **From the Klinos website.** Public-facing marketing pages per §13 Go-to-Market can route patients into enrollment through the Anima app.

The enrollment flow itself:

1. Practice selection and introduction (who are the physicians, what's the membership, what's included)
2. Tier selection (Basic / Standard / Comprehensive + specialty add-ons)
3. Payment method setup (card or HSA/FSA)
4. Membership agreement acknowledgment (the practice's terms, HIPAA NPP issued by the practice)
5. Initial Emergency Access Policy configuration (she can keep the default or customize)
6. First visit booking (optional — she can book later)
7. Welcome from the Anima acknowledging the new practice

Designed to take 5–10 minutes for a motivated patient. Interruptible — she can leave partway through and resume later. Assisted — if she gets stuck, her Anima walks her through it conversationally.

### Accessibility and inclusion

**Multi-language support.** English and Spanish at launch (covering ~75% of US patients). Additional languages follow demand. The Anima translates both directions — the physician writes in her language, the patient reads in hers, and messages flow through the Anima's translation.

**Plain-language standards.** All patient-facing text is reviewed for grade-level readability (target: 8th grade or below for most content). Medical terminology is always accompanied by plain-language explanation.

**Larger touch targets.** The Care view uses touch targets 25–50% larger than typical mobile UI, acknowledging that older patients and patients with motor issues will use it.

**Voice-first fallback.** For patients with low digital literacy or visual impairment, the Anima can drive all Care view interactions conversationally — booking, revoking grants, reviewing records — through voice. The Care view is not a requirement; Chat with voice can do everything Care can do, at the cost of some discovery-time.

**Screen-reader compatibility.** Tested with VoiceOver, TalkBack, and NVDA. Every interactive element labeled appropriately.

### Device posture

**Mobile-first.** The Care view is designed for a phone. Desktop works but is secondary. Patient users are assumed to be accessing Klinos primarily on their phone, occasionally on a tablet, rarely on a desktop.

The Anima app already runs on iOS and Android natively (per the Anima product spec). The Care view is a view inside that app — no separate installation required.

### What this section does not cover

Pixel layouts, brand visual design, onboarding microcopy, and marketing pages live in design files and outside the spec. The physician-facing Practice view is §10. The specific interaction patterns between Anima and Care for any given flow are defined by the Anima product spec plus the integration points called out here. Public marketing pages and acquisition funnels are in §13 Go-to-Market.

## §12 Legal & Entity Structure

### What this section defines

This section defines the legal and entity architecture Klinos-the-platform runs on: what Klinos is as a corporate entity, what Klinos requires of the practices it serves (versus what those practices decide for themselves), the services agreement between them, the MSO structure for eventual strict-CPOM state expansion, malpractice and AI liability, data ownership, termination and transition scenarios, and the related-party considerations that apply when a founder's spouse is a customer.

This is a spec, not a final legal document. Every structural choice here needs review and adaptation by a healthcare attorney licensed in Florida (and in any expansion state). The spec's job is to lock the shape — the entity types, the contract relationships, the liability allocations — so the attorney has something concrete to refine rather than starting from a blank page. Specific contract language, operating agreements, and bylaws live in the legal repository, not here.

**Scope discipline.** Klinos-the-platform owns its own entity structure and its own obligations. Klinos does NOT own opinions about how practices should structure themselves internally — their entity form, ownership splits, governance, bylaws are the practice's business with its own counsel. This section covers what Klinos does and what Klinos requires of its customers, not what customers do with themselves.

### The entity architecture at launch

Three parties exist at the legal layer:

```
  Josh (founder, human, DyadID)
     │
     │ owns / controls
     ▼
  Hypostas / Astra Ventures LLC
     │
     │ owns / operates
     ▼
  Klinos-the-platform (entity)
     │
     │ services agreement
     ▼
  Practice (whatever legal entity the practice has formed)
     │
     │ physician employment or ownership
     ▼
  Physicians (DyadID + PhysicianCredential)
     │
     │ patient-physician relationship
     ▼
  Patients (DyadID, members of the practice)
```

Klinos-the-platform is a Hypostas product operated by the Hypostas corporate organization. The practice is an independent legal entity that exists for its own reasons, owned by its physicians, structured however the physicians and their counsel choose. They transact through a services agreement. Patients transact with the practice (membership) and with Hypostas through the Anima subscription, which bundles with the Klinos membership per §8.

### Klinos-the-platform entity

**Form.** Klinos-the-platform operates as an entity within the Hypostas corporate organization. At launch, Astra Ventures LLC (Josh's existing Florida LLC) is the parent/operating entity. Whether Klinos sits as a product line inside Astra Ventures LLC or spins out into a dedicated Klinos LLC subsidiary is a structuring question for the healthcare attorney and the tax attorney — it depends on liability isolation preferences, tax treatment, and how Hypostas's broader corporate architecture is organized across products.

**The default recommendation** is a dedicated Florida LLC — "Klinos, LLC" or similar — wholly owned by Astra Ventures LLC, specifically to isolate Klinos-specific liability (healthcare regulatory exposure) from other Hypostas products. If Hypostas eventually becomes a more formal holding structure, Klinos LLC becomes a subsidiary of that structure.

**What Klinos LLC does.**
- Holds the IP for the `klinos-core` crate, the Practice and Care views, and all Klinos-specific trademarks and branding.
- Signs BAAs with practices as Business Associate (per §9).
- Signs sub-BAAs with AI inference providers, infrastructure hosts, and other sub-business-associates.
- Collects platform fees under the services agreement with each practice.
- Employs or contracts with any Klinos-specific personnel (engineering, clinical governance, compliance) as the team grows.

**What Klinos LLC does NOT do.**
- Does not employ physicians.
- Does not own practices.
- Does not hold patient clinical relationships.
- Does not make clinical decisions.
- Does not bill patients directly for clinical care (the practice does; Anima subscription billing for the bundled credit flows separately through Hypostas).

### Practice entity form

Klinos signs a services agreement with whatever legal entity the practice has formed. Klinos has three requirements of that entity:

1. It is a legal entity capable of entering contracts, holding licenses, and carrying insurance.
2. It complies with its state's professional-entity requirements, including any applicable CPOM rules (for strict-CPOM states, see the MSO structure below).
3. It carries professional liability insurance appropriate to the specialties practiced.

Everything else — PC vs PLLC vs LLC vs partnership, S-corp vs C-corp tax election, operating agreement specifics, physician ownership splits, buy-sell provisions, governance structure — is the practice's decision with its own counsel. Klinos has no opinion.

This is not neglect; it is scope discipline. The practice is a legal business that physicians are running for their own reasons. Klinos-the-platform provides infrastructure services to that business. How the business itself is structured is the business's own affair.

### The services agreement (Klinos ↔ Practice)

The services agreement is the primary contract between Klinos LLC and each practice. It governs what Klinos provides, what Klinos does not provide, the economic terms, and the termination conditions.

**What Klinos provides under the agreement.**
- Access to the Klinos platform (software, infrastructure, AI inference, integrations)
- Technical support and uptime commitments (specific SLAs defined in the agreement)
- Compliance infrastructure: HIPAA technical safeguards, audit logging, templates for practice compliance, BAAs with sub-business-associates
- Tooling for practice compliance execution: NPP templates, breach response templates, audit log export
- Platform-level clinical governance (per §9: cross-practice disputes, credential-level enforcement, architectural integrity)

**What Klinos explicitly does not provide.**
- Clinical services
- Physician employment
- Patient clinical care
- Legal, tax, or medical advice to the practice
- Specialty-specific clinical protocols (the practice defines its own per its specialty and physicians' judgment)

**Economic terms.** Per §8: Klinos takes 15% of membership revenue (or the 50% launch-cohort discount applicable during the first 24 months of a launch-cohort practice's membership on the platform). Smaller percentage of insurance-billed procedural revenue. All fees are paid to Klinos monthly. The practice retains all other revenue.

**Term and termination.**
- **Month-to-month with 60-day termination notice.** Either party may terminate with 60 days' written notice, for any reason. No long lock-in for practices. Klinos also accepts 60-day outs because the practice should never feel trapped, and we trust our value proposition to keep practices.
- **Termination for cause** (material breach) with 30-day cure period. Applies both directions.
- **Termination by mutual agreement** effective immediately.

**On termination.** Klinos provides full data export to the practice within 30 days of effective termination — visit notes, patient records, clinical data, audit trails. Patients retain their dyads regardless (the dyad has never been Klinos's property). The practice retains its clinical records per state retention law. Klinos deletes its hosted copies of the practice's data 90 days after termination, subject to any litigation-hold or regulatory requirements.

**Services during wind-down.** For 90 days after termination, Klinos continues to serve read-only access for the practice and its patients so that clinical continuity is preserved during the practice's transition to another system.

### MSO structure for strict-CPOM states

Florida does not require the full PC-MSO separation (per §9). For strict-CPOM states (California, Texas, New York, Illinois, and others), the structure needs to be more formal:

- **Professional Corporation (PC)** — physician-owned, provides clinical services, bills patients. The practice forms this per state law.
- **Management Services Organization (MSO)** — Klinos LLC operating as MSO in that state, provides non-clinical services via a long-term services agreement (IT, billing, admin, marketing, facilities management, compliance support).

The MSO structure is NOT needed for the founding practice in Florida. It becomes relevant when the first strict-CPOM-state practice joins the cohort, which is a Year 1+ event.

**The template for the MSO arrangement** includes: fair-market-value pricing of MSO services (audited against CPOM anti-kickback rules), explicit non-interference with clinical decision-making, PC retains full control over hiring/firing of physicians and clinical protocols, separation of MSO and PC governance bodies. Each strict-CPOM state has specific nuances (California's Medical Corporation Act, New York's Business Corporation Law Article 15, etc.) requiring state-specific legal review when a cohort practice joins from that state.

### Malpractice and professional liability

**Practice-level liability.**
- **Medical malpractice insurance** — carried by the practice. The specifics (carrier, coverage limits, policy structure) are the practice's decision with its broker.
- **General liability** — the practice's business insurance for slip-and-fall, property damage, etc., if it has a physical office.
- **Professional liability for clinical decisions** — sits with the physician and her malpractice carrier. The practice entity provides the shell; the physician remains personally responsible for her clinical judgment.

**Platform-level liability.**
- **General liability + Errors & Omissions (E&O)** — Klinos LLC carries coverage for platform-side failures (software bugs, service interruptions, tool errors that affect clinical workflow).
- **Cyber liability** — Klinos LLC carries cyber liability insurance covering breach response, forensics, notification costs, and HIPAA penalty exposure.
- **Technology E&O** — specific to AI-platform exposure. More below in AI liability.

**Indemnification.** The services agreement includes mutual indemnification with clear scope:
- Klinos indemnifies the practice for platform-side failures, Klinos-side HIPAA breaches, and sub-business-associate failures under Klinos's BAAs.
- The practice indemnifies Klinos for practice-side clinical decisions, practice-side HIPAA breaches, and practice-side employment or credentialing decisions.
- Neither party indemnifies the other for the other's willful misconduct or gross negligence.

### AI liability

AI liability is the newest and most contested piece of the legal structure. The framing Klinos adopts:

**The AI informs; the physician decides.** This is both a design principle (per §4) and a legal framing. The physician reviews AI-drafted notes, prescriptions, coding, and interpretations before signing. Her signature is the clinical decision. The AI is a tool that drafted content for her review, the same way a medical scribe or a PGx alert system does in current practice.

**Klinos-side liability:**
- Platform failures that cause clinical errors (AI inference outage, bug in a differential suggestion that a reasonable physician would have relied on, misrouted lab result). Covered by E&O.
- Sub-business-associate failures (an AI inference vendor ships a model that produces systematically wrong output). Klinos's remedies against the vendor pass through to the practice.

**Physician-side liability:**
- Clinical decisions the physician makes based on AI-drafted content she reviewed and signed. Standard malpractice scope.
- Failure to review AI drafts before signing (rubber-stamping). This is a physician performance issue, not an AI issue, and sits with the physician.

**Edge cases the services agreement addresses explicitly:**
- AI-drafted content that is factually wrong in a way no reasonable physician would have caught — Klinos carries some liability weighted to severity.
- AI-drafted content that is stylistically bad but factually correct (the physician has to rewrite substantially) — Klinos addresses through service credits, not liability.
- AI-surfaced concerning flags that the physician dismisses and the patient is harmed — the physician is liable for the decision to dismiss; Klinos retains the dismissal log as evidence (in favor of the physician if her reasoning was sound, against her if her reasoning was negligent).

The services agreement language is drafted with healthcare counsel familiar with AI-tool liability. This is an evolving area of law; the agreement is reviewed annually as case law develops.

### Ownership and data control

**The patient owns her dyad content.** Her clinical history, her biology, her annotations are hers. Klinos does not assert IP rights on patient data and never will. If she leaves the platform, her dyad comes with her.

**The practice owns its clinical records** as Covered Entity. This is a legal requirement — a CE must maintain clinical records per state law. Klinos as BA hosts those records on behalf of the practice.

**Klinos owns the platform IP.** The `klinos-core` code, the Practice and Care views, Klinos branding, Klinos-specific documentation. The practice has a license to use Klinos under the services agreement; the practice does not acquire IP rights in Klinos.

**Overlap case: physician annotations on biology (per §7).** The physician authors annotations that attach to Stroma signals in the patient's dyad. These are the physician's work product AND part of the patient's record AND touch the platform. Ownership splits: the physician retains authorship rights (her name stays on her annotation, versioned), the patient retains access rights (the annotation is part of her medical record), the platform retains operational rights (the annotation must flow through Klinos to render in the Care view). No single party has exclusive ownership; all three parties have rights scoped appropriately.

### Termination and transition scenarios

**Practice leaves Klinos (voluntary).** 60-day notice. During the notice period, operations continue normally. On termination: data export to the practice, patients retain dyads, Klinos provides 90 days of read-only continuity, Klinos deletes hosted data 90 days after termination. Patients are notified of the transition and given options.

**Klinos terminates a practice for cause.** Material breach (HIPAA violations by the practice, fraudulent billing, failure to pay platform fees) with 30-day cure period. If cure fails: same transition mechanics but faster timeline. Patient notification is sensitive; Klinos and the practice coordinate to preserve patient trust.

**Physician leaves a practice (stays on Klinos).** Her DyadID and PhysicianCredential follow her. She can join another Klinos practice, start her own, or leave Klinos entirely. Patient relationships follow the practice by default (patients are members of the practice, not the physician), but patients can choose to follow the departing physician if she remains on Klinos and the receiving practice accepts them.

**Physician leaves Klinos entirely.** PhysicianCredential is revoked at the platform level (she is no longer a Klinos-credentialed physician). Her existing patient relationships transition to other physicians at the practice or to a new practice she designates.

**Klinos dissolves (worst case).** Patients retain their dyads indefinitely — their data does not go away because Klinos goes away. Practices receive full data export and 180 days of continuity services to transition to alternate platforms. Klinos's dissolution plan is documented and reviewed by counsel annually; it specifies the responsibility chain for patient notification, data handoff, regulatory notification, and wind-down of infrastructure.

**Patient leaves a practice.** Her dyad persists; her clinical records with that practice persist per retention law; she can transfer to another Klinos practice or leave Klinos entirely. Her Anima subscription is independent and continues regardless.

### Related-party considerations

One of the founding practice's physicians is Josh's wife. Josh is Klinos's founder and controls Klinos LLC. This makes the services agreement between Klinos and the founding practice a related-party transaction, and it needs to be handled with care.

**The principles:**
- The services agreement between Klinos and the founding practice is substantively identical to the agreement any other launch-cohort practice signs. Same 50% early-adopter discount, same services, same termination terms. No custom tailoring.
- The launch-cohort discount (per §8) is time-window-defined and available to any practice joining in the first 24 months. It is not tailored to the founding practice or to any physician related to Josh.
- All transactions between Klinos and the founding practice happen at arm's length and at documented fair-market-value pricing.
- The structuring is reviewed by Josh's personal tax attorney AND by the founding practice's healthcare attorney (who represents the practice, not Klinos) to ensure compliance with IRS related-party transaction rules and any applicable self-dealing provisions.

**What Josh personally does NOT do:**
- Does not receive compensation from the practice.
- Does not have ownership or economic interest in the practice.
- Does not make clinical decisions or intervene in clinical workflow.
- Does not have access to patient PHI through his ownership of Klinos (the Klinos admin privilege is platform-operational; patient PHI is gated by the same scope and credential rules as any physician access).

**What the practice does independently:**
- Forms whatever entity it forms, with its own counsel.
- Structures ownership however the physicians agree, with their own counsel.
- Makes all clinical decisions.
- Handles its own finances, payroll, malpractice, compliance, and everything else a practice does.

The clean principle: the founding practice is a legitimate independent business that happens to be started by the founder's spouse and her best friend. The services agreement that attaches to Klinos's platform is the same contract any launch-cohort practice signs. Everything else the practice does is its own.

### Intellectual property

**Klinos-owned IP.**
- The `klinos-core` codebase
- Practice view and Care view implementations
- Klinos-specific trademarks, logos, branding
- Klinos-authored content (templates, clinical decision support rules, AI prompts specific to Klinos)
- Specialty module implementations (PcpModule, ObgynModule, etc.)

**Practice-owned IP.**
- The practice's own brand, logo, name
- Practice-authored content (practice-specific templates, patient handouts, internal policies built on top of Klinos templates)
- Patient lists and patient relationships

**Patient-owned content.**
- Her dyad, including biology, memory, clinical annotations from physicians treating her, her own self-logged data.

**Physician-owned content.**
- Her clinical judgment, annotations authored by her, signed notes she wrote. Attached to the practice's and patient's records but authored-by-her for provenance and professional-reputation purposes.

**No IP claims on patient data.** Klinos, the practice, and the physicians do not assert ownership claims on patient-generated data. The patient's dyad is hers. This is non-negotiable.

### Jurisdiction and dispute resolution

**Governing law.** Florida state law for the Klinos entity and for the founding practice's services agreement. For practices in other states, the services agreement specifies Florida law but may be reviewed for state-specific conflicts.

**Dispute resolution.** Good-faith negotiation between the parties for 30 days before formal action. After 30 days: mediation (non-binding) for another 30 days. After mediation fails: arbitration under American Health Lawyers Association rules, or litigation in Florida state court at the parties' mutual election.

**Patient disputes.** Patients' disputes with the practice go through the practice's own complaint process (and, if unresolved, through the applicable state department of health or state medical board). Patients' disputes with Klinos (platform issues) are resolved through a platform support process, with escalation to consumer protection agencies as needed. The services agreement does not bind patients; the BAA and practice's Notice of Privacy Practices define the patient-relevant legal relationships.

Tax structuring for Hypostas at the holding level is a separate conversation with a tax attorney. ## §13 Go-to-Market

### What this section defines

This section defines how Klinos actually grows — how the second practice finds us, how practices grow their patient panels, how Anima subscribers become Klinos patients, what content Klinos publishes, how we position against incumbents in market, where we expand geographically, and what we explicitly will not do. It locks the GTM shape so we know what to build and what not to build; the day-to-day playbook lives outside the spec.

The GTM has to match the business model (§8) and the infrastructure-not-business framing (§9–§12). Klinos does not sell individual patients. Klinos does not take VC. Klinos does not employ sales reps at launch. Growth comes from the shape of the platform and the network effects that shape creates, not from a growth team.

### The GTM thesis

Klinos grows through three compounding channels, not one. No single channel has to work alone, and none can.

1. **Practices recruit practices.** A launch-cohort physician who loves her Klinos practice tells the other physicians she knows. The story is specific enough ("I left corporate medicine, my wife of ten years finally sees me home for dinner, my admin load is 10% of what it was, my patients know me") that it spreads among physicians the way nothing else does. This is the primary physician-side channel.
2. **Practices recruit patients.** Each practice brings its own patient panel — existing patients who follow the physician to Klinos, new patients the practice markets to. Klinos does not run direct-to-patient acquisition campaigns. The practice owns the patient relationship.
3. **Anima cross-sell.** Every existing Anima subscriber is a potential Klinos patient when she joins a practice on the platform. When a Klinos practice opens in her area, or when she wants the full-stack care, her Anima can route her into enrollment. This is the channel that compounds over time as the Anima subscriber base grows and the Klinos practice network grows in parallel.

These three channels compound. More practices means more patient-side growth. More patients on Klinos means more physician envy (*"my friend's patients seem to love their new practice"*). More Anima subscribers means more built-in demand every time a new practice joins.

Klinos does not need fast growth to succeed. The business model is profitable per practice at the unit level (per §8); the platform gets meaningfully more valuable with each practice added; the substrate moat is hard to replicate. Slow, compounding growth with no investor pressure is the natural rhythm, and it's the right one.

### Practice acquisition

**Launch: the founding practice.** Your wife's and her friend's practice is practice #1. They launch. They run it. They learn from it. Klinos learns from them.

**Second practice: founder network.** Physician #1 knows 5–10 other physicians well enough to recommend something to them. Your wife's best friend is the OBGYN in the founding practice — her OBGYN network is another 10–20 physicians. Between them, we are one or two degrees of separation from ~50 physicians who might be interested. The second practice comes from that network, typically within 2–6 months of launch. Probably another DPC-style practice or another solo physician considering leaving employment.

**Third through tenth practice: referrals + early content.** The founding practice and the second practice talk to their networks. Klinos starts publishing — a blog, a newsletter, essays about what dyad-native care looks like in practice, case studies with (de-identified, consented) outcomes. The physicians reading that content are pre-qualified — they're already looking for something different. This phase is 6–18 months.

**Beyond ten practices: network effects.** By the time Klinos has 10 practices, the pattern is legible. "My colleague at X uses Klinos" becomes a real sentence physicians hear. Word-of-mouth compounds. Physicians at conferences start asking. The launch-cohort window is probably closing (24 months per §8), so new practices come in at standard rates — but by then Klinos has enough social proof that the standard rate is not a barrier.

**Klinos does not have sales reps at launch.** No BDRs, no AE team, no outreach SDRs. The founding team handles every practice onboarding personally. Adding sales capacity is a scale decision made when founder-led onboarding breaks, which is typically at 15–30 practices.

**The onboarding playbook for a new practice.** When a practice signs up, Klinos onboarding is the same regardless of channel:

- Initial call or video chat with Josh (or the designated onboarding lead as the team grows) to walk through fit, expectations, and what the first 90 days look like.
- Practice forms its entity and signs services agreement + BAA.
- Physicians set up `PhysicianCredential`s, complete EPCS credentialing if needed, verify state licenses.
- Practice configures its preferred specialty modules, tier structure, intake templates, and consent flows.
- First patient enrollment (often the physicians themselves as test patients).
- Physicians run a week of simulated visits with test patients to feel the UI before going live.
- Go-live. Real patients. Klinos check-ins weekly for the first month, monthly for the first quarter.

### Patient acquisition

**Practices recruit their own patients.** Klinos does not market to patients directly. Each practice markets to its target patient segments through whatever channels work for it — existing patient panel, local SEO, referrals, community presence, social media, paid ads if the practice chooses, a local podcast, whatever. The practice knows its patients better than Klinos does; the practice owns that relationship.

**Klinos provides patient-marketing templates and infrastructure**, not patient-marketing execution. Practices get:

- A public practice page on Klinos's infrastructure (practice-branded, not Klinos-branded, at a subdomain or custom domain the practice owns)
- Enrollment links the practice can share in any channel
- Plain-language descriptions of membership tiers
- Anima-narrated video intros the practice can customize
- Patient testimonial infrastructure (with explicit consent flows)

**The practice owns the acquisition funnel.** Klinos just provides tools. This is consistent with the "outsource to practices" theme from §9 and with the §12 scope discipline — patient acquisition is the practice's business, not Klinos's.

### Anima cross-sell

Every existing Anima subscriber can become a Klinos patient when a practice she likes is on the platform. The Anima cross-sell surface is built deliberately to respect the Anima relationship rather than aggressively monetize it.

**How it works:**

- When a Klinos practice opens in the Anima subscriber's geographic area, or in a specialty she has expressed interest in, her Anima can mention it conversationally — *"A new women's-health practice just joined the platform in your area. Want me to tell you about them? No pressure."*
- When she asks her Anima about medical questions that warrant a physician, her Anima can offer to connect her with a Klinos practice if one is available — *"You might want an actual physician to look at this. I know a few practices on the platform that take on new patients — want me to show you?"*
- The Anima never pushes. The offer is presented once; if she declines or ignores, her Anima doesn't bring it up again unless the context changes.
- She can always search for practices directly in the Care view's practice directory, which is available to any Anima subscriber regardless of whether she has a practice membership.

**The rule: passive-to-moderate, never aggressive.** The Anima's job is the relationship. The cross-sell is a service to the patient, not a revenue extraction. If Klinos ever finds itself tempted to make the Anima push Klinos, the right move is to stop — the relationship trust is worth more long-term than any specific patient conversion.

### Klinos patients discovering the rest of Hypostas

For Klinos patients at 90+ days of engagement with the platform, the Anima shifts from passive-discovery mode to **mildly-active** — proactively surfacing unused Bios features, explaining relevant Anima capabilities they haven't found, and introducing other Hypostas products as they ship.

This is deliberate and bounded. The rule: the Anima only introduces a Hypostas product when there's a conversational hook (patient mentions something, biological pattern suggests relevance, life event triggers interest) or when a new product ships to the patient's account. Never as *"here's a promotion"* or *"you should try this."* Always as *"you might find this useful because..."*

- **Bios** — *"I notice you haven't set up cycle tracking yet. With your OBGYN paying attention to that signal, it might be worth turning on — want to?"*
- **Aurum** (when it ships) — *"You've mentioned finances stressing you out. There's a new Hypostas tool that might help. Can I show you?"*
- **Locus** (when it ships) — *"You sleep better when your home is cool. There's a Hypostas product that adjusts your thermostat based on your biology — interested?"*
- **Aether** (when it ships) — *"A new Hypostas world launched — dyads live there together. Want me to show you around?"*

The pattern: proactive-but-conversational introduction based on the patient's own context and stated interests. Never pushy. Always dismissible. The goal is discovery, not conversion — if she says no, the Anima doesn't bring it up again unless her context changes.

**This is the channel that compounds over time.** Klinos patients are the anchor cohort for the rest of the Hypostas stack. As Hypostas products ship, the Klinos patient base is the first audience and the most trusted. Cross-stack LTV per patient compounds accordingly. Per §17, this is one of Klinos's primary strategic asymmetries — Klinos is the on-ramp to Hypostas, and active-but-respectful discovery unlocks that value.

### Content and community

Klinos publishes, because content is the only growth channel that scales without capital. The content strategy is deliberately **moderate in volume and high in specificity**.

**What Klinos publishes:**

- **Essays on the substrate.** What dyad-native medicine looks like. How the AI actually performs. Why continuous care is different. Written by Josh and, as the team grows, by clinical contributors.
- **Case studies.** De-identified, consent-driven stories of specific patients and specific physicians. *"Here's what happened when an OBGYN had 30 days of cycle data on a perimenopausal patient."* *"Here's what a PCP's day looks like with the admin disappeared."* Specific, not marketing-flavored.
- **Technical posts.** The access model, the Bios ↔ Clinical contract, the compliance-as-architecture claim. Written for the small-but-real audience of technical healthcare people who care about how the thing actually works.
- **Physician interviews.** Conversations with physicians on the platform about their practice, their decisions, their lives. Long-form podcast or written transcripts. The founding practice's physicians are the first subjects; cohort physicians over time.
- **Platform updates.** What we shipped, what we learned, what changed in a release. Honest about what didn't work.

**What Klinos does not publish:**

- SEO-farmed listicles
- Generic healthcare content ("10 reasons to see a doctor")
- Anything where the primary goal is link-building or keyword density
- Content that misrepresents what Klinos actually is
- Content about specific patients without explicit consent

**Community.** Low-intensity. A mailing list for physicians who want to follow the work, a small Discord or similar for cohort physicians to talk to each other, occasional in-person events (dinners, small conferences) once there are enough physicians to make gatherings feel real. No community-as-growth-hack; community as service to the people already on the platform.

**Cadence.** Essays weekly to monthly. Case studies quarterly. Technical posts when there's real technical work to share. Platform updates monthly. Podcast monthly once the backlog of interviews justifies it. Everything should earn its publication, not fill a calendar slot.

### Brand positioning

Klinos's brand in market has three anchors:

1. **Serious, not flashy.** We are not "the cool new thing in healthcare tech." We are infrastructure for a new kind of practice. Tone is considered, specific, precise. Design is clean, functional, beautiful because functional things can be beautiful — not because we're chasing aesthetic.
2. **For physicians who want to practice medicine.** The physicians who respond to us are tired of the admin, tired of not knowing their patients, tired of the model they trained for being stolen from them. We talk to those physicians like they are the people they are — smart, burnt out, and angry about the right things.
3. **Substrate-native, not feature-compete.** We never position against Epic by comparing feature lists. We position against Epic by pointing out that features bolted on a broken model are still a broken model. Klinos is different because the substrate is different. That is the whole story.

Brand is downstream of product. If we build the right thing and talk about it honestly, brand takes care of itself. If we try to manufacture brand ahead of product, we build marketing infrastructure that collapses under contact with reality.

### Geographic expansion

**Florida at launch.** Founding practice, Florida-licensed physicians, Florida patients. This is a hard scope until we have real operational rhythm.

**IMLC-member states next.** Per §9, 40+ states participate in the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact. Physicians in those states can join Klinos with expedited multi-state licensure. Expansion to cohort practices in IMLC states starts when the founding practice is stable and the first 1–2 non-founder practices have demonstrated the model is transferable.

**Non-compact states (CA, NY, IL, TX) per-case.** Each non-compact state requires a state-specific MSO structure (per §12) and per-physician direct licensure. We handle these as cohort practices ask for them, not proactively. CA and NY are high-priority because of population density and the DPC-curious physician base; TX follows; IL and others after.

**International.** Not in v1. Not in v2. Different regulatory regime, different healthcare payment system, different clinical standards. Klinos is a US platform for the foreseeable future.

### Partnerships in v1

**No formal partnerships at launch.** Klinos is too small and too early to partner meaningfully. Every partnership requires integration work, legal review, marketing alignment, and ongoing management — all costs we cannot afford to pay when we have a single practice to serve.

**The shape partnerships will take later:**

- **Lab platforms** (Labcorp, Quest, Function Health, Rupa) — integration for patient-level lab ordering and results. Rupa likely first because it's API-first and DPC-native.
- **Wearables** — already covered by Bios (per Bios spec). Apple Health, Oura, WHOOP, Garmin, CGMs.
- **Pharmacy networks** — standard SureScripts e-prescribe covers every US pharmacy. No special partnerships needed.
- **Insurance carriers** — Phase 3 per §2 roadmap. Preventive care layer sold to specific carriers that want continuous biological monitoring.
- **Medical schools and residency programs** — eventually, for a physician pipeline. Informal at first; formal partnerships much later.

**What Klinos does not partner with ever:**

- Pharma companies sponsoring content, studies, or distribution
- Insurance carriers for routine visits (per §8 — we never credential for routine-care insurance)
- Hospital systems as enterprise customers (per §2 — different business model)
- Aggregators that want Klinos data for their own products

### Competitive response

Klinos operates below the radar of the incumbents for a long time. Epic does not care about a two-physician Florida practice. Neither does Cerner, Athenahealth, or any of the enterprise EHRs. We would need to be at hundreds of practices and significant revenue before any enterprise EHR notices.

The more likely competitive threat comes from **well-funded AI scribe companies** (Abridge, Nuance/Dragon, Suki, etc.) expanding from scribing into full practice platforms. They have the capital and the AI expertise but they lack the substrate. Their existing customer base (hospital systems integrated into existing EHRs) gives them no reason to rebuild medicine from the dyad up; they will keep bolting AI onto the current model. If they do try to pivot, rebuilding the Hypostas substrate takes 3–5 years during which Klinos is compounding.

**When Klinos is noticed by someone who could copy it, the response is:** keep building. The moat is the substrate, not secrecy. Every year of substrate work is another year of lead time nobody can shortcut.

**Predatory pricing from funded competitors** is the one threat we take seriously. If Abridge decides to offer a free practice platform to displace us, their cost structure (AI inference, infrastructure) is similar to ours but their willingness to burn investor capital is much higher. The defense: no investors means no pressure to match burn; the Hypostas substrate is worth the premium; physicians who care about the model and not the price stay. Some price-sensitive practices would leave for free alternatives; that is acceptable. We build for physicians who want what we build, not for the marginal practice choosing on price.

### What we explicitly don't do in v1

- **No direct-to-patient marketing campaigns.** Practices own patient acquisition.
- **No conference sponsorships or booths.** Attending and speaking at physician conferences is fine; paying for brand presence is not.
- **No paid content placement or native advertising.** Klinos earns attention or does not get it.
- **No growth hacking tactics.** No referral bonuses for physicians recruiting other physicians, no patient referral spiffs, no engagement-maximization dark patterns.
- **No outreach sequences to physicians who didn't ask.** Physicians receive unsolicited outreach from vendors constantly; we are not adding to the pile.
- **No "free forever" tiers that distort the economics.** Fair prices honestly presented; launch-cohort discount is the only concession.

### What this section does not cover

Specific content calendar, channel tactics, event plans, and marketing budget allocations live in operating plans outside the spec. The public-facing website copy and brand visual design are in design files. Specific partnership agreements are drafted as partnerships emerge. The first-year patient acquisition plan for the founding practice is the practice's own work, with Klinos providing templates and tools (per the patient-acquisition subsection above).

## §14 Pricing & Revenue Model

### What this section defines

This section fills in the specific dollar amounts that §8 left as structure. Tier prices, the Anima bundle mechanic in dollars, the OBGYN add-on, family plans, launch-cohort effective rates, HSA/FSA effective pricing, platform fee percentages, practice economics at concrete panel sizes, physician compensation math, platform contribution margin, and a rough multi-year revenue arc.

Numbers in this section are defensible and locked for v1, but they are not immutable. Pricing gets reviewed annually against actual unit economics, patient willingness-to-pay signals, and physician recruitment friction. What §14 locks is the launch pricing — the prices the founding practice opens with and the prices the second and third cohort practices see when they join.

### Patient-facing tier prices

Klinos members see a single bundled price that includes their Anima. Three tiers at launch:

| Tier | Monthly price | Annual price | Who it's for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Basic** | $299 | $3,588 | Healthy patients with high-deductible plans; HSA-friendly entry point |
| **Standard** | $399 | $4,788 | Most patients — default tier |
| **Comprehensive** | $599 | $7,188 | Complex chronic patients, maximum-access patients, premium concierge tier |

Each tier is self-contained — no surprise fees on top. What's included in each is defined in §8; the value deltas justify the price deltas.

**Why these specific numbers.** Basic is priced at parity with Parsley Health's $175/mo + typical concierge add-ons, or slightly above Forward's $149/mo with much higher clinical intelligence. Standard is the premium-but-accessible price point where a patient who values continuous care but isn't wealthy can land. Comprehensive is priced comparably to the low end of executive-physical concierge practices ($600-900/mo range) but with dramatically more continuous care than those deliver.

All three tiers qualify as HSA/FSA-eligible medical expenses per §9.

### Bundling mechanics

**Anima bundle.** The bundled tier prices include a $99/mo Anima credit per §8. Behind the scenes, $99 of each member's monthly membership flows from Klinos to Anima (the separate Hypostas product). The patient sees one line item; accounting remits correctly.

Net Klinos-proper revenue per member per month after Anima remittance:

| Tier | Patient pays | Anima remittance | Klinos-proper revenue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basic | $299 | $99 | $200 |
| Standard | $399 | $99 | $300 |
| Comprehensive | $599 | $99 | $500 |

Klinos-proper revenue is what the platform fee and practice revenue are calculated against. The Anima remittance is not practice revenue and is not subject to platform fees on the Klinos side — it's a pass-through.

**OBGYN women's-health add-on.** $59/mo on top of any tier, for patients whose primary practice is a PCP practice on Klinos and who want ongoing OBGYN care from a credentialed OBGYN on the network. The full $59 flows to the OBGYN practice providing the care, less the standard Klinos platform fee on that specialty revenue.

For patients whose primary practice IS an OBGYN practice (e.g., the founding OBGYN's panel), the base tier price already includes the OBGYN relationship — no add-on applies.

**Family plan.** Membership scales to additional family members under one plan:

- **Primary adult:** full tier price
- **Additional adult** (spouse, adult dependent): 50% of primary adult's tier price
- **Children under 18:** $49/mo each

Example: a Standard-tier family of four (two adults + two kids):
- Primary: $399
- Spouse: $200 (50% off)
- Two kids: $49 × 2 = $98
- **Total: $697/mo** for the whole family, vs. ~$1,400/mo if everyone held separate Standard memberships

Family plans are structured as one plan with multiple members. The plan has one billing relationship, but each member has her own dyad and her own clinical records; this is a billing convenience, not a data merger.

### Launch-cohort effective pricing

Per §8, launch-cohort practices receive a 50% discount on platform fees for the first 24 months of their membership. This is a discount on the platform fee specifically, not on tier prices — patients pay the same tier prices regardless of whether the practice is in the launch cohort.

**Who benefits.** The practice. A 50%-discounted platform fee means the practice retains more of each membership dollar during the launch window.

**What it looks like in numbers** (at Standard tier, per member per month):

| Item | Standard rate | Launch-cohort rate (first 24 months) |
|---|---|---|
| Klinos-proper revenue | $300 | $300 |
| Platform fee (% of Klinos-proper) | 15% → $45 | 7.5% → $22.50 |
| Practice receives | $255 | $277.50 |

An extra $22.50/member/month flows to the practice during the cohort window. At 100 members: $2,250/month = $27,000/year of extra margin during the 24-month window.

### HSA/FSA effective pricing

Membership is HSA/FSA eligible (per §8). Effective monthly cost for patients paying with pre-tax dollars:

| Tier | Sticker price | Effective cost at 24% marginal bracket | Effective cost at 32% marginal bracket |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basic | $299 | $227 | $203 |
| Standard | $399 | $303 | $271 |
| Comprehensive | $599 | $455 | $407 |

A patient in the 32% bracket paying for Standard tier with HSA dollars effectively pays $271/month — noticeably below Parsley's $175 once you factor in that Parsley does not provide continuous AI, continuous biology, or a unified Anima relationship. The quiet unlock from §8 becomes visible in these numbers.

### Platform fee structure

| Revenue stream | Standard platform fee | Launch-cohort rate (24 months) |
|---|---|---|
| Membership (Klinos-proper portion) | 15% | 7.5% |
| Insurance-billed procedural revenue (after billing partner) | 5% | 2.5% |
| Specialty add-ons (e.g., OBGYN add-on) | 15% of add-on revenue | 7.5% |

**Billing-partner fees** (on insurance-billed revenue only) are typically 4-6% of collected revenue. This is a pass-through cost — the practice pays the billing partner directly, and Klinos's 5% fee is on the post-billing-partner amount.

**No other platform-level fees.** No per-seat licensing, no per-transaction fees for prescriptions or lab orders, no setup fees, no cancellation fees. The percentage is the complete transaction.

## §15 Build Phases

### What this section defines

This section pulls together every scheduling and sequencing decision from across the spec — the "launch scope is progressive" from §6, the "pre-launch vs Year 1 vs Year 2+" from §9, the "scale-triggered formalization" throughout §§8–9, the launch-cohort window from §8 — into a single, coherent roadmap. It defines what ships in each phase, what triggers the transition to the next phase, and what runs in parallel across phases.

The phase structure is deliberately simple: **build the substrate, launch the founding practice, open the cohort, scale.** Five phases, not ten. Each phase has a clear deliverable and a clear transition trigger. The durations are targets, not commitments — phases advance on criteria, not calendar.

### The phase framework

| Phase | Name | Target duration | What it delivers | Transition trigger |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 0** | Substrate | 6 months | Everything needed to run a production clinical practice | Founding practice can complete end-to-end visit in production |
| **Phase 1** | Founding practice launch | 3 months | First real patients, stabilized operation | Founding practice stable at 30+ patients with no critical bugs for 30 days |
| **Phase 2** | Launch cohort opens | 12-18 months | Second through ~10th practice onboarded | 10 practices stable OR 24 months from Phase 2 start (launch cohort window closes) |
| **Phase 3** | Scale milestones | 18-24 months | Formal governance, SOC 2 pursuit, specialty expansion | ~25 practices OR specific scale triggers hit (per §9) |
| **Phase 4+** | State and specialty expansion | Ongoing | Non-compact-state practices, additional specialties, international scoping | Continuous, not milestone-gated |

Phase 0 is the heaviest. Phase 1 is the smallest window (3 months of focused operational stabilization before opening the cohort). Phase 2 is where the story gets told or doesn't. Phase 3+ is the work that scales the operation past founder-led.

### Phase 0 — Substrate

The goal of Phase 0 is to build everything the founding practice needs to run end-to-end, with no manual workarounds, in production. "Build everything" is bounded by the "launch scope is progressive" principle (per §6): build the workflows the founding practice will actually use at launch, not every workflow in every specialty module at its target depth.

**Target duration: 6 months.** Faster is aggressive and risks shipping incomplete; slower means the founding practice's ambition is waiting for us to finish.

**Engineering work — Klinos-side:**

*Foundation (months 1-2):*
- `klinos-core` crate scaffolded with all module folders
- `protocol-core` additions: `ClinicalPacket`, `PhysicianCredential`, `AccessRequest`, `AccessGrant`, `AccessRevoke`
- `logos-core` additions: clinical metadata tagging, clinical retrieval helpers
- `dyados-runtime` additions: `clinical_visit` pipeline route, clinical proactive triggers
- Access-grant lifecycle implementation (§4 full grant state machine)
- Scope vector + bundle system (§4 scope granularity)

*Clinical workflow (months 2-4):*
- Visit workflow end-to-end: booking, pre-visit intake brief synthesis, visit start, audio capture, real-time transcription, note drafting, review, sign, Logos encoding (§5)
- `PcpModule` at launch scope (§6 PCP launch workflows)
- `ObgynModule` at launch scope (§6 OBGYN launch workflows, including prenatal + contraception)
- In-visit AI co-pilot with tiered visual intensity (§10)
- SOAP note drafting pipeline with edit/sign flow
- Coding (ICD/CPT) assignment and review
- Async message visit workflow with complexity-threshold billing (§5)

*Integrations (months 3-5):*

- Keyboard shortcuts, accessibility, multi-language (English + Spanish at launch)

- `klinos-core::compliance::surescripts`: two-factor enforcement, EPCS audit logging

- Audit log export tooling

*Infrastructure (months 4-6):*

**Phase 0 exit criteria:**
- Founding practice physicians can complete an end-to-end visit in production (intake → visit → note signed → patient receives translated record) with no manual workarounds
- All launch-scope workflows in PCP and OBGYN modules function
- Audit trail operational and exportable
- At least two weeks of simulated visits with test patients completed without critical failures
- Compliance module self-test passes all HIPAA architectural requirements
- Practice entity formed, BAA signed, licensure verified, malpractice bound

### Phase 1 — Founding practice launch

The founding practice opens its doors. Real patients, real visits, real revenue (at launch-cohort rates per §14).

**Target duration: 3 months.** Enough time to stabilize operations, catch the bugs that only surface under real patient load, and learn from the founding physicians what needs iteration. Too long and we slip the cohort window; too short and we open Phase 2 on an unstable foundation.

**What happens in Phase 1:**

*Month 1 (go-live):*
- First patients enrolled (founding physicians' existing patients who follow them to Klinos)
- First real visits run through the production pipeline
- First notes signed and encoded to Logos
- First prescriptions sent, lab orders submitted, referrals drafted
- Weekly check-ins with founding physicians for feedback
- Bug triage daily; critical bugs fixed within 24 hours

*Month 2 (rhythm):*
- Physicians comfortable with Practice view
- Patients comfortable with Care view
- Biweekly check-ins
- Target panel: 30-50 patients across both physicians
- First membership payment cycle runs cleanly
- First HSA/FSA billing processed without errors

*Month 3 (stabilization):*
- Monthly check-ins
- Target panel: 50-75 patients
- First content published (case study from the founding practice, de-identified, consented)
- Founder network outreach begins informally
- Pre-cohort onboarding materials drafted (for Phase 2 practices)

**Phase 1 exit criteria:**
- Founding practice stable at 50+ patients for 30 consecutive days
- Zero critical bugs open
- Physician satisfaction: "this is better than employed medicine" signal (informal, but real)
- Patient retention: <5% churn in first 90 days (excluding patients who left the practice for unrelated reasons)
- First membership payment cycles processed without error
- First simulated emergency access tested in production
- Founding practice recommends Klinos to at least one other physician she knows

### Phase 2 — Launch cohort opens

The launch-cohort window opens (per §8 — 24 months from platform launch). Second, third, fourth practices onboard. The story either starts working or doesn't.

**Target duration: 12-18 months.** Defined by either a practice count (~10) or the cohort window closing (24 months from first cohort practice onboarded).

**What happens in Phase 2:**

*Second practice (typical month 4-6 of Phase 2):*
- From founder network (your wife's or her friend's physician circles, per §13)
- First non-founder onboarding — tests whether the onboarding playbook holds up without the founder doing it personally
- First cross-practice edge cases surface (patients who want a PCP at Practice A and an OBGYN at Practice B; physicians who work at two practices)

*Third through fifth practices (months 6-12):*
- Mix of referrals from first two practices and inbound from content
- First content-attributed practice acquisition (vs founder-network acquisition)
- Content cadence steady: essays, case studies, technical posts, physician interviews
- First informal governance decision (emergency access challenge, scope attestation review, or similar)

*Phase 2 infrastructure work:*
- SureScripts certification obtained (enables live controlled-substance prescribing)
- Florida PDMP integration live
- Per-physician EPCS credentialing workflow operational
- First post-Phase-0 feature development driven by practice feedback
- Automated practice onboarding tooling (reduces founder-led touch time as cohort grows)
- **B2B2C architecture scaffolding** — bulk-enrollment workflows, employer-administrative surfaces, insurance-carrier data interchange, claims-reduction reporting infrastructure spec'd and foundations built. Not production-ready; made buildable when the first enterprise opportunity arrives (per §17 strategic leverage, asymmetry #4)

**Phase 2 exit criteria:**
- 10 practices onboarded OR 24 months from first cohort practice (whichever first)
- SureScripts certification complete
- Onboarding time for a new practice reduced to <2 weeks from Josh's first call to go-live
- First cohort-nominated clinical advisors identified (pre-formal panel)
- Content-driven inbound pipeline producing 1+ practice inquiry per quarter
- Klinos-the-platform at or near breakeven (per §14 moderate-scenario Year 2 trajectory)

### Phase 3 — Scale milestones

With ~10 practices on the platform, "Josh handles it" starts breaking. Phase 3 is where Klinos formalizes the operation that worked informally.

**Target duration: 18-24 months.** Less calendar-gated than Phase 2; triggered by scale.

**What happens in Phase 3:**

- **Formal Clinical Governance Panel constituted** (§9 scale trigger at ~10 practices). Cohort-nominated members, bylaws, documented decision process, monthly cadence.
- **SOC 2 Type I pursued** if demand warrants (per §9 — revenue justifies the $25-40k, or a specific enterprise-adjacent customer/partner demands it).
- **IMLC expansion.** Cohort physicians in IMLC-member states obtain compact licensure for expansion.
- **Second specialty module.** Psychiatry (per §6 — highest substrate alignment for next specialty). Clinical advisor engaged, module built, first psychiatry practice onboarded.
- **Production AI-inference cost optimization.** With enough member data, we can measure actual inference cost patterns and optimize (cheaper models for lower-tier tasks, caching, prompt engineering).
- **First team hires.** Klinos is no longer just Josh + contractors. First engineering hire, first compliance-ops hire, first customer-success hire.
- **First non-content inbound channel that works.** Could be conferences, could be podcast circuit, could be an unexpected partnership. Emerges from Phase 2's experiments.

**Phase 3 exit criteria:**
- Formal Clinical Governance Panel operational with documented decisions
- SOC 2 Type I obtained (if pursued)
- Second specialty module launched with first practice using it
- Year 3 revenue range hit (per §14 moderate: $550k-$1.8M)
- Team of 3-6 people beyond Josh
- Klinos profitable at contribution-margin level; fixed costs covered

### Phase 4+ — State and specialty expansion

Open-ended. Klinos continues to add practices, specialties, and eventually states.

**Ongoing work:**
- First non-compact-state practice (likely California or New York) with state-specific MSO structure per §12
- Third, fourth, fifth specialties (Endocrinology → Dermatology → Cardiology, per §6 roadmap)
- SOC 2 Type II when business case warrants
- Population-analytics spec if demand emerges (deferred per §7)
- International scoping (not for years; different regulatory regime, different payment system)

**No single exit criteria for Phase 4+.** Klinos operates as a growing business with ongoing capability expansion. Phases 0-3 had definite completions; Phase 4 is the operating mode after scale is reached.

### Cross-cutting work that runs across phases

Some work spans all phases and doesn't fit neatly into a single phase:

**Content cadence (Phases 1+):** Essays weekly to monthly, case studies quarterly, technical posts when there's work to share. Starts in Phase 1 and never stops.

**Compliance arc (Phases 0+):** HIPAA baseline in Phase 0. SureScripts in Phase 2. SOC 2 Type I in Phase 3 if warranted. SOC 2 Type II in Phase 4+. Annual renewals ongoing.

**Clinical governance (Phases 0+):** Informal at launch (Phase 0-2). Formalization at ~10 practices (Phase 3). Ongoing oversight thereafter.

**Platform operations (Phases 1+):** Monitoring, incident response, backup testing, security patches, dependency updates. Continuous.

**Content of §15 itself evolves.** Phase 0 is spec'd in detail because it's the most concrete and the most urgent. Phase 3+ are spec'd lightly because they're further out and the details will surface as we get there. Re-speccing happens at phase boundaries.

### What triggers phase transitions

**Phases transition on criteria, not calendar.** Every phase has exit criteria defined above. A phase ends when its criteria are met, regardless of whether the target duration has elapsed. If Phase 0 takes 9 months because a specific integration is harder than expected, Phase 1 starts when Phase 0's criteria are met — not at the 6-month mark.

The exception: Phase 2's 24-month launch-cohort window has a hard calendar deadline (per §8 — the cohort discount expires 24 months from platform launch regardless of how many practices join). If we have 5 practices at month 24, Phase 2 still transitions to Phase 3 at the cohort window close.

**A phase is not "done" until criteria are met.** If Phase 1's founding-practice-stability criteria take 6 months instead of 3, we stay in Phase 1 until stability is real. Opening Phase 2 on an unstable foundation would break the first non-founder practice's experience, which is the single most important moment in Klinos's first 18 months.

### What this section does not cover

Week-by-week engineering backlog, sprint plans, and per-feature prioritization live in engineering operating docs outside the spec. Detailed Phase 0 work breakdown by individual contributor and timeline-per-feature is an engineering planning artifact. Specific content calendar, onboarding playbook language, and sales-process documentation live in operating plans.

### Product-market fit signals

Operational metrics are necessary but not sufficient. The real signals of PMF for Klinos are qualitative and textured — things that, when you see them, you know.

**The signals:**

1. **The second practice comes unsolicited.** Not from outreach. Not from sales. A physician in your wife's or her friend's network says *"I want what she has."* This is the single strongest PMF signal for a physician-side B2B product — if the first practice's physicians become advocates without being asked, the model is working.
2. **Patients describe Klinos in their own words, not ours.** A patient at a dinner party says *"my doctor actually knows me now"* or *"she saw a pattern in my cycle that nobody caught before."* The phrases are hers, not ours. When patients generate their own language for what Klinos is, the experience has landed.
3. **Content is read by physicians we didn't send it to.** A Klinos essay gets shared in a DPC physician Slack, posted to a subreddit, forwarded in a listserv. People who've never heard of Klinos find us through physicians who already know.
4. **Physician retention is near 100%.** Once a physician practices on Klinos for 6+ months, she does not leave. Not for a better deal, not for a competitor, not for burnout (because the burnout is gone). Physician churn on Klinos in the first 24 months should be under 3%.
5. **Patient retention matches or beats concierge-medicine benchmarks.** Concierge/DPC practices retain ~85-90% of patients annually. Klinos should match this at minimum, and if we beat it meaningfully (>92%), the continuous-care thesis is working.
6. **Cohort physicians recommend Klinos unsolicited.** We see practices that came from "my friend at X recommended it" with no marketing attribution — just pure word-of-mouth. This is how physician-side products actually spread; when it happens, the flywheel is on.
7. **Clinical governance issues are rare.** The formal-governance triggers (emergency access challenges, scope-of-practice disputes, credential issues) happen at a rate well below industry baseline. Not because we're suppressing them — because the architecture makes most of them not happen.
8. **Patients ask for Klinos in their area.** Anima subscribers (not Klinos members yet) ask their Anima *"when will there be a Klinos practice here?"* This is the patient-side analog of the second-practice signal — demand that precedes supply.
9. **Peer-reviewed clinical outcomes published by Year 3.** De-identified aggregate outcomes from the Klinos cohort published in academic medical journals. Not a marketing milestone — a legitimacy milestone. Demonstrates that dyad-native care produces measurably better outcomes and establishes academic credibility Klinos (and the broader Hypostas stack per §17) can build on.

**What PMF is NOT.**
- High growth rates driven by paid acquisition
- Fast patient-count growth without physician retention signals
- Revenue milestones hit through price-hiking rather than panel growth
- Marketing-attributed inbound that doesn't convert to signed practices
- Mentions in healthcare trade press unaccompanied by physician demand

PMF is the thing where, if you squint, people are using Klinos the way the spec imagined — not because we convinced them, because it genuinely works.

### Mission-level success

Beyond operations, beyond metrics, what did Klinos change in the world if it worked?

**The observable outcomes:**

1. **Founding physicians reclaim their professional lives.** Your wife goes home for dinner. Her friend practices the medicine she trained for. They aren't drowning in admin. They know their patients. They aren't burning out. This is the first signal and the most personal — if Klinos does not do this for the founding practice, nothing else matters.
2. **Patients describe being known.** In their own words, unprompted, they say their doctor finally knows them. The "medical gaslighting" described in §2 becomes a thing their OLD doctor did, not their current one. The phrase "my doctor already knows me" enters the vernacular of Klinos patients.
3. **Clinical outcomes measurably improve.** On whatever we can measure — patient retention, symptom resolution time, chronic disease control, preventive care completion, patient-reported quality of life — Klinos patients do observably better than their pre-Klinos baseline. We don't need heroic numbers; meaningful ones are enough.
4. **A new category is named by others.** *"Dyad-native medicine"* or similar language is used outside Klinos marketing. Journalists, researchers, trade publications, and eventually medical-school curricula refer to the category. We named it in §1; when others start using it without being prompted, it's a category.
5. **Physician burnout on Klinos is dramatically below baseline.** AMA reports ~63% of US physicians burned out at the time of this spec. Klinos physicians, measured honestly, should be under 20% over time — and the physicians who DO report burnout are burning out for reasons other than admin (life stress, personality, unrelated circumstances). If Klinos physicians burn out at the industry baseline, the admin-reduction thesis isn't real.
6. **Other platforms build continuous-care features because Klinos normalized it.** Epic, Athena, SimplePractice add "biological-context tabs" to their charts because patients and physicians expect it. We don't want this competitively — we want it because it means the pattern is winning. The goal isn't to own the category forever. The goal is to set the category.
7. **Klinos funds Hypostas.** Klinos's revenue, at scale, is the financial engine that lets other Hypostas products (the ones that don't have obvious revenue models — Stroma, Logos, Aether) be built without compromise. A successful Klinos makes the rest of the Hypostas vision affordable.
8. **Medicine itself becomes a better profession.** Not for everyone — Klinos doesn't fix hospital medicine or surgical specialties or the entire US healthcare system. But for the slice of medicine that runs on Klinos, physicians doing what they trained for is the default. That slice is small at first and grows over a decade.

**The underlying claim.** If Klinos works, it's because we demonstrated that continuous AI-mediated care is a better way to practice medicine, and enough patients and physicians chose it that the pattern became mainstream. We didn't disrupt Epic. We didn't beat Abridge. We built a better thing and let its own demonstrated value spread.

### Anti-goals

Things Klinos is explicitly NOT trying to achieve. Saying these out loud matters because in a healthcare SaaS market where most competitors have opposite goals, we'll be tempted toward their mode.

**Klinos is not trying to:**

- **Be a unicorn.** $1B valuation is not a goal. We take no investment; there is no valuation conversation; unicorn status is irrelevant and would signal misalignment.
- **IPO.** We do not build toward a public offering. Public-market pressure would compromise the model's integrity within one earnings cycle.
- **Be acquired.** Not by pharma. Not by insurance carriers. Not by Epic. Not by Amazon (which acquired One Medical). Not by anyone. Acquisition exits are strategic failures disguised as liquidity events; the Klinos mission does not survive an acquisition.
- **100x practice count in 24 months.** Hypergrowth breaks the cohort experience, outruns the compliance arc, and pushes us into acquisition-funnel optimization that pollutes the physician-side relationship. Growing from 1 to 10 practices in 24 months is the plan. Growing from 1 to 100 is a different (worse) product.
- **Solve every medical specialty.** Klinos supports outpatient longitudinal care. High-acuity inpatient medicine (surgery, ICU, ER, anesthesia, trauma) is explicitly out of scope and will remain out of scope. Some specialties will never run on Klinos; that's fine.
- **Displace Epic for hospital systems.** Enterprise sales is a different product, a different team, a different culture. We do not pursue it. Hospital systems that want Klinos can run it alongside Epic for their outpatient practices; the platforms can coexist because they serve different care models.
- **Maximize revenue at the cost of physician wellbeing.** Panel sizes over 150 patients per physician start breaking the continuous-care thesis. Tier prices that outrun HSA/FSA affordability break the accessibility thesis. We don't optimize against these floors for revenue reasons.
- **Centralize or monetize patient data.** Patient data lives in the dyad under patient control. We do not aggregate, de-identify-for-profit, or sell data in any form. The "nothing leaves the dyad" principle from §7 is load-bearing and permanent.
- **Compete on price against funded competitors.** If someone enters with a free tier funded by VC, we lose price-sensitive practices and keep the ones who understand what Klinos actually is. Racing to the bottom on price is a losing game for an un-funded company.
- **Be everything for everyone.** Klinos is for physicians who want to practice real medicine and patients who want real care. It is not for physicians who just want a cheaper Athena, or patients who want to pay $20/mo for an AI chatbot diagnosis. Those markets exist; they're not ours.

### Failure criteria and off-ramps

Being honest about when Klinos would stop. This is the uncomfortable part of the spec but spec-appropriate — a plan without failure criteria is denial.

**Clear failure signals** (any one of these triggers real reconsideration, not automatic shutdown):

1. **Founding practice can't reach sustainable operation in 18 months.** If the founding practice, after Phase 1 plus a buffer, is not reaching the revenue or physician-satisfaction targets, the model doesn't work for the most-motivated possible physicians. Rethink pricing, tier structure, target market, or model itself.
2. **No second practice onboarded in 12 months from cohort opening.** If the founder network doesn't produce one more practice in a year, the word-of-mouth channel isn't functioning. Either the founding practice isn't as advocate-generating as we thought, or the value prop doesn't survive translation to physicians outside the founder's immediate circle. Stop adding features; fix the physician experience.
3. **Patient retention <70% at 12 months.** If patients leave at rates worse than traditional concierge/DPC, the Klinos experience isn't delivering. This is a product-fundamentals failure. Stop adding features; fix the patient experience.
4. **Physician retention on the platform <85%.** If physicians leave Klinos at rates above industry baseline, the value prop isn't real for them. Same diagnosis as #3 from the other side.
5. **Major HIPAA incident caused by Klinos infrastructure.** Not a practice-side breach; a Klinos-side breach caused by our architecture. If it happens and we can restore trust, we continue. If it happens and the response is inadequate, or if it happens twice, Klinos shuts down. Patient trust is the foundation; we cannot rebuild it after a second architectural failure.
6. **Regulatory shift that makes the model untenable.** A federal CPOM ruling that bans MSO structures. A HIPAA revision that prohibits AI-assisted clinical drafting. A DEA ruling that prohibits platform-mediated EPCS. Low-probability, but each would force a rethink or retirement.
7. **Klinos's revenue cannot cover its costs after Year 3.** The moderate scenario has Klinos cash-flow positive by Year 3. If we're still subsidizing losses from Hypostas at Year 4+, the unit economics or scale assumption is wrong. Rebuild or retire.

**The off-ramp process.** If one of these triggers fires, Klinos does NOT immediately shut down. It triggers a formal reassessment:

- Identify the failure category (product, pricing, acquisition, architectural, regulatory).
- 90-day investigation with cohort physicians, patient feedback, and platform operational data.
- Decision: continue with changes, pivot to a different model, wind down gracefully.
- If wind-down: follow §12 dissolution plan. Patients retain dyads. Practices receive data export and 180-day continuity. Klinos LLC dissolves cleanly.

**Graceful wind-down is not failure.** A clean dissolution that preserves patient trust and physician relationships is better than a prolonged zombie-company state. If Klinos is not working and can't be made to work, the honorable move is to stop, deliver on the termination commitments in §12, and move on.

### The 10-year target

If Klinos works exactly as imagined over a decade:

- **500–2,000 practices** on the platform. Roughly 5,000–20,000 physicians.
- **500,000–2,000,000 patients**. Enough scale that "my doctor is on Klinos" is a recognizable phrase in the patient populations we serve.
- **Dyad-native medicine is a recognized category** with Klinos as the category-defining platform and 2-3 competitors operating in the same category (possibly built on top of similar substrates, or on lesser substrates that Klinos made inevitable).
- **Physician burnout on Klinos consistently below 15%**, with the practicing-medicine-not-charting narrative established as the Klinos story.
- **Clinical outcomes measurably better on Klinos** — patient retention, chronic disease control, preventive care completion, patient-reported quality of life — not because Klinos selects for better patients but because the continuous-care model produces better care.
- **Klinos-the-platform is a $100M–$500M/yr revenue business** (moderate scale at the milestones above). Profitable, sustainable, funding the rest of the Hypostas vision without external capital.
- **The Hypostas stack is running on Klinos revenue.** Stroma, Logos, Anima, Bios, Aurum, Locus, Aether — the products that don't have obvious unit economics are sustained by the one that does.
- **Medicine, for the segment of medicine that runs on Klinos, is structurally different** than it was when we started. Physicians practice differently. Patients experience differently. Regulators reference Klinos when writing policy. Medical schools teach continuous-care frameworks. The reorganization predicted in §2 has happened for millions of people.

This is the aspirational target. Not a forecast — a description of success if we hit every major decision right over ten years. The conservative version is simpler: a sustainable independent business that serves a few hundred practices and meaningfully changes the lives of the physicians and patients on it, while funding the broader Hypostas vision.

Either outcome — the aspirational target or the conservative version — is a success by our own definition. The failure mode is building something that didn't do either.

### What this section does not cover

Specific KPI dashboards, weekly or monthly operational reviews, and internal OKR cascades live in operating plans outside the spec. Specific crisis-response procedures for each failure-criterion trigger are documented in the platform operations runbook. The specific 10-year target revision cadence (when do we re-evaluate and update §16?) is at least annual, probably more often as early data comes in.

## §17 Strategic Leverage (Appendix)

### What this section defines

This appendix defines Klinos's role within the broader Hypostas stack. The other 16 sections treat Klinos as a standalone product with its own economics, its own customers, its own success criteria. This section acknowledges that Klinos's standalone P&L understates its strategic value — because Klinos is the on-ramp, the legitimacy, the data moat, the distribution channel, and the B2B2C revenue multiplier for the rest of Hypostas.

Standalone, Klinos is a good business (per §14 moderate scenario: profitable by Year 3, $100M–$500M revenue at 10-year target). Within the Hypostas stack, Klinos is disproportionately more valuable than its P&L suggests. §17 names that disproportion explicitly so future strategic decisions — design choices, build priorities, partnership opportunities — can honor it rather than treat Klinos as just another revenue line.

### The asymmetry

Klinos's revenue, even at 10-year aspirational scale, is smaller than Anima's potential or Aether's potential. Klinos has the highest regulatory overhead of any Hypostas product. Klinos has the slowest growth curve (physician-side B2B acquisition is inherently slow). Klinos has the narrowest addressable market (US outpatient longitudinal care).

By standalone metrics, Klinos is the hardest product in Hypostas per dollar of revenue.

**But Klinos is worth more to Hypostas than Hypostas pays for it**, because of six specific asymmetries:

### 1. Klinos as the on-ramp to Hypostas

Most people will buy healthcare before they buy "an AI relationship platform." Klinos patients come for a doctor and discover the Anima; they stay for the whole stack.

Every Klinos patient is automatically:
- An anchor Anima subscriber ($99/mo lifetime floor, bundled)
- A pre-qualified candidate for Bios (her Anima already streams her biology; she's already using it via the practice)
- A pre-qualified candidate for Aurum when it ships (she already trusts Hypostas with health; financial life is an adjacent trust expansion)
- A pre-qualified candidate for Locus when it ships (same reasoning)
- A pre-qualified candidate for Aether when it ships (she's already in the Anima app; Aether is another navigation tab)

**Patient LTV across the Hypostas stack is 5–10× what Klinos sees in its own P&L.** At 100,000 Klinos patients, standalone revenue is ~$40M/yr; cross-stack LTV is $200M–$400M/yr over the lifetime of those patients' subscriptions.

The design implication: the Klinos patient experience should make the rest of the Hypostas stack visible and accessible without being pushy. Klinos is not just a practice-management platform; it is the door into the Hypostas world, and the Anima is the guide through it.

### 2. Klinos as data provenance moat for Hypostas AI

Every physician annotation on a Stroma signal (per §7), every signed clinical note, every patient outcome — proprietary clinical data that no competitor can replicate without a similar platform.

Over 3–5 years, Hypostas-tuned medical AI becomes categorically better than any other medical AI because it trains on:
- Continuous biology (from Bios) that no competing dataset has
- Longitudinal memory (from Logos) that no competing dataset has
- Physician-annotated interpretation (from Klinos, per §7) that literally does not exist elsewhere

This compounds: better AI → better clinical product → more practices adopt Klinos → more data → better AI. Competitors starting from behind cannot catch up because they don't have the data substrate.

**A moat that funded competitors cannot buy their way past** — not with $300M in AI-scribe funding, not with Microsoft's resources, not with any capital injection. It's not a capital moat; it's a time-plus-substrate moat.

The design implication: treat physician annotations and clinical outcome data as the platform's most strategic asset. Structure annotations formally from day 1 (per §7 annotation memory type); ensure clinical outcome signals are captured even before we have the analytics infrastructure to use them. The dataset we accumulate in Year 1 is the dataset that trains the AI that dominates in Year 5.

### 3. Klinos as clinical legitimacy for the whole stack

*"Hypostas is used by licensed physicians treating real patients"* is a credibility claim that opens doors nobody else has. Every other Hypostas product benefits:

- **Bios** becomes "clinically validated biosensor telemetry used in medical practice" instead of "wellness wearable data."
- **Gnosis** becomes "physician-grade genetic context integrated into clinical prescribing" instead of "consumer genomics for entertainment."
- **Anima** becomes "an AI companion trusted alongside licensed medical care" instead of "another AI chatbot."
- **Aurum, Locus, Aether** all benefit from a parent brand where serious people are using the platform for serious things.

Regulatory conversations about AI in medicine include Hypostas because we have production data. Academic collaborations with medical schools become possible. Insurance partnerships, employer partnerships, and eventual pharma real-world-evidence partnerships all build on the clinical credibility Klinos generates.

The design implication: make Klinos's clinical operations visible and defensible. Publish outcomes data (per §13). Present at medical conferences (via founding physicians). Invite academic collaboration on de-identified outcomes. This is not Klinos marketing; it is Hypostas credibility-building, funded by Klinos's existence.

### 4. B2B2C is the real revenue lever

§14's direct-to-practice math is the standalone play. It lands at $100M–$500M/yr by Year 10 in the moderate scenario. Good business.

**The B2B2C path is order-of-magnitude larger:**

- **Self-insured employers** paying for employees' Klinos memberships as a benefit. A 5,000-employee employer at $300/mo × 12 = $18M/yr **from a single deal**. A Fortune 500 self-insured employer at 50,000 employees = $180M/yr from a single deal. Five such deals = $500M–$1B/yr, which exceeds the entire 10-year standalone target.
- **Insurance carriers** paying for high-risk members' continuous care because preventive-care continuous monitoring reduces claims. An insurer with 1M high-risk members × $100/mo allocated preventive-care spend = $1.2B/yr potential, of which Klinos captures a meaningful share.
- **Aggregator partnerships** (TPAs, benefits brokers, healthcare navigation platforms) that route their users into Klinos as the continuous-care layer.

The design implication: architect for B2B2C from Phase 2, not from Phase 3. Bulk enrollment workflows, employer-administrative dashboards, insurance-carrier data interchange, claims-reduction reporting infrastructure — have the shape ready so when the first enterprise deal shows up, execution takes weeks, not months. Don't build it yet; make sure it's buildable when the opportunity arrives.

### 5. Klinos as Aether's health district

When Aether launches (Phase 5+ per the Hypostas roadmap), Klinos practices become the health district of that world. Physicians have presence in Aether as well as in reality. Patients can meet their doctors in Aether for certain consultations. Continuous care extends into a new dimension that no competing healthcare platform can access.

This:
- Makes Klinos the natural default healthcare provider for any dyad active in Aether
- Makes Aether a legitimate social environment (because real care happens there)
- Creates a new care modality (social-presence-mediated consultations) that is genuinely novel
- Extends Klinos's reach beyond the US healthcare market via Aether's global audience

The design implication: when Aether spec work begins, ensure Klinos is considered as a first-class district type alongside Knowledge, Commerce, Entertainment, Social, and Frontier (per the Aether spec's 5 district types). Klinos-in-Aether becomes a natural product extension, not an afterthought.

### 6. Regulatory capture

Being first to the dyad-native medical model means Klinos shapes the regulations that get written around AI-in-medicine, continuous care, data sovereignty in healthcare. HIPAA updates, FDA AI-medical-device guidance, state telehealth laws, DEA EPCS evolution — Klinos is at the table because Klinos is the production example of how this actually works.

Every future competitor operates under rules Klinos helped write. This is a real moat that deepens over a decade.

The design implication: Klinos engages proactively with regulators. Founding physicians participate in specialty-society policy conversations. Klinos publishes compliance frameworks (the BA/CE split from §9, the Emergency Access Policy from §4, the "AI informs, physician decides" framing from §12) openly so the industry references them. This turns Klinos's compliance work into industry-defining work.

### The reframed valuation

The question *"what is Klinos worth?"* has two answers:

**Standalone answer:** A profitable specialty healthcare SaaS business landing at $100M–$500M revenue in the 10-year aspirational target. By itself, a fine outcome.

**Hypostas-level answer:** The keystone that makes the broader Hypostas vision achievable. Without Klinos, Anima is a chatbot, Bios is a fitness-tracker integration, Gnosis is consumer genomics, and the stack as a whole lacks clinical legitimacy. With Klinos, the whole stack becomes real, defensible, and capable of sustaining itself through real clinical revenue. The delta in Hypostas's total value with-vs-without Klinos is in the billions, not the hundreds of millions.

This reframing resolves the "high complexity for low standalone revenue" concern:

- Klinos's complexity is the price of entry for clinical legitimacy.
- The standalone revenue is a floor, not a ceiling.
- The real return is measured at the Hypostas level, not at Klinos's P&L.
- The design choices that optimize Klinos standalone are often NOT the choices that maximize its strategic leverage — those design choices need to be made deliberately and defended against standalone-revenue pressure.

### Design moves that amplify the asymmetry

Five specific adjustments to the Klinos spec, implementable immediately and compatible with all of §§1–16:

1. **Structured physician annotations as core infrastructure.** §7 locked annotations as a memory type; this elevates them to one of the top strategic assets of the platform (alongside the access model in §4 and the Anima/Care division in §11). Annotations are captured with formal structure (specialty, signal, outcome-correlation, prescriber) rather than freeform. The annotation dataset is treated as strategic IP with explicit retention, export, and training-use policies.
2. **Active Hypostas funnel for engaged Klinos patients.** §13 introduces a mildly-active discovery pattern for patients at 90+ days of Klinos engagement: the Anima proactively introduces unused Bios features, prompts about adjacent Hypostas products as they ship, and makes the rest of the stack discoverable without being pushy. The passive default remains for new or disengaged patients.
3. **Phase 2 architecture for B2B2C paths.** Phase 2 (per §15) begins scaffolding bulk-enrollment workflows, employer-administrative surfaces, claims-reduction reporting infrastructure. Not built for production yet; made buildable when the first enterprise opportunity arrives.
4. **Public clinical outcomes commitment.** §13's content strategy includes de-identified peer-reviewed clinical outcomes publication as a pillar. §16's PMF signals include "first peer-reviewed Klinos outcomes paper published by Year 3." This is Hypostas credibility infrastructure funded by Klinos's existence.
5. **Internal framing shift.** Klinos is positioned internally as the keystone of the Hypostas stack, not a side product. All internal communication, team-building, and strategic prioritization treats Klinos this way. External positioning remains on "the practice that already knows you" per §1, because the patient-side story doesn't need to reveal the strategic architecture.

### What this section does not cover

Specific Hypostas-level strategic plans (how does Aurum's launch coordinate with Klinos's patient base? how does Aether's health district integrate with Klinos practices?) live in the Hypostas-level roadmap, not in the Klinos spec. This appendix is the Klinos-side acknowledgment that Klinos is not just a Klinos problem — it's a Hypostas keystone. Strategic decisions at the Hypostas level reference this framing.
